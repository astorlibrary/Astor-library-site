Shakespeare

Shakespeare.

Shakespeare’s plays have never stood still. Read them through early print, the stories they borrowed and centuries of performance, from playhouse to screen.

Tragedy

Romeo and Juliet

The play first appeared in quarto in 1597 and again in 1599. Against the Montague–Capulet feud, Romeo and Juliet’s secret marriage is undone by Tybalt’s violence, Romeo’s banishment and Friar Lawrence’s failed message. Mercutio gives the tragedy another of its defining voices, one later actors and film-makers have continually rediscovered.

Henry VI, Part 3

Printed in 1595 as The True Tragedie of Richard Duke of Yorke, the early text stands behind the later Folio play. Queen Margaret, York, Edward IV, Warwick and Richard carry the Wars of the Roses through Towton and Tewkesbury, pressing the story onwards towards Richard III.

Comedy

Much Ado About Nothing

Printed in quarto in 1600 and in the First Folio in 1623, the comedy sets two courtships against one another in Messina. Beatrice and Benedick talk their way towards love; Hero and Claudio are nearly destroyed by overhearing, false evidence and public shame. Dogberry’s watch uncovers what the court misses, a contrast that stage and screen versions keep finding afresh.

Comedy

The Comedy of Errors

Our first records are a Gray’s Inn performance and the text in the First Folio; behind them lie sources in Plautus. In Ephesus, two sets of twins turn ordinary troubles with debt and marriage into apparent magic and multiplying confusion. Recognition finally restores order, though later musical versions have found more room for the joke.

Tragedy

Titus Andronicus

The 1594 quarto and Henslowe’s Rose record bring us close to a Roman revenge tragedy of sacrifice, mutilation and banquet horror. Lavinia, Tamora and Aaron stand at the centre of its violence. The extremity of their stories has also shaped the authorship debate and the play’s difficult return to the modern stage.

Comedy

A Midsummer Night's Dream

The play survives in a 1600 quarto and the 1623 Folio, drawing on Ovid, Chaucer and the Robin Goodfellow tradition. Its Athens, quarrelling fairies and mechanicals have inspired music by Mendelssohn and Britten, Peter Brook’s 1970 RSC production and many screen versions.

Late romance

The Tempest

First printed in the 1623 First Folio, the play draws on reports of the Sea Venture and Bermuda, as well as Montaigne. On Prospero’s island, Miranda, Ariel and Caliban make power inseparable from masque and music. Colonial readings and later screen versions continue to test who controls the island and its story.

Comedy

The Taming of the Shrew

The First Folio text sits beside the related A Shrew and stories by Gascoigne and Ariosto. From Christopher Sly’s strange induction to the marriage bargaining in Padua, Katherina, Petruchio and Bianca make this an uneasy comedy. Its history on stage and screen is also a history of argument.

History

Richard II

This history, written entirely in verse, survives first in a 1597 quarto. Its sources in Holinshed and the later textual problem around the deposition scene open a deeply political play, in which Gaunt and Bolingbroke challenge Richard’s belief in divine kingship. The Essex performance record and later productions show why that conflict mattered.

Comedy

The Merry Wives of Windsor

The 1602 quarto and 1623 Folio bring Falstaff into the domestic world of Mistress Ford and Mistress Page, where each attempt at deception meets a sharper counter-plot. Ford’s jealousy and Anne Page’s suitors add to the confusion, while the buck-basket, the Brentford disguise and Herne’s Oak give it unforgettable comic shapes.

History

Henry VI, Part 2

Published in 1594 as The First Part of the Contention, the play follows Gloucester, Suffolk and Queen Margaret while York quietly advances his claim. Jack Cade’s rebellion and the battle of St Albans turn political rivalry into civil war. Later performance records show how the play returned to the stage.

History

Henry IV, Part 1

The 1598 quarto turns Holinshed’s history into three competing worlds: court, rebellion and Eastcheap. Henry IV, Prince Hal, Falstaff and Hotspur each offer a different idea of honour on the road to Shrewsbury. Screen versions have repeatedly returned to the tension between them.

Tragedy

Othello

A Whitehall performance is recorded in 1604; the play then survives in a 1622 quarto and the 1623 First Folio, alongside Shakespeare’s source in Cinthio. As the action moves from Venice to Cyprus, Iago’s plot closes around Othello, Desdemona and the handkerchief. Screen versions reveal how differently that narrowing tragedy can be seen.

Tragedy

Hamlet

Q1, Q2 and the First Folio preserve substantially different early texts of Hamlet. Behind Elsinore lie the Amleth tradition and the conventions of revenge tragedy; at its centre are Hamlet and Ophelia, both changed by the demands placed upon them. Its stage and screen history shows that performance never leaves the play quite as it found it.

Tragedy

King Lear

The 1608 quarto and 1623 First Folio preserve two distinct early texts of Lear’s division of the kingdom. Cordelia’s story is shadowed by that of Gloucester and Edgar, binding the two families’ suffering together. The play’s sources, Tate’s adaptation, RSC records and screen versions show how radically its ending and emphasis have changed.

History

Richard III

The 1597 quarto and 1623 First Folio draw on More and Holinshed to shape Richard’s rise and fall. Clarence, Lady Anne, the princes and Buckingham mark stages on his way to Bosworth. Cibber’s adaptation, RSC records and screen versions reveal how later performers have remade that journey.

Dark comedy

All's Well That Ends Well

First printed in the 1623 First Folio, the play draws on stories by Boccaccio and Painter. Helena’s pursuit of Bertram, with Parolles nearby, turns rank, merit and the bed trick into an uneasy comedy. Its unsettled ending explains both the problem-play label and the questions visible in its RSC performance history.

Tragedy

Macbeth

The play’s print and source history lead directly to its arguments about ambition, kingship, prophecy and guilt. Its violence and supernatural atmosphere have never belonged to one period alone: stage productions, opera, screen versions and later responses show every age remaking them.

Comedy

As You Like It

Banished from court, Rosalind enters the Forest of Arden dressed as Ganymede. There she finds Orlando writing her name on the trees and turns disguise into a way of directing their courtship. The forest makes room for play, argument and self-invention—but exile, labour and unequal desire remain visible beneath the comedy.

Tragedy

Antony and Cleopatra

Antony is divided between Roman command and Cleopatra’s Alexandria, but the play refuses to make either world simple. News, messengers and shifting alliances cross the Mediterranean while the lovers continually perform versions of themselves. Their political defeat becomes a struggle over how their story will be told.

Tragedy

Coriolanus

Caius Marcius can take Corioli but cannot ask Rome’s citizens for their voices. Renamed Coriolanus and urged towards office by Volumnia, he treats political custom as humiliation. Banishment turns his contempt into an attack upon the city, until the final appeal from his family makes public survival depend upon private feeling.
