Writers/Mary Shelley

1797–1851 · Novelist, editor and biographer

Mary
Shelley.

Shelley is often reduced to one remarkable fact: she began Frankenstein while still in her teens. The fact deserves attention, but not because youth makes the novel an accident. Its control of voice, argument and consequence belongs to a writer formed among radical books and made exact by years of loss, travel, revision and literary work.

A writer among writers

Inheritance was an argument, not a shelter.

Mary Wollstonecraft died shortly after her daughter’s birth, but her writing remained present. William Godwin’s home brought books, political discussion and visitors into Mary’s education. That inheritance was intellectually rich and personally difficult. When Mary left England with Percy Bysshe Shelley in 1814, family rupture, financial uncertainty and public hostility became part of the life in which she wrote.

Frankenstein emerged from this world of reading and debate, then exceeded it. Shelley drew on the Gothic, travel writing, natural philosophy, Milton and arguments about education and political responsibility. The surviving notebooks also make the labour visible: Mary drafted and fair-copied the novel; Percy made changes and suggestions; the work’s design and authorship are hers.

She continued far beyond her first novel. Her fiction includes Valperga, The Last Man, Lodore and Falkner. She wrote travel books and biographical essays, and after Percy’s death she worked to prepare his poetry for readers. Frankenstein is the right place to begin, but it should open the shelf rather than close it.

How the writing works

Reading Shelley closely.

01

No voice stands alone

Walton writes to his sister; Victor tells Walton his history; the creature gives Victor another life story inside it. Each speaker asks to be believed and each turns another listener into a judge. Shelley makes the route by which knowledge reaches us inseparable from the knowledge itself.

02

Creation is followed by conduct

The novel does not stop at the moment life begins. Its longest pressure falls on what Victor does afterwards: recoil, silence, pursuit and repeated refusal to accept a relationship he made possible. Scientific power becomes a question of sustained obligation.

03

Landscape changes scale

Alpine valleys, Arctic ice and violent weather give private anguish a world large enough to move through. Sublime scenery can steady a mind for a moment, but it cannot repair responsibility. The landscape enlarges feeling without settling its moral claim.

A route through the novel

Keep changing the witness.

Begin by marking the listener in every account. Walton addresses Margaret Saville, Victor tries to govern Walton’s judgement, and the creature must persuade the person who already fears him. Ask what each narrator wants from the act of telling: warning, sympathy, vindication, companionship or action.

Next, separate origin from responsibility. Victor often speaks as though the act of animation has already determined the disaster. The plot repeatedly returns him to later choices: whether to remain, speak, protect, promise or confess. This keeps fate from becoming an excuse for conduct.

Finally, compare 1818 with 1831 rather than assuming there is one neutral text. Shelley altered language, relationships and the pressure of destiny, and her new introduction shaped the story of the novel’s own creation. Revision is part of the work’s history.
