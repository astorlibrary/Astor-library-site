Writers/H. G. Wells

1866–1946 · Novelist, journalist and social critic

H. G.
Wells.

Wells did not write predictions dressed up as stories. He took one alteration—a traveller through time, an invisible body, a biological experiment, an invasion from Mars—and asked what it would expose in the world already around him.

Science in ordinary streets

The strange thing arrives somewhere exact.

Wells’s early scientific romances begin with impossible events, but their surroundings are sharply recognisable. The first Martian cylinder does not land in a vague future. It falls on Horsell Common. People walk out to look. A journalist gathers news. Soldiers arrive with the confidence of a nation that expects its organisations and weapons to work. The precision of the setting gives the shock its force.

That method grew from an unusual combination of education and experience. Wells trained in biology under Thomas Henry Huxley, taught science and wrote textbooks before fiction made him widely known. Evolution, anatomy and the scale of geological time entered his imagination as ways of unsettling human importance. He knew enough science to make an invention persuasive, but the story rarely rests with the invention itself. The real subject is how people behave after the old explanation of the world has stopped being sufficient.

He also wrote far beyond the books now usually grouped as science fiction. Kipps and The History of Mr Polly attend to class, work and thwarted lives; A Modern Utopia and later histories move into political design on a much larger scale. The career is argumentative, uneven and extraordinarily wide. The early romances remain the strongest entrance because they make an idea move at the speed of a story.

How the writing works

Reading Wells closely.

01

Begin with the familiar

Roads, commons, houses, railway lines and newspaper offices make the extraordinary measurable. Wells lets the reader recognise the route before he destroys its safety. Place is not background; it is the scale against which invasion becomes imaginable.

02

Change the balance of power

His speculative machinery often creates an inequality rather than a fair contest. The Martians do not meet Britain on equal military terms. Once the balance changes, habits that once looked like civilisation—command, property, empire, expertise—must prove what they are worth.

03

Let knowledge arrive late

The narrator can observe carefully and still misunderstand what he sees. Explanation follows terror in pieces. That delay keeps the book from becoming a lecture and prevents scientific vocabulary from being confused with mastery.

A route through the novel

Keep asking who understands the event.

At Horsell Common, separate seeing from knowing. The crowd watches the cylinder, the newspapers turn the event into information and the authorities assume that whatever has arrived can be contained. The Heat-Ray destroys those assumptions before anyone has a useful name for the weapon.

As the invasion widens, follow systems rather than only spectacle. News travels unevenly; roads and railways fill; military organisation meets a force built on another technological scale. Wells shows a modern society connected enough to spread panic, but not informed enough to act together.

In the ruined house and the artilleryman’s garden, listen to competing explanations. Religion, social planning and scientific observation each promise order. None of them is useless, but each can become a shelter from what is actually happening. The Martians finally die through their relation to Earth’s smallest life, not through the grand plan of any human speaker.
