Close reading 01 · Hamlet

“To be, or not to be”

The speech is famous because it seems to offer a single question. Its sentences do something less settled: they test an answer, revise it and stop themselves.

William ShakespeareAct 3, Scene 1

Probably written around 1600–01. The wording below follows the Project Gutenberg text of the play.

How to read this page: read the speech once without stopping. Then select or focus a numbered phrase to bring its note forward.

Hamlet3.1

To be, or not to be1, that is the question:
Whether ’tis nobler2 in the mind to suffer
The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune,
Or to take arms against a sea of troubles3,
And by opposing end them?

To die—to sleep,
No more; and by a sleep to say we end
The heart-ache, and the thousand natural shocks
That flesh is heir to: ’tis a consummation
Devoutly to be wish’d.

To die, to sleep.
To sleep, perchance to dream—ay, there’s the rub4,
For in that sleep of death what dreams may come,
When we have shuffled off this mortal coil,
Must give us pause.5

Hamlet, Act 3, Scene 1 · public-domain text

1

No “I”

The infinitives make the opening feel general: “to be”, not “shall I live?” The speech sounds inward, but its grammar removes the speaker from the question.

2

A moral ranking

“Nobler” asks which choice has greater worth. Hamlet does not begin with happiness or relief. He turns suffering and resistance into rival claims about honour.

3

An impossible action

A person can take arms against an army, but not a sea. The image gives resistance heroic energy and makes its object boundless at the same time.

4

The metaphor changes sides

Sleep first promises an end to pain. The thought of dreaming makes it uncertain. Hamlet’s own figure produces the objection that stops him.

5

The pause enacted

The line names the hesitation the speech is performing. The thought does not travel cleanly towards an answer; it doubles back when language reveals a further problem.

What the wording changes

The speech thinks by revising itself.

Hamlet first makes death sound simple: sleep, an ending, release from the shocks inherited by flesh. Then one word inside that comparison—“dream”—reopens everything. If death is sleep, perhaps it does not end consciousness at all.

The repeated infinitives give the speech a measured surface, but the punctuation records interruption: colon, dash, question and pause. Its form matters because Hamlet is not reporting a finished belief. We hear the act of thought, including the moment when an attractive answer becomes frightening.

This is also why the speech should not be reduced to indecision. Hamlet follows consequences with unusual seriousness. The cost is that each imagined action creates another question.

Take one question away

Does Hamlet fail to reach an answer—or discover that the question was never as simple as it sounded?
