Passage Room/The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes/Irene Adler

Close reading 04 · Sherlock Holmes

“always the woman”

Watson begins the first collected Holmes story by naming the person who escaped his friend’s usual categories. Before the mystery starts, the result is already in view.

Arthur Conan DoyleA Scandal in Bohemia · opening

First published in The Strand Magazine in July 1891 and collected in 1892.

How to read this page: notice that every judgment comes through Watson. Then select or focus a numbered phrase to see what his framing adds.

A Scandal in BohemiaOpening

To Sherlock Holmes1 she is always the woman2. I have seldom heard him mention her under any other name. In his eyes she eclipses and predominates3 the whole of her sex.

It was not that he felt any emotion akin to love for Irene Adler. All emotions, and that one particularly, were abhorrent to his cold, precise but admirably balanced mind.

He was, I take it4, the most perfect reasoning and observing machine5 that the world has seen, but as a lover he would have placed himself in a false position.

A Scandal in Bohemia · public-domain text

1

A relation, not a fact

The opening limits its claim: this is what Adler is “to” Holmes. It tells us about the place she holds in his memory, not the whole of who she is.

2

Singular and reduced

The title makes Adler incomparable, but also emblematic. Watson praises her by separating her from “the whole of her sex”, a pattern the story both uses and exposes.

3

Light and power

“Eclipses” suggests that Adler blocks other women from view; “predominates” makes her dominant. Watson’s verbs stage the contest before he narrates it.

4

Watson shows his hand

“I take it” briefly marks interpretation. The famous account of Holmes’s cold mind is not a clinical fact but the admiring friend’s construction.

5

A metaphor tested by the story

A machine should be predictable and untouched by pride. Holmes’s disguise, miscalculation and retained photograph make the comparison less complete than Watson wants it to be.

What the wording changes

The case opens with its exception.

Most detective stories hide the solution and move towards it. This one begins by telling us that Irene Adler holds a permanent place in Holmes’s imagination. The question is not simply whether Holmes will succeed, but how the woman we know he remembers will earn that position.

Watson also creates the Holmes against whom Adler is measured. “Cold”, “precise”, “balanced” and “machine” make detachment sound like perfect method. Yet “I take it” reminds us that this portrait belongs to a narrator who admires, arranges and occasionally misunderstands his friend.

Adler’s distinction is framed in gendered terms: “the woman”, “her sex”, later “a woman’s wit”. She defeats the King’s effort to control her and anticipates Holmes’s plan, but the story still reaches her through the judgments of men. Close reading means holding both facts together.

Take one question away

Does the title “the woman” give Irene Adler unusual authority—or turn an individual woman into an exception that leaves the old rule intact?
