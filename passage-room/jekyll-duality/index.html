Passage Room/Jekyll and Hyde/Jekyll’s divided self

Close reading 02 · Jekyll and Hyde

“man is not truly one”

Jekyll offers the sentence as a discovery about everyone. Read closely, his confident division into two selves is already becoming more crowded and less secure.

Robert Louis StevensonHenry Jekyll’s Full Statement of the Case

First published in 1886. The passage comes from the confession that closes the novella.

How to read this page: read Jekyll’s claim once as an argument. Then select or focus a numbered phrase and watch how the argument shifts.

Jekyll and HydeFinal statement

Though so profound a double-dealer1, I was in no sense a hypocrite; both sides of me were in dead earnest2.

With every day, and from both sides of my intelligence, the moral and the intellectual, I thus drew steadily nearer to that truth, by whose partial discovery I have been doomed to such a dreadful shipwreck: that man is not truly one, but truly two3.

I say two, because the state of my own knowledge does not pass beyond that point. Others will follow, others will outstrip me on the same lines; and I hazard the guess that man will be ultimately known for a mere polity of multifarious, incongruous and independent denizens4.

It was on the moral side, and in my own person, that I learned to recognise the thorough and primitive duality of man5.

Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde · public-domain text

1

A social meaning first

Before it names two bodies, “double” describes Jekyll’s concealed life. The experiment gives physical form to a division already created by secrecy and reputation.

2

Not innocence

Jekyll refuses “hypocrite” without denying either appetite. “Dead earnest” makes both sides authentic, but it also lets him present responsibility as a problem of competing selves.

3

Whose truth?

The claim sounds universal, yet it comes from Jekyll’s private statement. A character is turning his own experience into a theory of humankind.

4

Two becomes many

“Multifarious” means varied or numerous. Within two sentences, the neat pair has become a whole “polity”: a community of inhabitants.

5

Science and morality

“Primitive” and “recognise” give the account the tone of discovery. But the experiment cannot separate moral choice from the person who arranged it.

What the wording changes

Jekyll’s theory is also his defence.

The statement explains the experiment, but it also shapes how Jekyll wants the experiment judged. If two authentic selves inhabit one person, Hyde can seem like a separate agent rather than a freedom Jekyll deliberately sought.

Stevenson does not let the division stay clean. Jekyll admits that “two” marks only the limit of his knowledge, then imagines many independent inhabitants. His vocabulary moves from moral confession to scientific speculation and political metaphor. The self is a pair, a shipwreck, a territory and a population.

The passage is therefore more interesting than the formula “good Jekyll, evil Hyde”. Jekyll enjoys Hyde’s liberty before he fears Hyde’s power. The struggle is not between an untouched good self and an alien evil one, but between desire, action and the stories a person tells about responsibility.

Take one question away

Does Jekyll discover a divided human nature—or invent a language that helps him divide himself from his choices?
