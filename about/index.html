About Astor Library

The book stays at the centre.

Astor Library is an independent publisher of classic editions, study editions and free literature resources.

The name

Why Astor Library?

This is a modern, independent publisher. Its name is a nod to one of the great library names of the nineteenth century.

The original Astor Library in New York was founded through the bequest of John Jacob Astor. In 1895, it was combined with the Lenox Library and the Tilden Trust to form The New York Public Library.

The Astor Library was not a circulating library. Its books were there to be consulted and studied. Astor Editions take that reference-library idea into the text itself, with notes, summaries, analysis and context placed around the original work.

The main editions

Made for readers.

The main Astor Editions are for anyone who wants to read a classic with some help close at hand.

In prose editions, every chapter is supported by a clear summary and a factual context section. These help readers follow the book’s movement, understand the world around it and notice details that might otherwise pass by.

Shakespeare editions preserve the plays with scene divisions and line numbers. Supporting notes sit beside the work without turning the play into a set of answers.

Astor Study

For classrooms and exams.

The study editions are the classroom and exam-preparation side of Astor Library.

They bring together summaries, context, quotations, criticism, model paragraphs and reading notes for students and teachers. They are designed for practical use across different exam boards, school systems and countries.

The website extends that work through free guides, booklets, PowerPoints and classroom resources. These are separate from the main Astor Editions, but they follow the same rule: help the reader understand the work without replacing it.

How it is made

Clear, checked and useful.

Dates, publication details and historical claims are checked. Sources stay visible. Plot and character are explained plainly, while interpretation remains open enough for readers to make their own judgements.

The prose should sound like one reader speaking honestly to another. No padding, no unnecessary terminology and no pretending that a great book can be reduced to a handful of approved answers.

One clear rule

The added material should help, not get in the way.

Notes, summaries and context should make the original work easier to enter and richer to return to. If they begin to draw attention away from the book, they are no longer doing their job.

The original work remains at the centre.
