Analysing quotations in Jekyll and Hyde.

A quotation is not a password for a mark. It is a small piece of language that has to do work inside an argument. This guide shows how to choose less, notice more and return every observation to the question.

Choose less. Read further.

The strongest paragraph rarely contains the longest quotation. It keeps one exact phrase in view while the argument develops around it.

1 · Select

Take only what proves the point

A short phrase is easier to place inside your own sentence and harder to hide behind. Select the word, image or grammatical pattern that the paragraph genuinely needs; do not copy the whole line for safety.

2 · Examine

Ask what changes because of this word

Word class and connotation can help, but naming them is only the beginning. Explain what the choice makes possible: does it turn a person into an animal, make change sound gradual, or leave a threat deliberately without a clear shape?

3 · Return

Make the question visible again

Context, form and an alternative reading belong in the paragraph only when they sharpen its claim. The final sentence should not repeat the question mechanically; it should show how the evidence has altered the answer.

Work one sentence from the inside out.

Jekyll writes that “man is not truly one, but truly two”. The statement matters for its sound and position as well as its subject.

The claim

Jekyll makes his experience universal

He does not say that he alone is divided. The general noun “man” turns a personal confession into a theory of human nature. That enlargement can sound insightful, but it can also protect Jekyll from personal responsibility.

The pattern

One and two are made brutally simple

The balanced numbers give a complicated self the shape of a clean opposition. Repeating “truly” insists upon certainty. Ask whether that confidence reveals knowledge or whether Jekyll is imposing order on conduct he cannot control.

The position

The explanation arrives after the damage

The sentence belongs to Jekyll’s final written account. Stevenson delays it until Utterson and the reader have encountered sealed doors, missing voices and conflicting documents. The theory therefore explains earlier events while remaining the testimony of an interested speaker.

Build memory around pairs.

A quotation becomes more useful when it can be placed beside another moment. The pair creates change, tension or contradiction for the paragraph to explain.

  • “Pale and dwarfish” / “hardly human”Both descriptions struggle to make Hyde legible through the body. Ask what the observers believe appearance can reveal, and why their language remains vague despite their confidence.
  • “Trampled calmly” / “my devil had been long caged”The first phrase places composure beside violence; the second turns repression into an imprisoned creature. Together they test whether Jekyll controls Hyde or merely creates stories about control.
  • “A great chocolate-coloured pall” / “like a district of some city in a nightmare”Fog does not simply announce that the setting is Gothic. It makes boundaries uncertain and gives a respectable city the unstable form of Jekyll’s divided life.
  • “I concealed my pleasures” / “I was slowly losing hold of my original and better self”Secrecy appears before transformation. The later phrasing makes loss sound gradual, but the possessive “my” and moral ranking “better” show Jekyll still arranging the selves from which he claims to be separating.

    PEAL and PETAL are useful scaffolds, not forms to announce. The reader should feel the movement without seeing the labels.

    Open

    Make a claim that can be tested

    “Stevenson presents duality” is a topic, not yet an argument. Decide what the novella suggests about division: that it is universal, that respectable culture produces it, or that Jekyll uses it to escape responsibility.

    Develop

    Let the evidence complicate you

    Move from the whole phrase into one or two details, then consider narrative position or another moment in the text. If an alternative reading does not change the claim, it is not adding depth.

    Close

    Answer with greater precision

    Return to the exact wording of the question. A good final sentence may qualify the opening claim: perhaps division is real in the novella, but the neat split between Jekyll and Hyde is also a self-serving fiction.

    A five-minute revision test. Choose one quotation and write three things without using the words “shows” or “suggests”: what changes inside the phrase, who says or sees it, and which later moment makes it harder to interpret. If all three answers are different, you have the beginning of analysis rather than a list of devices.

    Read the sentence in the whole novella.

    The Robert Louis Stevenson Archive provides a clear route into the work and its structure. Use the public-domain text to check the words around any quotation before placing it in an essay.
