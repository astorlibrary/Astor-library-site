A guide to Shakespeare’s history play as both political action and verbal performance. It follows Richard and Bolingbroke through banishment, return and deposition while keeping the dangers of succession in view.

Who can make a king?

The play tests the difference between possessing a title and persuading a country that the title still carries authority.

Richard

Language becomes a kingdom

As Richard loses practical command, his verbal power grows more elaborate. His self-dramatisation can reveal suffering while also showing why performance alone cannot recover political control.

Bolingbroke

Action looks like necessity

Bolingbroke presents each advance as a limited response to injustice. The accumulation of those apparently practical steps makes a revolution without requiring him to name it at the beginning.

Elizabethan stage

Deposition was a live danger

Questions of succession and the association with Essex made the play politically charged. Context sharpens the performance without reducing it to a coded report of one event.

A useful way in. Track how often Richard and Bolingbroke name what they are doing. Political change in the play advances through carefully limited claims as well as open declarations.
