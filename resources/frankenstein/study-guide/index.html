Study tip. Compare Victor’s account with the Creature’s before deciding where the novel asks for sympathy or assigns responsibility.

Who is telling the story?

Frankenstein does not give us an all-knowing account. It gives us people trying to make another person understand them.

Walton

The listener at the edge

Walton writes to his sister while pursuing glory in the Arctic. His ambition makes him receptive to Victor, but the danger facing his crew also gives him a decision Victor repeatedly avoided: whether another person’s life matters more than his own project.

Victor

A confession that still defends itself

Victor can describe terror, regret and loss with great force. He can also pass quickly over the Creature’s first needs and his own long absences. His narrative asks for sympathy while revealing the limits of his responsibility.

The Creature

A reader who becomes a speaker

The Creature learns language by observing the De Lacey household and reading. His account makes him an interpreter of human society as well as its victim. The novel then asks how far suffering explains violence without excusing it.

Six moments that hold the novel together

Return to these when tracing an argument across the whole book.

  • Walton’s opening lettersExploration, isolation and the desire for a friend appear before Victor enters the narrative. The frame makes his story a warning that Walton must decide how to use.
  • The animation of the CreatureVictor’s scientific success is followed immediately by recoil. Shelley places the moral failure not in the making alone but in the abandonment that follows it.
  • William’s death and Justine’s trialVictor possesses knowledge that could alter the case, yet remains silent. Private secrecy becomes public injustice.
  • The De Lacey narrativeThe Creature learns affection, history, property and exclusion together. Education enlarges his moral understanding and his knowledge of what society denies him.
  • The demand for a companionVictor and the Creature argue about duty, danger and the right to happiness. Neither can control the future consequences he predicts.
  • The Arctic endingVictor dies still pursuing revenge. Walton turns back; the Creature gives his own last account. The close offers testimony and judgement, not a neat settlement.

    • Context that changes the reading

    Scientific experiment matters, but the novel is not a forecast of one invention. It was written amid arguments about life, matter and the powers of experiment; it also draws on Romantic writing about nature, education and the isolated self. The subtitle, The Modern Prometheus, places Victor beside a mythic maker whose gift to humanity brings punishment. Use context where it clarifies a choice in the novel—especially creation, education, parenthood or ambition—rather than adding it as a detached paragraph.

    Use the guide as a map

    Begin with the narrative layers, then follow one problem through all three principal speakers. Responsibility, sympathy, isolation and knowledge look different depending on who is speaking and who is listening.
