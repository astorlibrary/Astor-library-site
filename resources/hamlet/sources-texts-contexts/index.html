Hamlet: sources, texts and contexts.

There is no single early Hamlet from which every modern edition simply descends. The play survives in three markedly different printed texts, and every editor or director has to decide what kind of Hamlet an audience will meet.

Three witnesses, not one settled script.

Q1, Q2 and F1 do not preserve the same words in the same order. A modern text is therefore an edited argument about the evidence.

Q1 · 1603

Shorter and differently arranged

The First Quarto is little more than half the length of the later texts. Polonius is Corambis, the “To be” speech appears in another place and many passages have different wording. It has traditionally been called a “bad quarto”, but that label is a theory, not a neutral description.

Q2 · 1604–05

The longest early text

The Second Quarto announces itself as enlarged and closer to a “true and perfect copy”. It contains material not found in F1 and often forms the base of a modern edition, though length alone cannot settle every reading.

F1 · 1623

A later theatrical witness

The Folio text is shorter than Q2 but includes lines absent from it. Some differences may reflect theatrical use or revision. Editors may combine Q2 and F1, privilege one, or print more than one version so the disagreement remains visible.

Use the traditional label carefully. The PDF introduces Q1 as the “bad quarto” and explains the once-influential theory that an actor reconstructed it from memory. Current scholarship keeps several possibilities open: memorial reconstruction, adaptation for performance, authorial revision or a more complicated movement between texts. Treat the disagreement itself as the important fact.

The story existed before Shakespeare’s play.

A source is not a hidden answer. It lets us see what Shakespeare retains, changes or makes newly difficult.

Saxo Grammaticus

Amleth survives by performing madness

A version of the Danish prince’s story appears in Saxo’s Latin history. The hero knows the truth, disguises his intelligence and eventually completes a successful revenge. Shakespeare inherits a plot of delay and performance, then removes its certainty.

François de Belleforest

The revenge story gains inward pressure

Belleforest retells the Amleth material in French, enlarging motives and moral reflection. His version helps bridge the medieval narrative and the theatrical story known in Shakespeare’s London.

The lost play

The Ur-Hamlet remains a gap

Writers in the 1580s and 1590s allude to an earlier stage Hamlet, now lost. We cannot inspect its script or state exactly how Shakespeare used it. A responsible source history leaves that uncertainty in place.

Context should alter the scene.

The guide ranges widely. These four pressures are most useful when attached to a particular choice in the play.

  • Revenge and public justiceThe Ghost gives Hamlet a private command in a court whose public institutions are already compromised. Compare his delay with Laertes’s speed and ask whether either route produces justice rather than further succession of violence.
  • Confessional uncertaintyThe Ghost speaks of purgatorial suffering, while Hamlet studies at Wittenberg and fears a devil may assume a pleasing shape. The scene gives him reasons to question what he has seen without reducing the delay to indecision.
  • Succession and the stateThe play belongs to the last years of an ageing, childless Elizabeth I. Denmark’s damaged succession and Fortinbras’s arrival could carry political force for early audiences, but the parallel should be offered as a lens rather than a one-to-one allegory.
  • Theatre inside theatreHamlet uses acting to test a story, instructs the Players and performs versions of himself before different audiences. Every production then adds another layer by cutting, moving and embodying the early texts.

    • Turn a contextual fact into a reading.

    Begin with a line or staging decision. Then use the history to make the interpretation more exact.

    Text

    Compare an actual difference

    Find a line present in Q2 but absent from F1, or compare the location of a speech in Q1 with the later texts. Explain what changes for pace, motive or performance. “There are three texts” is background; the difference is analysis.

    Context

    Choose the pressure the scene needs

    The Ghost may call for religious history; the final entrance of Fortinbras may call for succession; the Players call for theatrical conditions. One relevant context used closely will usually do more than several facts listed together.

    Judgement

    Keep possibility distinct from proof

    Write “may have resonated” when an audience response cannot be documented, and “editors differ” when the evidence does not settle one text. Careful uncertainty is not weakness; it is historical accuracy.

    A useful edition check. Look at the symbols or editorial note in the Hamlet you are using. The Folger text marks F1-only language with pointed brackets and Q2-only language with square brackets. Once you can see the joins, the apparently seamless modern play becomes a record of choices.

    See the early texts for yourself.

    Folger gives a clear introduction to the three versions and links to digitised early copies. Its Lost Plays Database sets out the evidence and competing theories around the earlier Hamlet tradition.
