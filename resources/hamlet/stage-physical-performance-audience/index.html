Inside the PDF

A play understood through the act of watching.

The guide moves from the physical conditions of performance into the special knowledge given to spectators. Its central question is simple: what can the audience see, hear or suspect that one person on stage cannot?

Pages 2–4

Shared stage space

Early modern spectators and actors occupied the same light. A thrust stage brought action into a crowd that could see itself watching, so court ceremony, public grief and apparent privacy were always exposed to communal judgement.

Use this section to move beyond a vague reference to the Globe. The useful point is not that the theatre had few sets; it is that entrances, levels, doors and visible spectators made space active.

Pages 5–7

Uneven knowledge

The Ghost, the arras, staged meetings and soliloquies distribute information unevenly. Spectators may know that someone is hiding, see a figure another character cannot see or hear a thought before it becomes public action.

This produces more than dramatic irony. It makes the audience practise judgement while knowing that its privileged view is still incomplete.

Pages 8–10

Acting and the body

Hamlet instructs the players, watches Claudius watch a murder and repeatedly tests whether outward behaviour can disclose inward truth. The final pages return that question to physical action.

The graveyard and duel make bodies, objects and speed impossible to treat as decoration. Thought becomes theatrical when it changes posture, distance, rhythm or risk.

Six stage pictures that organise the play.

Each moment joins a piece of text to a visible problem. They are useful starting points for lessons, rehearsal or an essay paragraph.

  • The Ghost on the battlementsDecide where it first appears, who sees it and whether it seems to command the space or remain at its edge. The audience’s belief in the revenge story begins with this physical encounter.
  • The court watches HamletIn the first court scene, clothing, distance and stillness can make grief either private resistance or public display. Hamlet is not simply mourning; he is being observed while he does it.
  • Listeners behind the arrasConcealment turns rooms into traps. If the audience can see the hidden figures, every apparently private exchange acquires a second set of reactions and another source of danger.
  • The MousetrapActors perform for the Danish court while Hamlet and Horatio study Claudius, and the theatre audience studies them all. Arrange the watchers and the scene becomes a visible chain of interpretation.
  • The graveyardA skull is evidence, prop and human remain. Hamlet’s handling of it gives abstract thought about death a weight, texture and social history that speech alone cannot supply.
  • The duelCups, swords, exchanged weapons and changing bodies carry the plot at speed. Clear staging must let spectators understand the trap while preserving the characters’ unequal knowledge of it.

    • Write about performance without inventing a whole production.

    A strong paragraph needs one exact choice and its consequence, not a catalogue of costumes, lighting and gestures.

    Text first

    Find the physical instruction already present

    Begin with a stage direction, entrance, object, interruption, form of address or reference to looking. State what the scene requires before suggesting what a production might choose.

    One choice

    Make the decision precise

    Place a listener in view, let Hamlet cross a boundary, hold a silence after a line or decide when a weapon becomes visible. Name who notices the choice and who does not.

    Audience effect

    Explain what spectators must now judge

    Does the choice create sympathy, suspicion, fear or uncomfortable laughter? Does it confirm Hamlet’s control or show him losing it? The effect matters because it changes the argument of the scene.
