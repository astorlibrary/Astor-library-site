A route through the tragedy

The plot is easiest to remember when each event is tied to the decision that makes the next one possible.

  • 1.3 — The first predictionThe Weird Sisters do not command Macbeth to kill Duncan. They give him a future to imagine, and his own response supplies the violence.
  • 1.7 — The choiceMacbeth can state the political, moral and personal reasons not to murder Duncan. The scene matters because he acts with full knowledge of them.
  • 2.2 — The murder offstageShakespeare keeps Duncan’s death out of view and fixes attention on Macbeth and Lady Macbeth immediately afterwards: their sounds, hands and broken sentences.
  • 3.4 — The public collapseBanquo’s ghost turns private guilt into a spectacle before the Scottish nobles. Macbeth remains king, but the performance of secure rule has failed.
  • 4.1 — The second visitMacbeth goes looking for the Sisters and hears only the reassuring surface of their answers. His confidence now depends upon language he has chosen to misread.
  • 5.1–5.8 — Separation and consequenceLady Macbeth walks alone, Malcolm’s forces move towards Dunsinane and Macduff confronts the tyrant. The marriage that planned the first murder no longer shares a scene.

    • Three threads to follow

    These are not separate themes. Shakespeare keeps bringing them into the same scenes.

    Speech

    Equivocation

    The Sisters speak truths that invite false conclusions. Macbeth gradually adopts the same habit, hiding intention behind ceremony and asking murderers to do what he will not name openly. When studying a quotation, ask both what it says and what a listener wants it to mean.

    Body

    Blood and sleep

    Blood begins as evidence that can be washed away, then becomes an image the mind cannot dismiss. Sleep moves in the opposite direction: from ordinary rest to the peace Macbeth believes he has destroyed. Their repetition charts damage more clearly than a simple count of crimes.

    Rule

    Kingship and tyranny

    Duncan’s murder is not only a private sin. It breaks hospitality, succession and public trust. Macbeth can seize the crown, but he cannot make his rule legitimate; the play measures power by what happens to households, loyalties and the country beyond the palace.

    Use the PDF well

    Read the summary first if the sequence is uncertain. On a second pass, choose one turning point and trace a repeated image through an earlier and a later scene. That gives an essay an argument about change, rather than a row of disconnected quotations. The main Macbeth page adds text history, performance and further reading.
