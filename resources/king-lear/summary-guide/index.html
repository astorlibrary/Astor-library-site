Study tip. Revise Lear’s family and Gloucester’s family side by side; their parallels make the play’s ideas about sight, loyalty and authority easier to trace.

Keep the two families together

The play’s double plot is not a main story with a smaller copy attached. Lear and Gloucester make different mistakes, but each misreads a loyal child and gives power to those who will use it against him.

Lear’s house

Love becomes a public test

Lear asks his daughters to perform devotion before he divides the kingdom. Cordelia refuses exaggeration; Kent challenges the decision; Goneril and Regan inherit both land and the authority their father still expects to command.

Gloucester’s house

A forged letter replaces knowledge

Edmund turns his father’s anxiety about legitimacy into evidence against Edgar. Gloucester trusts the performance of loyalty and rejects the son whose disguise will later let him remain close.

The crossing point

Private error becomes civil disaster

The families meet at Gloucester’s castle. Lear is shut out, Kent is punished, Cornwall and Regan take control, and Gloucester’s attempt to help the king leads to his blinding. Household power and political power can no longer be separated.

Six turning points

Use these scenes to rebuild the plot, then ask what each changes about authority, knowledge or care.

  • 1.1 — The divisionLear tries to keep the name and privileges of kingship after surrendering its material power. Cordelia’s banishment and Kent’s exile make truth politically dangerous from the beginning.
  • 2.4 — The doors closeGoneril and Regan reduce Lear’s followers and refuse his demands. The scene exposes how quickly command becomes dependence once other people control shelter and force.
  • 3.2–3.4 — The storm and the hovelThe weather does not simply mirror madness. It strips the king of protection and brings him into contact with the “poor naked wretches” he had rarely imagined while ruling.
  • 3.7 — Gloucester is blindedCornwall and Regan turn punishment into spectacle. Gloucester loses physical sight after gaining moral knowledge, while a servant’s resistance shows that obedience has limits.
  • 4.6–4.7 — Dover and recognitionEdgar guides his despairing father through an invented fall; Lear meets Gloucester; Cordelia restores care without undoing the harm already done.
  • 5.3 — The final returnLear enters carrying Cordelia. The image defeats any easy promise that suffering produces justice, even though the play has made acts of service, recognition and forgiveness matter.

    • Three words to trace

    Sight moves between eyesight, judgement and recognition. Nature can mean family bond, appetite, cosmic order or ruthless self-interest depending on who is speaking. Nothing begins as Cordelia’s refusal and returns whenever language, property or identity is stripped away. Following one word through several speakers will produce a stronger argument than collecting quotations by character alone.
