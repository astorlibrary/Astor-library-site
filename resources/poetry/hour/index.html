Hour, Carol Ann Duffy.

An hour is brief, ordinary and easily spent. Duffy’s sonnet makes it richer than flowers, wine, jewels or artificial light. This guide follows the poem’s argument about time and value without reducing it to a list of images.

From shortage to abundance.

The poem begins by making love poor in time. It ends with value multiplying. Everything between those points tests what an hour can hold.

The opening

Time controls the supply

The compressed phrase “Love’s time’s beggar” makes love dependent on something it cannot own. Yet the dropped coin immediately complicates the poverty. The hour may be small and found by chance, but attention makes it shine.

The middle

Ordinary things defeat luxury

The lovers do not need purchased emblems of romance. Summer sky, a ditch, hair, light and a blade of grass become their materials. Duffy does not merely say that nature is beautiful; she shows value being remade by the way one person sees another.

The ending

Love answers time with making

Time is finally named as hostile, but it does not receive the last action. Love spins rather than owns: it turns the plain material of an hour into abundance. The fairy-tale echo makes the transformation triumphant while retaining the knowledge that magic is temporary.

Read the form as pressure.

The poem has fourteen lines and enters the long tradition of the love sonnet, but its language is conversational, mobile and full of economic exchange.

One sentence

The moment keeps moving

Much of the poem runs forward through enjambment and linked clauses. The lovers have only an hour, so the syntax resists stopping even while the speaker wants time itself to slow.

Here. Now.

Two full stops hold the present

The brief fragments interrupt that movement. They refuse a grander location or a promised future: value exists in this place and this instant. On the page, the punctuation makes the reader pause where the lovers wish time would pause.

Sound and return

Gold gathers force

Wealth words recur from begging and coins to treasure, millionaires, jewels and gold. The final repetition does not introduce a new idea. It releases the abundance that the whole poem has been making from apparent poverty.

Build the paragraph around value.

Instead of naming every image, follow one change from the start of the poem to the end.

Claim

Love cannot defeat duration

Duffy does not pretend the hour will last. Its power comes from changing the value of limited time, not escaping limitation altogether. That distinction gives the argument more weight.

Evidence

Use a pair, not a pile

Place the dropped coin beside the final gold, or conventional flowers beside cuckoo spit. A pair lets you explain movement and contrast rather than attaching the same point to several quotations.

Interpretation

Keep the fairy tale double-edged

Midas and spun gold bring wonder, but both stories also know that transformation has a cost or cannot last. Decide whether those echoes darken the poem’s confidence or make its brief triumph more precious.

A comparison worth trying. Compare the way another love poem handles duration. Does it promise permanence through memory, writing or marriage, or does it accept that value can belong to a moment precisely because the moment ends?

Duffy and the modern love sonnet.

Hour appears in Rapture, Duffy’s 2005 sequence of love poems. The Poetry Archive profile places that collection within her career and includes recordings of Duffy reading other work.
