Voice is part of the argument

The poems do not only describe communities under pressure. Their language and performance create a public space in which those communities speak for themselves.

Language

Creole refuses a borrowed centre

Johnson’s spelling and grammar are not departures from an imagined neutral English. They carry history, sound and authority, asking the reader to meet the poem on its own linguistic ground.

Performance

Rhythm gathers an audience

The printed line is one part of the work. Repetition, pace and dub performance turn individual testimony into an address that can be heard and answered collectively.

Policing

Power enters ordinary space

Streets, homes and workplaces become political through surveillance and confrontation. The poems measure not an abstract institution but its daily effect upon movement, safety and belonging.

A useful way in. Read the lines aloud before paraphrasing them. Sound will not replace analysis, but it will reveal emphasis, pace and collective address that the page alone can hide.
