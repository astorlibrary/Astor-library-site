American.

American fiction shaped by the sea, civil war and the northern wilderness, with publication histories, historical context, visual records and adaptations.

1851

Moby-Dick; or, The Whale, Herman Melville

Two Astor editions trace the novel’s British and American publication in 1851 and Melville’s maritime sources, including the Essex disaster and Mocha Dick. Readings focus on nineteenth-century whaling, Ishmael’s narrative method, Ahab’s authority, key passages and later stage and screen versions.

1895

The Red Badge of Courage, Stephen Crane

Crane places Henry Fleming’s fear, flight, shame and accidental wound within the Civil War and Chancellorsville contexts. It also considers the 1895 D. Appleton book edition, Crane’s psychological realism and colour imagery, and John Huston’s 1951 film.

1903

The Call of the Wild, Jack London

Buck’s journey from a California estate to the Klondike, the sled team and the wolf pack opens questions about naturalism, labour, hunger and violence. Its publication and screen history show how Buck’s story has been repeatedly reshaped.
