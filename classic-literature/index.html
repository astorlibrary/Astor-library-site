There is no single doorway into classic literature. One reader may arrive through the quarrel between Achilles and Agamemnon, another through Elizabeth Bennet’s first judgement of Mr Darcy, and another through the locked door of Dr Jekyll’s laboratory. What joins the books here is not a claim that they are beyond question. They have lasted because readers keep finding new questions to ask of them. These pages set each work in its own time, follow what happens in it and trace the forms in which later readers have encountered it.

Reading the books

The text stays at the centre.

An Astor book page is not a substitute for reading the work. It is a place to get your bearings and then look more closely. The opening account tells you what the book does without reducing it to a string of themes. Publication, historical setting and form are explained where they matter. Important passages are placed in context, and adaptations are treated as interpretations rather than as a decorative list.

The published Astor Editions carry that same method into the book. Prose works have chapter summaries and factual context; Shakespeare editions use scene divisions, line numbers and explanatory notes. Study editions go further into quotation, criticism and writing for readers preparing lessons or examinations.
