The War of the Worlds, H. G. Wells

A strange cylinder falls on Horsell Common near Woking. When it opens, the Martians reveal weapons and machines far beyond anything Victorian Britain can resist. The narrator watches order fail: soldiers burn, refugees choke the roads, trains and newspapers lose authority, and London becomes a city of panic.

That reversal is the point. Britain, accustomed to imagining itself as the stronger civilisation, is suddenly observed, attacked and displaced as though it were the weaker species. Wells makes an invasion story carry an argument about empire without slowing the terrifying movement from Horsell Common to the emptied city.

At a glance

How the invasion first appeared and how it overturns Victorian assumptions.

  • Original serial The British serial ran in Pearson's Magazine from April to December 1897.
  • Book publication William Heinemann published the first British book edition in 1898.
  • American route The American serial appeared in Cosmopolitan; unauthorised American newspaper versions relocated the invasion to familiar local settings.
  • Woking setting Wells lived in Woking during the period when he planned major early scientific romances.
  • Martian technology The Heat-Ray, black smoke and tripods turn invasion into a problem of industrialised asymmetry.
  • Imperial reversal The book asks British readers to imagine being treated as colonised subjects, animals or insects.
  • Microbial ending Human victory is not military. The Martians die because they have no inherited resistance to Earth bacteria.
  • Afterlife The story has been repeatedly remade through radio, film, television, comics, music and immersive live events.

    • Text and publication history

    The book belongs to late-Victorian magazine culture as well as to early science fiction. Its first readers met the invasion in monthly instalments.

    1866-1946

    H. G. Wells

    • Herbert George Wells trained in science and studied under T. H. Huxley.
    • His early scientific romances include The Time Machine, The Island of Doctor Moreau, The Invisible Man and The War of the Worlds.
    • Those books link speculative invention with social, biological and political argument.

    April-December 1897

    Pearson's Magazine

    • The British serial appeared across nine monthly instalments.
    • Serial form suited the story's repeated cliff edges: first sighting, cylinder opening, Heat-Ray, military failure, flight and aftermath.

    1898

    First book edition

    • William Heinemann published the first British book edition.
    • Harper & Brothers issued the American book edition.
    • The shift from instalment to volume made the invasion readable as a complete historical testimony.

    Woking and Horsell Common

    The novel is precise about Surrey and London geography. The first landing site makes the invasion local before it becomes national.

    Horsell Common

    The first cylinder

    The first Martian cylinder lands on common land rather than in a symbolic capital city, making the opening feel provincial, witnessed and reportable. The crowd gathers around it like spectators at a local curiosity until the Heat-Ray changes the scale of the event.

    London

    Panic, roads and mass movement

    London is not protected by its size or administrative importance. The exodus turns the empire's capital into a human bottleneck, and the novel becomes especially strong on crowds, rumour, failed systems and the collapse of confidence.

    Scientific romance and Victorian science

    Wells grounds the terror in extrapolated science: astronomy, evolution, weaponry, physiology, disease and technological inequality.

  • Mars Late nineteenth-century readers knew Mars through popular astronomy and speculation about life.
  • Heat-Ray The weapon kills before most witnesses understand what it is, making courage and formation nearly useless.
  • Tripods The fighting-machines condense the novel's fear of mobile, impersonal technology.
  • Bacteria The Martians defeat human organisation but fail against microscopic life.
  • Imperial reversal Britain is made vulnerable to the kind of technological asymmetry associated with imperial violence.
  • Witness form The narrator survives, observes and records; he does not defeat the Martians.

    • Adaptations

    Each major adaptation relocates or reframes the invasion to make it immediate for a new audience.

    1938

    Orson Welles radio broadcast

    • Broadcast by CBS as an episode of The Mercury Theatre on the Air on 30 October 1938.
    • Howard Koch's adaptation moved the opening landing to Grovers Mill, New Jersey.
    • The simulated news-bulletin format became one of the most famous events in radio history.

    1953

    George Pal film

    • Directed by Byron Haskin and produced by George Pal.
    • The film relocated the invasion to mid-century America.
    • Its flying war machines replaced the tripods, shaping Cold War visual memory of the story.

    1978

    Jeff Wayne musical version

    • Jeff Wayne's Musical Version of The War of the Worlds turned the novel into a concept album.
    • Richard Burton's narration gave the project a strong link to Wells's witness structure.

    2005

    Steven Spielberg film

    • Directed by Steven Spielberg and starring Tom Cruise.
    • The film returns strongly to family survival, refugee movement and sudden urban disaster.

    2019

    BBC television version

    • The BBC version returned the story to a period British setting.
    • Its handling of empire, gender and aftermath made the adaptation less straightforwardly triumphal.

    The invasion is recorded, not conquered

    Wells denies the narrator a conventional heroic victory. His importance lies in observing systems fail and piecing together what happened after survival became possible.

    The narrator

    A trained observer can still misread

    He begins with astronomical and evolutionary language, but knowledge does not make him calm or complete. He reports fragments, follows rumours and sometimes understands an event only after danger has passed.

    The retrospective voice gives order to experience that was chaotic at the time. The novel’s authority comes from reconstruction, not from an all-seeing witness.

    The curate

    Catastrophe overwhelms explanation

    The curate turns disaster into a religious crisis centred on human sin and divine punishment. His panic is destructive, but Wells also shows the narrator becoming harsh as hunger and concealment reduce both men’s capacity for sympathy.

    The ruined house makes interpretation a survival problem: speech itself can reveal them to the Martians.

    The artilleryman

    A grand plan replaces difficult work

    He imagines an underground society that will preserve the strongest humans, learn Martian technology and eventually fight back. The scheme sounds energetic after the narrator’s despair.

    Its weakness appears in practice. The small trench and neglected labour expose a fantasy more interested in future power than in the discipline needed to build a life.

    Six stages of collapse

    The Martians do not merely destroy buildings. Each advance removes another belief about distance, authority or human importance.

  • The cylinder opensThe crowd treats the pit as a spectacle and assumes that whatever arrives can be watched at a safe distance. The Heat-Ray ends that confidence before officials or soldiers understand the threat.
  • The fighting-machines riseThe tripods turn familiar Surrey roads and fields into exposed ground. Their height and speed make human formations look like remnants of an older military age.
  • The Thunder Child attacksThe torpedo ram briefly damages the invaders and protects escaping ships. Its sacrifice supplies a rare image of effective resistance, but the larger technological imbalance remains.
  • London emptiesNews, trains and roads cannot organise the population they helped to gather. Rumour travels faster than reliable instruction, and the imperial capital becomes a mass of competing attempts to escape.
  • The red weed spreadsMartian life begins to remake the English landscape, suggesting colonisation at a biological level. Its later failure anticipates the invaders’ own vulnerability to earthly organisms.
  • The still machinesThe Martians die from bacteria rather than battle. Humanity survives through an inherited relation to the environment, not because it proved more rational, moral or technologically capable.

    • After the ending

    Victory does not restore the narrator to the person he was before the first cylinder fell.

    Knowledge

    The universe has become less secure

    The invasion is over, but Mars remains in the sky and other worlds remain possible. Scientific curiosity now carries the memory of technological inferiority and surprise.

    Home

    Return repeats the shape of grief

    The narrator reaches his damaged house expecting emptiness, then is reunited with his wife. The private recovery matters because the invasion repeatedly turned homes into ruins, hiding places and abandoned property.

    Empire

    The reversal cannot be forgotten

    Britain has experienced flight, dispossession and the contempt of a technologically stronger force. The ending leaves readers to decide whether that imaginative reversal changes how imperial power should be understood.
