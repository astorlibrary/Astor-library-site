The War of the Worlds, H. G. Wells

A strange cylinder falls on Horsell Common near Woking. When it opens, the Martians reveal weapons and machines far beyond anything Victorian Britain can resist. The narrator watches order fail: soldiers burn, refugees choke the roads, trains and newspapers lose authority, and London becomes a city of panic.

The novel is not only an invasion story. It reverses the imperial gaze. Britain, accustomed to imagining itself as the stronger civilisation, is suddenly treated as the weaker species: observed, attacked, displaced and nearly exterminated by a more advanced power.

Records worth knowing

Short factual points that frame the novel before reading, teaching or comparing adaptations.

  • Original serial The British serial ran in Pearson's Magazine from April to December 1897.
  • Book publication William Heinemann published the first British book edition in 1898.
  • American route The American serial appeared in Cosmopolitan; unauthorised American newspaper versions relocated the invasion to familiar local settings.
  • Woking setting Wells lived in Woking during the period when he planned major early scientific romances.
  • Martian technology The Heat-Ray, black smoke and tripods turn invasion into a problem of industrialised asymmetry.
  • Imperial reversal The book asks British readers to imagine being treated as colonised subjects, animals or insects.
  • Evolutionary pressure The Martians are terrifying because they are intelligent, adapted and technologically organised, not because they are supernatural.
  • Microbial ending Human victory is not military. The Martians die because they have no inherited resistance to Earth bacteria.
  • Afterlife The story has been repeatedly remade through radio, film, television, comics, music and immersive live events.

    • Text and publication history

    The book belongs to late-Victorian magazine culture as well as to early science fiction. Its first readers met the invasion in monthly instalments.

    1866-1946

    H. G. Wells

    • Herbert George Wells trained in science and studied under T. H. Huxley.
    • His early scientific romances include The Time Machine, The Island of Doctor Moreau, The Invisible Man and The War of the Worlds.
    • Those books link speculative invention with social, biological and political argument.

    1895-97

    Composition and Woking

    • Wells lived at Maybury Road, Woking, during the years associated with the novel's planning.
    • The local geography matters: Horsell Common, Maybury Hill, Byfleet, Weybridge and London form the invasion route.
    • The real map makes the impossible event feel reportable.

    April-December 1897

    Pearson's Magazine

    • The British serial appeared across nine monthly instalments.
    • Serial form suited the story's repeated cliff edges: first sighting, cylinder opening, Heat-Ray, military failure, flight and aftermath.
    • The magazine context placed Wells beside popular journalism, politics and speculative fiction.

    1897

    Cosmopolitan serial

    • The American magazine serial appeared in the same year.
    • US newspaper versions also adapted the invasion into American settings.
    • This localisation becomes a pattern in later adaptations, including the 1938 radio broadcast.

    1898

    First book edition

    • William Heinemann published the first British book edition.
    • Harper & Brothers issued the American book edition.
    • The shift from instalment to volume made the invasion readable as a complete historical testimony.

    1924

    Atlantic Edition

    • Wells later revised and standardised much of his work in collected editions.
    • The 1924 Atlantic Edition became important for later textual history.
    • Modern editions often have to choose between serial, first book and later revised readings.

    Place record: Woking and Horsell Common

    The novel is precise about Surrey and London geography. The first landing site makes the invasion local before it becomes national.

    Horsell Common

    The first cylinder

    • The first Martian cylinder lands on common land rather than in a symbolic capital city.
    • That choice makes the opening feel provincial, witnessed and reportable.
    • The first crowd around the cylinder behaves like spectators at a local curiosity before the Heat-Ray changes the scale of the event.

    Maybury Hill

    Narrator's vantage point

    • The narrator's position near Woking lets Wells combine domestic routine with disaster observation.
    • Normal social life continues for a short time beside impossible danger.
    • The gap between what people know and what is already happening creates much of the early tension.

    London

    Panic, roads and mass movement

    • London is not protected by its size or administrative importance.
    • The exodus from London turns empire's capital into a human bottleneck.
    • The novel is especially strong on crowds, rumour, failed systems and the collapse of confidence.

    Scientific romance and Victorian science

    Wells does not use magic. The terror comes from extrapolated science: astronomy, evolution, weapons, physiology, disease and technological inequality.

    Mars

    Astronomy and speculation

    • Late nineteenth-century readers knew Mars through popular astronomy, observation reports and speculation about life.
    • Wells turns Mars into an old, cooling world under pressure.
    • The Martian invasion begins as ecological desperation as much as conquest.

    Heat-Ray

    Invisible force and modern weaponry

    • The Heat-Ray is terrifying because it kills before most witnesses understand what it is.
    • It makes courage, crowd size and military formation nearly useless.
    • The weapon turns modern science into a theatre of unequal power.

    Tripods

    Machine bodies

    • The fighting-machines are part vehicle, part animal image, part industrial weapon.
    • Their height gives the Martians a predator's view of human movement.
    • Later visual culture often centres the tripod because it condenses the novel's fear of mobile, impersonal technology.

    Bacteria

    Evolutionary ending

    • The Martians defeat human organisation but fail against microscopic life.
    • That ending shifts the scale from empire and artillery to biology.
    • The narrator's relief is uneasy because humanity survives without proving itself morally or militarily superior.

    Imperialism and social collapse

    Wells writes from the centre of the British Empire and makes that centre feel breakable.

    Imperial reversal

    Britain as the invaded land

    • The Martians treat humans with the indifference imperial powers often showed colonised people.
    • The narrator explicitly asks readers to consider human treatment of other species and peoples.
    • The invasion makes British superiority look like a local assumption, not a universal fact.

    The artilleryman

    Survival fantasy

    • The artilleryman's underground plan sounds energetic but becomes evasive and impractical.
    • He imagines a new human elite surviving beneath Martian rule.
    • His failure exposes the difference between talking about reconstruction and doing the work.

    The curate

    Religious collapse

    • The curate cannot process disaster except through broken fear and spiritual panic.
    • His breakdown traps the narrator in a moral crisis as well as a physical one.
    • Wells uses him to test what happens when inherited explanations fail.

    The narrator

    Witness rather than hero

    • The narrator survives, observes and records; he does not defeat the Martians.
    • His authority depends on testimony, memory and interpretation.
    • The book often feels like a retrospective report after a national trauma.

    Chronological record

    Dated points for reading, teaching and cross-reference.

    1866

    Wells born

    • Herbert George Wells was born in Bromley, Kent.
    • His education and early work gave him direct experience of class pressure, teaching, science and journalism.

    1895

    The Time Machine

    • Wells's first major scientific romance appeared in book form.
    • It established many of his recurring interests: time, evolution, class, technology and speculative narrative.

    1896

    The Island of Doctor Moreau

    • Wells pushed biological speculation into horror and ethical argument.
    • The book is a useful companion for studying Wells's use of science without comfort.

    1897

    The Invisible Man and serial invasion

    • The Invisible Man was published in book form.
    • The War of the Worlds appeared serially in Britain and the United States.

    1898

    The War of the Worlds in book form

    • The first British book edition appeared from Heinemann.
    • The novel quickly became one of Wells's most durable speculative premises.

    1901

    The First Men in the Moon

    • Wells continued to use other-world travel to examine human politics and social assumptions.
    • It is often read beside The War of the Worlds for Wells's interest in space, empire and civilisation.

    1946

    Wells dies

    • Wells died in London.
    • By then, his early scientific romances had become foundation texts for later science fiction.

    Adaptation record

    Every major adaptation solves the same problem differently: how to make invasion immediate for its own audience.

    1938

    Orson Welles radio broadcast

    • Broadcast by CBS as an episode of The Mercury Theatre on the Air on 30 October 1938.
    • Howard Koch's adaptation moved the opening landing to Grovers Mill, New Jersey.
    • The simulated news-bulletin format became one of the most famous events in radio history.
    • Later accounts often exaggerate the scale of panic, but the broadcast's media afterlife remains central to the story's reputation.

    1953

    George Pal film

    • Directed by Byron Haskin and produced by George Pal.
    • The film relocated the invasion to mid-century America.
    • Its flying war machines replaced the tripods, shaping Cold War visual memory of the story.

    1978

    Jeff Wayne musical version

    • Jeff Wayne's Musical Version of The War of the Worlds turned the novel into a concept album.
    • Richard Burton's narration gave the project a strong link to Wells's witness structure.
    • The album's touring afterlife made the story part of live spectacular culture.

    1988-90

    Television sequel

    • The syndicated television series treated the 1953 film's events as backstory.
    • It shows how Wells's premise could become continuing franchise mythology.
    • The title survived even when the plot moved far from Wells's structure.

    2005

    Steven Spielberg film

    • Directed by Steven Spielberg and starring Tom Cruise.
    • The film returns strongly to family survival, refugee movement and sudden urban disaster.
    • Post-9/11 imagery shaped much of its atmosphere and reception.

    2019

    BBC television version

    • The BBC version returned the story to a period British setting.
    • Its handling of empire, gender and aftermath made the adaptation less straightforwardly triumphal.
    • It records a modern attempt to read Wells through late-imperial anxiety.

    Key passages for study

    These passages carry much of the novel's analytical weight.

  • Opening astronomy Frames human confidence against Martian observation and planetary scale.
  • The first cylinder Turns local curiosity into first contact.
  • The Heat-Ray Shows the first full break between human expectation and Martian power.
  • The Thunder Child A rare scene of human resistance, but still not a strategic victory.
  • The London exodus Social order becomes crowd movement, rumour and survival pressure.
  • The ruined house The narrator and curate are trapped beside the Martians, turning spectacle into claustrophobia.
  • The artilleryman's plan Survival rhetoric collapses into idleness and fantasy.
  • The dead Martians The ending reduces imperial power and technology before bacteria.
  • The final return The narrator survives, but the familiar world is permanently unsettled.

    • Teaching pathways

    Useful routes through the text for study, revision and classroom planning.

    Context

    Empire in reverse

    • Track moments where humans are watched, studied, displaced or destroyed by superior force.
    • Compare this with Victorian imperial assumptions.
    • Ask whether the ending restores human superiority or only human survival.

    Methods

    Report, witness and delayed knowledge

    • Mark where the narrator knows less than the reader expects an invasion narrator to know.
    • Track newspapers, rumours, military reports and eyewitness accounts.
    • Study how Wells builds panic through partial information.

    Science fiction

    Speculation and plausibility

    • Separate impossible invention from plausible scientific framing.
    • Track astronomy, evolution, machines, heat, gas and bacteria.
    • Use those details to explain why the story feels modern rather than supernatural.
