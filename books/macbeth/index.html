Macbeth, William Shakespeare

On a stormy Scottish heath, three strange figures greet Macbeth with a prophecy: he will be king. What begins as a thought becomes an obsession. Driven by ambition, urged on by Lady Macbeth, and haunted by what he must do to seize the crown, Macbeth murders Duncan in his sleep and takes the throne. But the prize brings no security. Friends become threats, blood calls for more blood, Banquo’s ghost returns, Lady Macbeth unravels, and Scotland sinks into fear under a ruler who can no longer turn back.

Fast, violent and psychologically intense, Macbeth remains one of Shakespeare’s most gripping plays: a tragedy of prophecy, guilt, marriage, power and moral collapse. This Astor Library Edition presents the complete play with clear scene summaries and notes, alongside original introductory material on Shakespeare, the play’s sources, performance history, film and stage afterlives, language, imagery and major interpretive questions.

Text, sources and early records

Before the later production record, Macbeth already has a source history, a print history and an early performance witness.

1611 / 1623

Forman’s note and the First Folio

  • Simon Forman’s account of seeing Macbeth at the Globe is the earliest surviving eyewitness record usually cited for the play in performance.
  • Macbeth was not printed in quarto.
  • The 1623 First Folio is the first printed text of the play.
  • The Folio text is short by the standards of Shakespearean tragedy, which is one reason the play’s stage history and textual history are often discussed together.

1587 / c.1606

Holinshed, Banquo and James I

  • Shakespeare drew on Holinshed’s Chronicles for the story of Duncan, Macbeth and Banquo.
  • The play’s treatment of kingship and succession belongs to the Jacobean court culture of James VI and I.
  • James had published Daemonologie in 1597, which helps explain the play’s interest in witchcraft.
  • The Banquo of the play is far more honourable than the Banquo of Holinshed’s account.
  • The Stuart line claimed descent from Banquo, which made the character politically useful on the Jacobean stage.

1040-1057

The historical Macbeth

  • The historical Macbeth ruled Scotland, or Alba, from 1040 until 1057.
  • Duncan was not the aged saintly figure of Shakespeare’s play; Shakespeare reshapes the history into a sharper theatrical murder story.
  • Macbeth’s Scotland was eleventh-century, not the medieval-gothic world often suggested by later stage designs.
  • The play’s famous “Scottishness” is therefore a mixture of chronicle source, Jacobean politics and centuries of performance design.

Restoration and eighteenth-century stage history

The play’s life after the reopening of the theatres was shaped by adaptation, music, spectacle and star actors.

1664-1668

Davenant’s Restoration Macbeth and Pepys

  • Sir William Davenant’s adaptation was performed by the Duke’s Company after the Restoration.
  • The version expanded the witches and made music, song and spectacle central to the performance.
  • Samuel Pepys saw Macbeth several times between 1664 and 1668.
  • On 5 November 1664 Pepys wrote that the play was “admirably acted”.
  • The adaptation helped shape how later audiences expected the witches to appear and sound.

1744 / 1747 / 1785

Garrick, Pritchard and Siddons

  • David Garrick revived Macbeth in 1744 and wrote a new death speech for Macbeth.
  • Hannah Pritchard first played Lady Macbeth opposite Garrick in 1747.
  • Sarah Siddons first played Lady Macbeth at Drury Lane on 2 February 1785.
  • Siddons became one of the defining Lady Macbeths of the eighteenth century.
  • William Beechey painted Siddons with the emblems of tragedy in 1793, fixing her public image as a tragic actress.

Nineteenth-century theatre and visual culture

Macbeth became part of actor-manager theatre, transatlantic rivalry, opera and Victorian image-making.

1820-1851 / 1849

Macready, Forrest and the Astor Place Riot

  • William Charles Macready played Macbeth across more than thirty years, beginning at Covent Garden in 1820.
  • On 10 May 1849, rival Macbeth performances by Macready and Edwin Forrest formed part of the background to the Astor Place Riot in New York.
  • Macready performed at the Astor Opera House; Forrest performed nearby at the Broadway Theatre.
  • More than twenty people were killed.
  • The event shows that Shakespeare performance in the nineteenth century could sit inside arguments about class, national identity and theatre ownership.

1847 / 1865

Verdi’s opera Macbeth

  • Verdi’s Macbeth premiered at the Teatro della Pergola, Florence, on 14 March 1847.
  • The libretto was by Francesco Maria Piave, with later contribution from Andrea Maffei.
  • Verdi revised the opera for Paris in 1865.
  • The Paris version added material required by French grand-opera practice, including ballet music.
  • Verdi’s opera is one of the clearest examples of Macbeth moving beyond Shakespeare’s stage text into a separate performance tradition.

1888 / 1889

Henry Irving, Ellen Terry and Sargent

  • Henry Irving’s Macbeth opened at the Lyceum Theatre, London, on 29 December 1888.
  • Irving played Macbeth opposite Ellen Terry as Lady Macbeth.
  • Arthur Sullivan composed incidental music for the production.
  • John Singer Sargent painted Ellen Terry as Lady Macbeth in 1889 after seeing the production.
  • Terry’s green dress was decorated with iridescent beetle wings; the National Trust holds the costume at Smallhythe Place.
  • The painting became one of the most famous images attached to any Shakespeare performance.

Twentieth century and later

Modern dress, public theatre, Black performance history, studio staging and screen versions changed the look and scale of the play.

1923 / 1936

Barry Jackson and Orson Welles

  • Barry Jackson’s 1923 Birmingham Repertory Theatre production is often cited as a major modern-dress Macbeth.
  • Orson Welles directed the Federal Theatre Project’s Black-cast Macbeth at the Lafayette Theatre, Harlem, opening on 14 April 1936.
  • The setting was moved from Scotland to a Caribbean island associated with Haiti.
  • The production became widely known as the “Voodoo Macbeth”.
  • Surviving footage appears in the WPA film We Work Again.

1976-1978

Trevor Nunn: Ian McKellen and Judi Dench

  • Nunn’s production opened at The Other Place on 9 September 1976.
  • The small-scale production became one of the RSC’s defining modern Macbeths.
  • It transferred to Newcastle, the Royal Shakespeare Theatre, the Donmar Warehouse and the Young Vic.
  • Thames Television filmed the production in March 1978.
  • Ian McKellen won a London theatre critics’ best actor award; Judi Dench won a SWET best actress award.

1999 / 2011 / 2018 / 2023

Later RSC records

  • Gregory Doran’s 1999 RSC production starred Antony Sher and Harriet Walter.
  • Michael Boyd’s 2011 RSC production starred Jonathan Slinger and Aislín McGuckin.
  • Polly Findlay’s 2018 RSC production starred Christopher Eccleston and Niamh Cusack.
  • Wils Wilson’s 2023 RSC production starred Reuben Joseph and Valene Kane.
  • Daniel Raggett’s 2025 production returned Macbeth to The Other Place with Sam Heughan and Lia Williams.

Recent productions and stage afterlives

Recent productions are selected only where the record gives clear cast, venue or production evidence.

2021

Yaël Farber: Almeida Theatre

  • The Almeida production ran from 2 October to 27 November 2021.
  • Yaël Farber directed.
  • James McArdle played Macbeth.
  • Saoirse Ronan made her UK stage debut as Lady Macbeth.
  • The Almeida record lists production photographs by Marc Brenner and creative credits including Soutra Gilmour, Joanna Scotcher, Tim Lutkin and Peter Rice.

2023-2025

Max Webster: Donmar Warehouse

  • The Donmar production opened after previews from 8 December 2023 and ran until 10 February 2024.
  • Max Webster directed.
  • David Tennant played Macbeth and Cush Jumbo played Lady Macbeth.
  • The production used binaural sound, with audience members wearing headphones.
  • The filmed stage production was scheduled for cinema release from February 2025.

2010 / 2013 / 2023

Other modern records

  • David Greig’s Dunsinane continues the story after Macbeth’s death and was first staged by the RSC in 2010.
  • Kenneth Branagh and Rob Ashford co-directed Macbeth for Manchester International Festival in 2013; Branagh played Macbeth opposite Alex Kingston as Lady Macbeth.
  • Simon Godwin directed a site-specific Macbeth starring Ralph Fiennes and Indira Varma in 2023-24.

Screen versions

Film versions often treat Macbeth as a test of location, landscape, violence and camera style.

1948 / 1957 / 1971 / 2015 / 2021

Major screen versions

  • Orson Welles directed and starred in Macbeth in 1948 for Republic Pictures / Mercury Productions.
  • Akira Kurosawa’s Throne of Blood relocated the play to feudal Japan and was released in 1957.
  • Roman Polanski’s 1971 film starred Jon Finch and Francesca Annis, with a screenplay by Polanski and Kenneth Tynan.
  • Justin Kurzel’s 2015 film starred Michael Fassbender and Marion Cotillard.
  • Joel Coen’s The Tragedy of Macbeth starred Denzel Washington and Frances McDormand and was released by A24 and Apple TV+.
