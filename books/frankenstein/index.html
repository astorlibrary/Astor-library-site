Mary Shelley

Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus

Victor Frankenstein gives life to a being he immediately abandons, setting creator and creation on a path of loneliness, violence and revenge. The novel’s nested voices never let either of them own the story completely, and its 1818 and 1831 forms continue to speak differently to readers.

Two Astor editions. The commentary edition is made for close study of the 1818 and 1831 texts. The introduced edition offers Shelley’s 1831 text with a new essay to read before or after the novel.

Complete text with commentary

Frankenstein, Mary Shelley

First published anonymously in 1818 and substantially revised in 1831, Frankenstein follows Victor Frankenstein’s creation of a living being assembled from dead matter. The Creature’s rejection, isolation and demand for recognition drive the novel from Geneva to the Arctic.

The commentary edition reads the novel in company with both of its major early forms. Chapter companions and essays move between Shelley’s revisions, Romantic science, galvanism and the book’s reception, while the chronology, glossary and bibliography make it easier to follow an interest without breaking the reading experience apart.

At a glance

Key details about the novel’s narration, publication and cultural history.

  • Anonymous 1818 The first edition did not name Mary Shelley on the title page.
  • Godwin dedication The first edition was dedicated to William Godwin, Shelley’s father.
  • Layered narration Walton frames Victor’s narrative, and Victor frames the Creature’s account.
  • 1818 / 1831 The two major authorial versions differ in tone, emphasis and explanation.
  • No laboratory assistant The familiar assistant figure belongs to stage and film afterlives, not Shelley’s novel.
  • Name confusion Frankenstein is Victor’s name. The being is usually called the Creature, not Frankenstein, in serious textual discussion.

    • Text and publication

    The novel has two major textual lives: the anonymous 1818 three-volume first edition and the revised 1831 one-volume edition for Bentley’s Standard Novels.

    1818

    First edition: anonymous, three volumes

    • Published in London by Lackington, Hughes, Harding, Mavor & Jones.
    • Issued anonymously, with a preface by Percy Bysshe Shelley.
    • The title page uses the full title: Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus.
    • The Milton epigraph from Paradise Lost frames the Creature through creation, accusation and abandonment.

    Manuscript record

    Composition and revision

    • The manuscript record shows the novel as a worked text, not a single unaltered inspiration.
    • The famous creation scene was drafted and revised before publication.
    • The Shelley-Godwin Archive is a key reference point for manuscript study.

    1831

    Bentley edition and new introduction

    • The 1831 edition appeared in Bentley’s Standard Novels.
    • Shelley added a new introduction explaining the ghost-story competition and the novel’s origin.
    • The text was revised in ways that make fate, providence and moral warning more explicit.
    • The frontispiece was the first major printed image of the Creature in an authorised book context.

    Mary Shelley and 1816

    The novel comes from a specific Romantic circle: Mary Godwin, Percy Shelley, Lord Byron, John Polidori, Geneva, bad weather and a challenge to write a ghost story.

    1797-1851

    Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley

    • Born Mary Wollstonecraft Godwin in 1797.
    • Daughter of Mary Wollstonecraft and William Godwin.
    • She began Frankenstein as a young writer in the circle of Percy Shelley and Byron, but the novel is not reducible to that circle.
    • Its structure, ethical pressure and narrative control are Shelley’s own achievement.

    Villa Diodati, 1816

    The ghost-story challenge

    • In 1816, Mary Godwin, Percy Shelley, Lord Byron, Claire Clairmont and John Polidori were near Geneva.
    • Bad weather kept the party indoors and encouraged reading and discussion of ghost stories.
    • Byron’s challenge to write a ghost story produced two important Gothic afterlives: Shelley’s Frankenstein and Polidori’s The Vampyre.

    Romantic science

    Galvanism, anatomy and life

    • The novel belongs to debates around life, electricity, chemistry, anatomy and the limits of experiment.
    • Galvani, Aldini, Erasmus Darwin and Humphry Davy all form part of the wider scientific context.
    • Shelley does not write a laboratory manual. She writes an ethical narrative about ambition, secrecy and abandonment.

    Structure and narrative method

    The novel works through nested testimony. Every major account is mediated, edited, remembered or addressed to someone else.

    Walton

    Arctic frame

    • Robert Walton writes to his sister, Margaret Saville.
    • His polar ambition mirrors Victor’s intellectual ambition.
    • Walton’s frame turns the novel into a warning passed from one aspiring man to another.

    Victor

    Confession and self-defence

    • Victor presents himself as warning, victim and witness.
    • His account is powerful but not neutral: he selects, judges and withholds.
    • Readers have to test his language against his actions, especially his failure to take responsibility after creation.

    The Creature

    Education and claim-making

    • The Creature learns language, history and morality by observing the De Lacey family and reading books.
    • His narrative is a demand to be recognised as feeling, reasoning and wronged.
    • The novel’s central ethical difficulty is that his suffering is real and his violence is also real.

    1818 and 1831

    The 1818 and 1831 texts are close enough to be recognisably the same novel, but different enough to change interpretation.

    1818 text

    Younger, sharper, less providential

    • The 1818 version is often read as politically sharper and less overtly moralised.
    • Victor appears more responsible for his own choices.
    • Family, education and social responsibility are more strongly felt as human structures rather than fate.

    1831 text

    Revised, framed and morally darkened

    • The 1831 text adds Shelley’s introduction and changes parts of the narrative emphasis.
    • Victor’s language of destiny and irresistible forces becomes more pronounced.
    • The revised edition helped fix the version many nineteenth- and twentieth-century readers knew.

    Editorial question

    Which Frankenstein?

    • A study page should say clearly which text an edition uses.
    • The full Astor commentary edition foregrounds comparison between 1818 and 1831.
    • The introduced Astor edition presents Shelley’s 1831 text with introduction.

    Stage and screen adaptations

    The afterlife of Frankenstein often changes the story more radically than readers expect: assistants appear, the Creature becomes mute, electricity dominates, and the name Frankenstein shifts towards the monster.

    1823

    Presumption; or, the Fate of Frankenstein

    • Richard Brinsley Peake’s play is the first recorded theatrical adaptation.
    • It opened at the English Opera House in London in July 1823.
    • Mary Shelley saw the production on 29 August 1823.
    • The famous stage line “It lives!” belongs to Peake’s play, not Shelley’s novel.

    1910

    Edison Studios: first screen adaptation

    • Directed by J. Searle Dawley for Edison Studios.
    • Charles Ogle played the Creature.
    • The film changes the novel’s ending and turns the Creature into a more explicitly psychological projection.
    • It is now public domain and preserved as an important early horror-film record.

    1910 image record

    Before Karloff

    • The 1910 Creature looks little like the later Universal monster.
    • The image is useful because it shows that Frankenstein iconography was unstable before 1931.
    • Adaptation history should not treat the bolt-necked monster as inevitable.

    1931 / 1935

    James Whale and Universal

    • James Whale’s 1931 film, starring Colin Clive and Boris Karloff, became the most influential screen version.
    • The film drew on stage adaptation as well as Shelley’s novel.
    • Bride of Frankenstein followed in 1935 and further shaped popular memory of the Creature.

    Key dates

    A concise timeline of publication, reception and adaptation.

    1797

    Mary Wollstonecraft Godwin born

    • Born in London on 30 August 1797.
    • Her parents were Mary Wollstonecraft and William Godwin.

    1816

    Geneva and the ghost-story challenge

    • The Villa Diodati circle helped generate the initial idea for Frankenstein.
    • The novel grows out of conversation, reading and nightmare, but becomes a carefully structured narrative of responsibility.

    1818

    Anonymous first edition

    • Published in three volumes.
    • Mary Shelley’s name did not appear on the title page.

    1823

    Second edition and stage success

    • The second English edition followed the success of Peake’s Presumption.
    • Mary Shelley was named on the title page.

    1831

    Revised edition

    • The Bentley edition added Shelley’s introduction and revised the text.
    • This became the most widely circulated nineteenth-century form of the novel.

    1851

    Mary Shelley dies

    • Shelley died in London on 1 February 1851.
    • Frankenstein had already moved beyond the book trade into theatre and popular culture.
