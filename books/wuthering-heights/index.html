Wuthering Heights, Emily Bronte

First published in 1847 under the pseudonym Ellis Bell, Wuthering Heights is Emily Bronte’s only novel and one of the most unsettling works of nineteenth-century English fiction.

When Mr Earnshaw brings the orphaned Heathcliff home to Wuthering Heights, the household is altered permanently. His bond with Catherine Earnshaw becomes the centre of a violent story of love, humiliation, revenge, inheritance and possession, reaching from one generation into the next.

This Astor Library Edition presents the complete novel with notes and focused study material on Bronte’s life, publication, narrative form, Gothic conventions, dialect, character, themes, key passages and interpretation.

Records worth knowing

Short factual records for reading, teaching and comparison.

  • Ellis Bell Emily Bronte published under a masculine pseudonym, matching Charlotte’s Currer Bell and Anne’s Acton Bell.
  • Newby edition Thomas Cautley Newby published Wuthering Heights with Agnes Grey in a three-volume set.
  • Only novel Emily Bronte died in December 1848, about a year after publication.
  • Frame narration Lockwood’s diary opens the book, but most of the past is narrated by Nelly Dean.
  • Second generation Young Cathy, Hareton and Linton Heathcliff are not an appendix. They are the novel’s test of whether repetition can be broken.
  • Dialect Joseph’s Yorkshire dialect was softened in Charlotte Bronte’s 1850 edition, one of the textual issues attached to the novel.

    • Publication and textual history

    The novel’s first life was complicated: pseudonyms, shared volumes, a small print run, hostile reviews and a posthumous edition shaped by Charlotte Bronte.

    1847

    First edition: Thomas Cautley Newby

    • Wuthering Heights appeared under the name Ellis Bell.
    • The first two volumes were Emily Bronte’s novel; the third volume was Anne Bronte’s Agnes Grey under Acton Bell.
    • The form matters because early readers encountered the sisters’ work inside a shared publishing arrangement, not as separate branded classics.
    • Recent auction reporting notes that original-cloth first editions are exceptionally scarce, with the 1847 printing estimated at about 250 copies.

    1850

    Charlotte Bronte’s edition

    • After Emily’s death, Charlotte edited a new edition of the novel.
    • She supplied a biographical notice and a preface which shaped later public understanding of Emily.
    • Charlotte also regularised parts of the text, including punctuation and some dialect forms.
    • For teaching, the difference between Emily’s 1847 text and Charlotte’s 1850 presentation is a useful route into authorship, family memory and editorial control.

    Reception

    Early shock

    • Some early reviewers found the novel powerful but morally disturbing.
    • The cruelty, domestic violence, revenge plot and refusal of neat moral reward made the book difficult for many Victorian reviewers to classify.
    • Its later reputation was built partly through the recovery of what early reviewers resisted: intensity, structure, strangeness and psychological force.

    Emily Bronte and Haworth

    The novel should not be reduced to biography, but Haworth, the parsonage, the moors and the Bronte household remain part of its historical record.

    1818-1848

    Emily Bronte

    • Emily was born at Thornton, near Bradford, and spent most of her life at Haworth Parsonage.
    • She published poetry before the novel, using the name Ellis Bell.
    • Her imaginative world was shaped by family writing, reading, moorland walking, household work and the siblings’ shared habit of making private fictional worlds.
    • She died at Haworth on 19 December 1848.

    Haworth

    The parsonage

    • The Bronte family moved to Haworth in 1820 after Patrick Bronte became perpetual curate.
    • The parsonage was the domestic and working space where Charlotte, Emily and Anne wrote their major novels.
    • The Bronte Parsonage Museum now preserves the house and collection associated with the family.

    Moorland record

    Top Withens and the problem of models

    • Top Withens has long been associated with the novel in literary tourism.
    • There is no secure one-to-one building model for Wuthering Heights or Thrushcross Grange.
    • The safer historical point is atmosphere and terrain: exposure, distance, weather, rough tracks and isolation.

    Setting

    Yorkshire, but not map fiction

    • The novel draws on northern speech, social difference and moorland weather.
    • It does not offer a simple guidebook map of Haworth.
    • The two houses function as moral, legal and emotional systems as much as places.

    Narrative design

    The book looks wild, but its structure is controlled: nested testimony, broken chronology, partial witnesses and repeated household scenes.

    Lockwood

    The outsider who begins wrongly

    • Lockwood rents Thrushcross Grange and misreads almost everything at Wuthering Heights.
    • His narration gives the reader distance, awkward comedy and a first encounter with household hostility.
    • The window scene introduces the possibility of haunting before the family history is explained.

    Nelly Dean

    Memory, witness and involvement

    • Nelly is not a neutral recording machine.
    • She witnesses, interprets, judges, withholds and sometimes acts inside the events she later narrates.
    • Her role makes the novel useful for teaching unreliable mediation without reducing her to a liar.

    Two generations

    Repetition and repair

    • The first generation produces humiliation, dispossession and revenge.
    • The second generation inherits damage through names, property and forced relationships.
    • Young Cathy and Hareton matter because their ending changes the emotional pattern without erasing the dead.

    Documents

    Books, inscriptions and letters

    • Catherine’s marginal writing, Isabella’s letter and Lockwood’s diary all make text part of the plot.
    • Written records are unstable: they survive, but they need readers who may misunderstand them.
    • The novel repeatedly asks who controls a story after the speaker is absent.

    Context and themes

    These are study routes, not slogans: property, class, gender, law, violence, religion, race, nature, childhood and names.

  • Inheritance Heathcliff’s revenge depends on acquiring houses, controlling marriages and waiting for legal transfer.
  • Class Heathcliff is brought into the Earnshaw household without a secure social identity, then repeatedly humiliated through rank and property.
  • Gender Catherine and Isabella are constrained by marriage, household control and bodily vulnerability.
  • Violence The novel includes domestic abuse, cruelty to children, cruelty to animals and emotional coercion.
  • Religion Joseph’s language gives the novel a harsh Calvinistic pressure, but the book does not settle into orthodox moral comfort.
  • Names Catherine, Linton, Earnshaw and Heathcliff circulate through generations; names become claims, repetitions and traps.

    • Key passages

    Useful anchors for close reading and revision.

    Chapter 3

    The window

    Lockwood’s nightmare and the hand at the window establish the novel’s grammar of return: the dead are not simply past, and domestic space is not secure.

    Chapter 9

    Catherine’s confession

    Catherine’s account of Edgar and Heathcliff is often misread as pure romance. It is also a record of class calculation, self-division and social fear.

    Chapter 10

    Heathcliff returns

    His return alters the legal and emotional balance of both houses. Revenge becomes patient, social and financial.

    Chapter 13

    Isabella’s letter

    Isabella’s account of marriage to Heathcliff is one of the novel’s clearest records of domestic horror and failed romantic projection.

    Chapters 29-34

    The late Heathcliff

    The final movement shifts from domination to exhaustion, haunting and withdrawal.

    Ending

    The graves

    The closing scene does not cancel the violence. It leaves the reader with weather, burial, local rumour and an uneasy form of quiet.

    Character records

    The main figures are organised by households, generations and legal pressure.

  • Heathcliff Foundling, outsider, servant, property-holder and revenger. His history is partly visible, partly withheld.
  • Catherine Earnshaw Child of Wuthering Heights who chooses Edgar Linton while declaring a deeper bond with Heathcliff.
  • Edgar Linton Polished, fragile and legally powerful; often underestimated because the novel’s violence is louder than his authority.
  • Isabella Linton Romanticises Heathcliff, then becomes one of the clearest witnesses against him.
  • Hindley Earnshaw Displaced son, violent master and ruined gambler whose decline opens the house to Heathcliff’s revenge.
  • Hareton Earnshaw Degraded heir whose recovery depends on education, affection and young Cathy’s change of conduct.
  • Cathy Linton Second-generation Catherine, caught between Thrushcross Grange and Wuthering Heights.
  • Nelly Dean Servant, narrator, moral commentator and participant in the plot.
  • Joseph Servant whose dialect and religious severity give the novel a local and doctrinal edge.

    • Chronological production and adaptation record

    Adaptations repeatedly decide what to do with the second generation, Nelly’s narration, Heathcliff’s race and the novel’s violence.

    1920

    A. V. Bramble silent film

    • The earliest known film version was made in Britain.
    • No complete print is known to survive in general circulation.
    • Its record matters because the novel entered screen culture before the 1939 Hollywood image fixed the public romance.

    1939

    William Wyler: Oberon and Olivier

    • Directed by William Wyler for Samuel Goldwyn Productions.
    • Merle Oberon played Catherine and Laurence Olivier played Heathcliff.
    • The film cuts the second generation and adapts roughly the first sixteen chapters, turning the novel towards doomed romance.
    • Gregg Toland won the Academy Award for black-and-white cinematography.

    1953 / 1962

    BBC television and Nigel Kneale

    • Nigel Kneale’s television script was produced by the BBC in 1953 and again in 1962.
    • The 1953 live broadcast starred Richard Todd and Yvonne Mitchell.
    • The 1962 version starred Claire Bloom and Keith Michell.

    1970

    Robert Fuest film

    • Timothy Dalton played Heathcliff and Anna Calder-Marshall played Cathy.
    • The film belongs to a cycle of later twentieth-century versions that keep returning to the first-generation romance.

    1978

    BBC serial

    • The BBC serial form allowed more room for the second generation than many cinema adaptations.
    • Ken Hutchison played Heathcliff and Kay Adshead played Catherine.

    1992

    Peter Kosminsky film

    • Ralph Fiennes played Heathcliff and Juliette Binoche played both Catherine Earnshaw and Cathy Linton.
    • The film explicitly markets itself through Emily Bronte’s name and restores more of the generational structure than the 1939 film.

    2009

    ITV two-part adaptation

    • Tom Hardy played Heathcliff and Charlotte Riley played Catherine.
    • The adaptation foregrounds obsessive attachment, revenge and generational damage for a television audience.

    2011

    Andrea Arnold film

    • Kaya Scodelario played Catherine and James Howson played Heathcliff.
    • Arnold’s film uses a rougher, more physical visual language and casts a Black actor as Heathcliff, making visible racial questions often softened in earlier versions.

    2022

    Emma Rice / National Theatre

    • Emma Rice’s adaptation played the National Theatre after work with Wise Children, Bristol Old Vic and York Theatre Royal.
    • The production uses ensemble theatre, music and movement to make the moor a speaking, collective presence.

    1951 / 1958 / later

    Opera, ballet and musical versions

    • Bernard Herrmann composed an opera between 1943 and 1951.
    • Carlisle Floyd’s opera premiered at Santa Fe Opera in 1958.
    • Later stage afterlives include musical theatre, ballet and touring theatre versions.

    Teaching pathways

    Routes into the novel for students, teachers and independent readers.

  • Form first Start with who is speaking: Lockwood, Nelly, Catherine’s writing, Isabella’s letter, reported speech.
  • Map the houses Track who controls Wuthering Heights and Thrushcross Grange at each stage.
  • Separate love from possession Treat Cathy and Heathcliff as a problem, not a model romance.
  • Read the second half Do not let the 1939 film define the novel. Young Cathy and Hareton are structural, not optional.
  • Use dialect carefully Joseph’s speech is part of class, region and religious satire; it also raises editorial and accessibility questions.
  • Adaptation comparison Ask what each version cuts: Nelly, Hareton, Linton, Joseph, violence, race, property, religion.
