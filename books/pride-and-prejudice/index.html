Astor Library edition

Pride and Prejudice, Jane Austen

Elizabeth Bennet trusts her own judgement. Mr Darcy appears proud. Mr Wickham appears charming. Jane Bennet appears secure in Mr Bingley's affection. Austen slowly tests every one of those readings through letters, proposals, family embarrassment, social rank and the financial conditions attached to marriage.

The material pressure keeps the comedy honest. The Bennet daughters have no secure inheritance at Longbourn, so Mrs Bennet's marriage campaign is ridiculous without being groundless. Every courtship is also a question of property, reputation, female choice and who has learned to read other people well.

At a glance

The first title, early publication and social pressure behind the comedy.

  • Anonymous publication The first edition was credited to the author of Sense and Sensibility, not to Jane Austen by name.
  • Earlier title The first version was called First Impressions, a title that still explains the book's main test of judgement.
  • Copyright sale Austen sold the copyright to Thomas Egerton for £110 rather than publishing on commission.
  • Three-volume form The 1813 first edition was issued in three volumes, the normal format for much expensive fiction of the period.
  • Fast success The first edition sold well enough for a second edition in the same year.
  • Entail plot Longbourn will pass to Mr Collins, making the Bennet daughters' marital futures a practical family problem.
  • Militia context The presence of officers at Meryton is not decorative. It drives Lydia's plot and Wickham's social access.
  • Free indirect style Austen often lets the narration sit close to Elizabeth's perspective, so the reader shares her mistakes before revising them.
  • Exam use For AQA 8702 in 2026, students need close analysis of an extract and secure knowledge of the novel as a whole.

    • Text and publication history

    The novel grew from an unpublished youthful manuscript into a revised Regency bestseller, marking a decisive stage in Austen's professional life.

    1775-1817

    Jane Austen

    • Austen was born at Steventon, Hampshire, and spent her later writing years at Chawton.
    • Her novels were published anonymously during her lifetime.
    • Pride and Prejudice was her second published novel, after Sense and Sensibility.

    1796-97

    First Impressions

    • Austen wrote an early version of the novel in her twenties.
    • The earlier title points to the speed with which characters read and misread one another.
    • No full manuscript of this early version survives, so its exact form has to be inferred from later evidence.

    1797

    Cadell refusal

    • Austen's father, George Austen, offered the manuscript to the London publisher Thomas Cadell.
    • Cadell declined without seeing the manuscript.
    • The rejection left the novel unpublished for more than a decade.

    1811-12

    Revision after Sense and Sensibility

    • The success of Sense and Sensibility made another Austen novel more commercially plausible.
    • Austen revised the old manuscript before publication.
    • The title became Pride and Prejudice, a phrase already familiar from moral and literary discourse, including Frances Burney's Cecilia.

    28 January 1813

    First publication

    • Thomas Egerton published the novel in London in three volumes.
    • The title page identifies the book as by the author of Sense and Sensibility.
    • The book appeared in a market where circulating libraries made expensive multi-volume novels more widely accessible than private purchase alone.

    1813

    Second edition

    • The first edition sold strongly enough for a second edition later in 1813.
    • This immediate reprint marks the novel as a commercial success in Austen's lifetime.
    • A third edition followed in 1817.

    AQA GCSE English Literature

    As checked in July 2026, Pride and Prejudice remains an AQA 8702 Paper 1 set text in the 19th-century novel section.

    Paper 1

    Extract and whole text

    Students answer one question on their chosen nineteenth-century novel. They write in detail about a printed extract, then connect it to the novel as a whole. For Pride and Prejudice, that means knowing how a short passage works and how it connects to character, theme, plot and Austen's methods.

    Assessment focus

    What students need to show

    A strong answer makes a clear argument about the task and pays close attention to Austen's language, structure and narrative method. Links to context should be confident but purposeful, sharpening the interpretation through questions of marriage, gender, class, property and reputation.

    Useful revision route

    Track judgement

    Follow Elizabeth's changing view of Darcy alongside the reader's changing view of Wickham. Then connect both patterns to the letters, proposals, Pemberley, Lydia's crisis and Lady Catherine's confrontation.

    Regency context: marriage, property and rank

    Austen sets courtship inside a practical system of income, inheritance, reputation and family alliance.

    Longbourn

    Entail and female insecurity

    Mr Bennet's estate is entailed away from his daughters. Because the Bennets have no son, Mr Collins will inherit Longbourn. That legal arrangement makes marriage economically urgent, even when Austen makes the marriage market comic.

    Income

    Marriage as material calculation

    Bingley's income makes him immediately desirable in the neighbourhood, while Darcy's wealth shapes how characters read him before they know him. Charlotte Lucas's marriage to Collins shows the calculation made by a woman with fewer romantic expectations and fewer financial choices.

    Meryton

    Militia, display and danger

    The officers bring glamour, uniform, movement and gossip into the Bennet sisters' world. Wickham uses Meryton's social openness to construct his attractive public story; Lydia's elopement then shows how quickly reputation can become a family crisis.

    Pemberley

    House as character evidence

    Elizabeth's visit to Pemberley alters her understanding of Darcy. The estate is more than wealth: it is a social record of taste, responsibility, management and reputation. The housekeeper's testimony matters because it comes from someone who has seen Darcy's conduct over time.

    Form, language and narrative method

    Austen's plot depends on conversation, withheld information, letters, reported speech and narration that often sits close to Elizabeth's mind.

    Free indirect style

    Staying close to Elizabeth

    • The narration often adopts Elizabeth's assumptions without fully separating itself from them.
    • This makes the reader's first reading of Darcy and Wickham unstable.
    • When Elizabeth revises her judgement, the reader has to revise with her.

    Letters

    Private text inside public plot

    • Darcy's letter changes the structure of the novel by forcing Elizabeth to reread past events.
    • Letters allow hidden motives, family news and crisis management to enter the narrative quickly.
    • Lydia's elopement is processed through written and reported information before it is socially resolved.

    Dialogue

    Speech as social test

    • Characters reveal judgement, vanity, intelligence and self-deception through conversation.
    • Mr Collins's language exposes dependence and self-importance.
    • Lady Catherine's speech turns rank into aggression, while Elizabeth's replies test the limits of deference.

    Irony

    Comic distance

    • The opening sentence is both a social generalisation and a joke about who actually wants whom.
    • Austen's irony rarely needs heavy authorial comment.
    • The style lets foolishness expose itself through rhythm, repetition and misplaced certainty.

    Characters

    The novel tests how its characters judge, speak, read and correct themselves.

  • Elizabeth Bennet Intelligent and witty, but too ready to trust her own quick readings of Darcy and Wickham.
  • Fitzwilliam Darcy Proud, socially guarded and initially ungenerous in manner, but capable of self-correction and practical action.
  • Jane Bennet Gentle and generous in judgement, which makes her vulnerable to being misread by Bingley and Darcy.
  • Charles Bingley Affectionate and open, but socially dependent on Darcy's judgement.
  • George Wickham Attractive public manners hiding debt, resentment and predatory conduct.
  • Mr Collins Clerical dependence and social absurdity, tied to the future inheritance of Longbourn.
  • Charlotte Lucas Practical intelligence without Elizabeth's romantic confidence or financial room for refusal.
  • Lydia Bennet Youth, vanity and poor supervision, turning flirtation into family damage.
  • Lady Catherine Aristocratic authority at its most intrusive, certain that rank should control marriage.

    • Timeline

    From First Impressions to the Peacock edition.

    1775

    Jane Austen born

    • Austen was born at Steventon Rectory in Hampshire.
    • Her family environment supported reading, amateur theatre, letters and early writing.

    1796-97

    Drafting First Impressions

    • The young Austen drafted the novel that later became Pride and Prejudice.
    • The title suggests a stronger early emphasis on immediate judgement.

    1797

    Cadell refusal

    • George Austen approached the publisher Thomas Cadell.
    • The manuscript was refused without review.

    1811

    Sense and Sensibility

    • Austen's first published novel appeared anonymously as by "A Lady".
    • Its success helped prepare the way for Pride and Prejudice.

    1813

    Pride and Prejudice

    • Published by Thomas Egerton in three volumes.
    • A second edition appeared later the same year.

    1814-16

    Later lifetime novels

    • Mansfield Park appeared in 1814.
    • Emma appeared in 1815, dated 1816 on the title page.

    1817

    Austen's death

    • Austen died in Winchester at the age of forty-one.
    • Northanger Abbey and Persuasion were published posthumously.

    1894

    The Peacock edition

    • George Allen published Hugh Thomson's illustrated Pride and Prejudice.
    • The gilt peacock binding became one of the most famous visual afterlives of the novel in book design.

    Editions and illustration

    Bindings, illustrations and cover design have repeatedly changed how readers first encounter the novel.

    Chawton

    Austen's writing house

    • Austen lived at Chawton from 1809.
    • The Chawton years are closely connected with revision, publication and the final shape of her major novels.
    • The house now anchors much of the public memory of Austen as a working novelist.

    1894

    Hugh Thomson's Peacock edition

    • Thomson's illustrated edition gave the novel one of its most recognisable decorated bindings.
    • The peacock design turns display, beauty and pride into an object on the book cover itself.
    • The edition is now a major collectible record in Austen book history.

    Modern covers

    From manners to romance branding

    • Later editions often shift emphasis between satire, romance, heritage costume, social comedy and school-study use.
    • Cover history matters because it records what publishers think readers are buying.
    • An exam edition and a gift edition can frame the same novel very differently.

    Stage and screen afterlives

    Adaptations tend to reveal which part of the novel a period wants most: courtship, comedy, class, family, costume, sexual tension or modern reinvention.

    1938

    Helen Jerome stage adaptation

    • Helen Jerome's stage version became one of the major twentieth-century theatre adaptations.
    • It helped fix the novel as a performable drawing-room comedy.
    • The adaptation also fed into later screen treatment.

    1940

    MGM film

    • Directed by Robert Z. Leonard.
    • Starring Greer Garson as Elizabeth Bennet and Laurence Olivier as Darcy.
    • The film reshapes Regency manners through Hollywood comedy and costume convention.

    1980

    BBC television serial

    • The BBC adaptation starred Elizabeth Garvie and David Rintoul.
    • The serial form allowed more space for family, dialogue and sequence than a feature film.
    • It belongs to the long BBC tradition of Austen television.

    1995

    BBC television serial

    • Adapted by Andrew Davies.
    • Starring Jennifer Ehle as Elizabeth Bennet and Colin Firth as Darcy.
    • The adaptation became one of the defining modern screen versions of Austen's novel.

    2001

    Bridget Jones's Diary

    • Helen Fielding's modern comic rewriting translates Austen's courtship pattern into late twentieth-century romantic comedy.
    • The Darcy name becomes part of the adaptation joke.
    • The afterlife shows how Austen's plot can survive major shifts in setting and social code.

    2004

    Bride and Prejudice

    • Directed by Gurinder Chadha.
    • The film moves Austen's marriage plot into a transnational musical and family-comedy form.
    • It makes global matchmaking, diaspora and cultural translation part of the adaptation record.

    2005

    Joe Wright film

    • Starring Keira Knightley as Elizabeth Bennet and Matthew Macfadyen as Darcy.
    • The film emphasises landscape, household movement, youthful feeling and visual intimacy.
    • It remains one of the most widely recognised twenty-first-century adaptations.

    2016

    Pride and Prejudice and Zombies

    • Based on Seth Grahame-Smith's mash-up novel.
    • The film turns Austen's social choreography into Gothic-comic action.
    • Its existence records how deeply adaptable the original plot has become.

    Key passages for study

    These passages usually carry the most weight in close analysis and exam work.

  • Opening sentence Establishes irony, marriage economics and social generalisation.
  • Meryton assembly First impressions of Darcy, Bingley and local social judgement.
  • Mr Collins proposes Marriage as property, self-interest and social absurdity.
  • Charlotte's decision Practical marriage and female economic reality.
  • Darcy's first proposal Love damaged by pride, insult and social calculation.
  • Darcy's letter A structural turning point: Elizabeth must reread Wickham, Darcy and herself.
  • Pemberley House, reputation and character evidence change Elizabeth's judgement.
  • Lydia's elopement Reputation, gender and family security become urgent.
  • Lady Catherine's visit Rank confronts individual choice.

    • Ways to study the novel

    Three clear approaches for close reading and revision.

    Judgement

    Track first impressions

    • List what Elizabeth believes about Darcy and Wickham at each stage.
    • Mark the evidence she uses and the evidence she ignores.
    • Use Darcy's letter and Pemberley as revision points.

    Context

    Marriage and money

    • Track every reference to income, inheritance and family advantage.
    • Compare Elizabeth, Charlotte, Lydia and Jane as different marriage plots.
    • Separate romantic feeling from economic risk without treating them as unrelated.

    Methods

    Narration, dialogue and irony

    • Analyse how Austen lets foolish characters expose themselves through speech.
    • Watch when narration sits near Elizabeth's viewpoint.
    • Use contrast between public politeness and private judgement.
