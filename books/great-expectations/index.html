Great Expectations, Charles Dickens

A poor orphan receives a fortune and learns the cost of becoming a gentleman. Pip’s life begins in the Kent marshes, where a terrifying encounter with an escaped convict leads, years later, to unexpected wealth, London education and a mistaken belief about his own future.

The novel combines social realism, mystery, Gothic atmosphere and the coming-of-age form. Its central questions are not simply whether Pip can rise, but what he loses when he tries to become someone other than Joe Gargery’s boy from the forge.

Great Expectations, introduced Astor Library edition

The complete 1861 text of Charles Dickens’s Great Expectations, with new and original introductory material from Astor Library.

Pip is an orphan growing up on the Kent marshes when a terrifying encounter with an escaped convict changes the course of his life. After visits to Miss Havisham’s decaying Satis House and the cold beauty of Estella, Pip begins to feel ashamed of his home, his work and the loyal Joe Gargery.

When an unknown benefactor gives Pip the chance to become a gentleman, he leaves the forge for London. But his ‘great expectations’ are built on a mistake, and the truth behind his fortune will force him to confront guilt, snobbery, loyalty, love and the real cost of ambition.

Records worth knowing

Brief factual points that help frame the novel before reading or teaching it.

  • First person This is Dickens’s second fully first-person novel after David Copperfield.
  • No serial illustrations The British All the Year Round serial carried no illustrations, unlike many earlier Dickens serials.
  • Harper’s Weekly The American serial publication included illustrations by John McLenan.
  • Kent and London The novel moves from marsh country and forge to the legal, financial and social spaces of London.
  • Convict money Pip’s gentility is funded not by Miss Havisham, but by Magwitch: class ascent rests on crime, empire and secrecy.
  • Changed ending The published ending is not the one Dickens first wrote. The altered conclusion remains one of the major textual questions around the novel.

    • Serial text and first edition

    Great Expectations first reached readers as weekly instalments. Its pace, cliff-hangers and withheld revelations all matter to the way the novel works.

    1861

    Chapman and Hall book edition

    • The novel was issued in book form in three volumes after serial publication.
    • The title page identifies Dickens as author and Chapman and Hall as publisher.
    • Modern editions usually need to explain both the serial text and later textual changes, especially the ending.

    1 Dec 1860 - 3 Aug 1861

    All the Year Round

    • The British serial ran in Dickens’s own weekly journal.
    • Dickens needed a strong serial after Charles Lever’s A Day’s Ride had not sustained circulation.
    • The novel’s form suits weekly reading: each stage releases partial information and withholds the identity of Pip’s benefactor.

    24 Nov 1860 - 5 Aug 1861

    Harper’s Weekly

    • The American serial began slightly earlier than the British run.
    • John McLenan illustrated the American serial.
    • This creates a useful contrast: British readers met the novel without images; American readers received a visual version from the start.

    Dickens, Kent and the working novel

    The novel is not autobiography, but it is close to Dickens’s own late-career world: Kent, public success, private strain, magazine management and intense work at Gad’s Hill.

    1875 image record

    Dickens and his characters

    • Buss’s unfinished painting is a later memorial image, not a publication image for Great Expectations.
    • It is useful because it shows Dickens’s characters as a crowded imaginative world around the author.
    • Pip, Miss Havisham and Magwitch belong to the late phase of that world: darker, more retrospective, more concerned with guilt and self-deception.

    1857 onward

    Gad’s Hill and late Dickens

    • Dickens moved into Gad’s Hill Place in 1857.
    • The Kent setting matters: marsh, river, road and Rochester associations feed directly into the atmosphere of Great Expectations.
    • The novel’s movement between rural Kent and London is not decorative; it structures Pip’s class shame and divided loyalties.

    1860-61

    Work under pressure

    • Dickens was writing as editor, proprietor and serial novelist.
    • The weekly schedule sharpened the novel’s suspense: Magwitch, Estella’s origin, Compeyson and Miss Havisham’s part in the plot are all delayed disclosures.
    • The form rewards re-reading because Pip’s assumptions are exposed as errors after the fact.

    Locations and social geography

    The novel’s spaces form a moral map: marsh, forge, Satis House, Jaggers’s office, Barnard’s Inn, Newgate, the river and the attempted escape route.

    Kent marshes

    Opening with graves, cold and terror

    • The first scene makes Pip read the world through fear: churchyard, graves, marsh, gibbet and convict.
    • Magwitch enters as a threat, but the scene later becomes the foundation of Pip’s moral education.
    • The marsh is both local landscape and psychological space: exposed, watched, haunted and difficult to leave behind.

    The forge

    Joe Gargery and honest work

    • Joe represents affection, labour and moral steadiness rather than social polish.
    • Pip’s shame at Joe is one of the novel’s most important ethical failures.
    • The forge is not sentimental simplicity: it is the place Pip must learn to value after mistaking gentility for worth.

    London

    Gentility, debt and legal power

    • London initially appears as the city where Pip can become a gentleman.
    • Jaggers, Wemmick, Barnard’s Inn and Newgate connect social ascent to law, money, crime and paperwork.
    • Pip’s education is financial as well as moral: he learns how quickly expectations turn into debt.

    Miss Havisham, Satis House and damaged education

    Miss Havisham does not simply haunt the novel. She trains Estella, misleads Pip and turns private injury into a system of harm.

    Satis House image record

    “Break their hearts”

    • The illustration fixes one of the novel’s major visual afterlives: Miss Havisham seated amid decay, Estella trained as weapon, Pip exposed to humiliation.
    • Satis House is a stopped clock, a theatre of injury and a class lesson.
    • Pip mistakes Miss Havisham’s attention for patronage, then mistakes Estella’s cruelty for refinement.

    Estella

    Adopted daughter, social instrument

    • Estella is raised to attract and punish men.
    • The novel refuses a simple romance plot: Pip loves an image formed by class desire and emotional deprivation.
    • Her later suffering complicates the question of responsibility without excusing the harm she causes.

    Miss Havisham

    Victim and agent of harm

    • Miss Havisham is damaged by betrayal, but she also damages others.
    • Her repentance comes late and cannot undo Pip’s self-deception or Estella’s training.
    • The fire scene turns her frozen bridal image into physical catastrophe.

    Magwitch, crime and the false gentleman

    The revelation of Pip’s benefactor overturns the social fantasy of the first half of the novel.

    Benefactor revealed

    Magwitch returns

    • The returned convict destroys Pip’s belief that Miss Havisham made him a gentleman for Estella.
    • Pip’s money comes from Magwitch’s labour after transportation, linking gentility to punishment, empire and hidden criminal history.
    • The scene is structured as reversal: the figure Pip feared as a child becomes the source of his expectations.

    New South Wales

    Transportation and colonial money

    • Magwitch is transported after conviction and makes money abroad.
    • The plot brings colonial wealth back into English class aspiration.
    • Dickens uses this money to expose how respectable identity can depend on invisible violence and exclusion.

    Jaggers and Wemmick

    Law, office and compartmentalised feeling

    • Jaggers manages secrets and converts crime into professional information.
    • Wemmick separates office life from domestic feeling at Walworth.
    • The novel repeatedly asks whether moral feeling can survive systems built on calculation.

    The two endings

    The ending is not a minor variant. It changes the pressure placed on Pip, Estella, regret and possible repair.

    Original ending

    Piccadilly and separation

    • In Dickens’s first ending, Pip meets Estella in London after her second marriage.
    • The scene is brief and restrained.
    • It gives Pip emotional recognition, but not romantic reunion.

    Revised ending

    Satis House and ambiguity

    • After Edward Bulwer-Lytton read the proofs, Dickens revised the ending.
    • The published ending brings Pip and Estella together at the ruined Satis House.
    • The final wording remains carefully uncertain: it gestures towards no further parting without stating a conventional marriage plot.

    1868 wording

    “No shadow of another parting”

    • Dickens later adjusted the final sentence again.
    • The wording has become central to debates about whether the ending is hopeful, evasive or deliberately unresolved.
    • Modern editions often discuss both endings because each gives a different shape to Pip’s education.

    Chronological record

    Dated points for reading, teaching and cross-referencing.

    1812

    Dickens born

    • Charles Dickens was born in Portsmouth on 7 February 1812.

    1824

    Debtors’ prison and blacking factory

    • Dickens’s father was imprisoned for debt when Dickens was a child.
    • Dickens worked at Warren’s Blacking Warehouse, an experience often linked to his later writing on shame, labour and childhood vulnerability.

    1857

    Gad’s Hill Place

    • Dickens moved into Gad’s Hill Place in Kent.
    • The Kent atmosphere of Great Expectations belongs to this late-career period.

    1860-61

    Serial publication

    • The novel appeared in All the Year Round across thirty-five weekly numbers.
    • Its last instalment appeared in August 1861.

    1870

    Dickens dies

    • Dickens died at Gad’s Hill on 9 June 1870.
    • Great Expectations remained his penultimate completed novel, followed by Our Mutual Friend.

    Stage and screen afterlives

    Adaptations often simplify the novel by reducing Orlick, compressing London and turning the ending into a clearer romance.

    1939

    Alec Guinness stage version

    • Guinness adapted the novel for stage before David Lean’s film.
    • Martita Hunt played Miss Havisham in the stage version and reprised the role in the film.
    • The stage version helped shape Lean’s compressed screen structure.

    1946

    David Lean film

    • Directed by David Lean.
    • Cast included John Mills as Pip, Valerie Hobson as Estella, Martita Hunt as Miss Havisham, Finlay Currie as Magwitch, Bernard Miles as Joe and Alec Guinness as Herbert Pocket.
    • The film won Academy Awards for black-and-white cinematography and art direction.
    • It remains one of the most influential Dickens films, especially in its graveyard opening and Satis House imagery.

    1998 / 2012 / 2023

    Later screen versions

    • Alfonso Cuarón’s 1998 film relocates the story to modern America and turns Pip into an artist.
    • Mike Newell’s 2012 film returns to period Dickens with Jeremy Irvine, Holliday Grainger, Helena Bonham Carter and Ralph Fiennes.
    • The 2023 television version, developed by Steven Knight, recasts the novel through darker crime and trauma emphases.
