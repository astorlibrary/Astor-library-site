William Shakespeare

Coriolanus

Caius Marcius can take Corioli but cannot ask Rome’s citizens for their voices. Renamed Coriolanus and urged towards office by Volumnia, he treats political custom as humiliation. Banishment turns his contempt into an attack upon the city, until the final appeal from his family makes public survival depend upon private feeling.

Tragedy

Coriolanus, William Shakespeare

Probably written around 1608, Coriolanus is Shakespeare’s political tragedy of pride, class conflict and a soldier unable to adapt to civilian power.

Rome is divided by hunger and resentment. The citizens accuse the patricians of hoarding grain, while Caius Marcius despises their demand for political influence. After he captures Corioli and returns a celebrated hero, Volumnia urges him to seek the consulship—but the office requires him to request the people’s votes and display a humility he cannot convincingly perform.

Provoked into denouncing popular government, Coriolanus is banished. He joins his former enemy Tullus Aufidius and leads a Volscian army against Rome, leaving Volumnia to make the appeal that may save the city at the cost of her son. How the book works

Three ways to see the structure before getting lost in the detail.

Body

Wounds become political evidence

Rome asks Coriolanus to show the scars won in its service. He wants wounds to prove worth without having to display or narrate them for the people.

Voice

Who speaks for Rome?

Citizens, tribunes, senators, generals and family members all claim a language of public necessity. The play does not give one group an uncontested voice.

Family

Volumnia made the soldier

Coriolanus’s public identity is bound to his mother’s idea of honour. Her final appeal succeeds because politics returns through the family that formed him.

A closer reading

Politics in this play is never abstract. It is heard in the crowd, displayed on the body and rehearsed within a family.

Body

Wounds become political evidence

Coriolanus earns honour through bodily risk, but the consulship requires him to display his wounds and ask for votes. What he regards as private proof of service must be turned into a public performance.

His refusal is both pride and a serious misunderstanding of civic power. Rome does not ask only what he has done; it asks whether military achievement can submit to forms of consent and speech.

Hunger

The first argument is about bread

The citizens enter because they believe the patricians have withheld grain. Menenius answers material grievance with the fable of the belly, translating unequal distribution into a story of natural cooperation.

The play never lets that translation settle the matter. Political language can explain, flatter, provoke or conceal. Tribunes, senators, citizens and soldiers all struggle to decide whose account of Rome will count as the city itself.

Volumnia

Public honour begins inside the family

Volumnia has trained her son to understand wounds, victory and reputation as the substance of identity. She can instruct him in political performance, but she cannot give him the flexibility required to sustain it.

Her final appeal saves Rome by activating the family claims his public role tried to suppress. The victory is devastatingly double: the mother is honoured by the city at the moment her persuasion sends her son towards death.

People to keep in view

The figures who carry the main pressures of the work.

  • Caius Marcius CoriolanusRoman soldier whose courage, contempt and inability to perform humility make him both hero and danger.
  • VolumniaHis mother, proud architect of his martial identity and the most powerful speaker in the final appeal.
  • MeneniusPatrician mediator whose fable of the belly tries to turn social hierarchy into a natural body.
  • Tullus AufidiusVolscian commander, rival and uneasy ally, drawn to Coriolanus even while preparing his destruction.
  • Sicinius and BrutusTribunes who defend plebeian political power and know how quickly public opinion can turn.
  • VirgiliaCoriolanus’s wife, whose quieter language of fear and affection differs sharply from Volumnia’s pride.
