William Shakespeare

Coriolanus

Caius Marcius can take Corioli but cannot ask Rome’s citizens for their voices. Renamed Coriolanus and urged towards office by Volumnia, he treats political custom as humiliation. Banishment turns his contempt into an attack upon the city, until the final appeal from his family makes public survival depend upon private feeling.

Tragedy

Coriolanus, William Shakespeare

Probably written around 1608, Coriolanus is Shakespeare’s political tragedy of pride, class conflict and a soldier unable to adapt to civilian power.

Rome is divided by hunger and resentment. The citizens accuse the patricians of hoarding grain, while Caius Marcius despises their demand for political influence. After he captures Corioli and returns a celebrated hero, Volumnia urges him to seek the consulship—but the office requires him to request the people’s votes and display a humility he cannot convincingly perform.

Provoked into denouncing popular government, Coriolanus is banished. He joins his former enemy Tullus Aufidius and leads a Volscian army against Rome, leaving Volumnia to make the appeal that may save the city at the cost of her son. How the book works

Three ways to see the structure before getting lost in the detail.

Body

Wounds become political evidence

Rome asks Coriolanus to show the scars won in its service. He wants wounds to prove worth without having to display or narrate them for the people.

Voice

Who speaks for Rome?

Citizens, tribunes, senators, generals and family members all claim a language of public necessity. The play does not give one group an uncontested voice.

Family

Volumnia made the soldier

Coriolanus’s public identity is bound to his mother’s idea of honour. Her final appeal succeeds because politics returns through the family that formed him.

A closer reading

Politics in this play is never abstract. It is heard in the crowd, displayed on the body and rehearsed within a family.

Body

Wounds become political evidence

Coriolanus earns honour through bodily risk, but the consulship requires him to display his wounds and ask for votes. What he regards as private proof of service must be turned into a public performance.

His refusal is both pride and a serious misunderstanding of civic power. Rome does not ask only what he has done; it asks whether military achievement can submit to forms of consent and speech.

Hunger

The first argument is about bread

The citizens enter because they believe the patricians have withheld grain. Menenius answers material grievance with the fable of the belly, translating unequal distribution into a story of natural cooperation.

The play never lets that translation settle the matter. Political language can explain, flatter, provoke or conceal. Tribunes, senators, citizens and soldiers all struggle to decide whose account of Rome will count as the city itself.

Volumnia

Public honour begins inside the family

Volumnia has trained her son to understand wounds, victory and reputation as the substance of identity. She can instruct him in political performance, but she cannot give him the flexibility required to sustain it.

Her final appeal saves Rome by activating the family claims his public role tried to suppress. The victory is devastatingly double: the mother is honoured by the city at the moment her persuasion sends her son towards death.

People to keep in view

The figures who carry the main pressures of the work.

  • Caius Marcius CoriolanusRoman soldier whose courage, contempt and inability to perform humility make him both hero and danger.
  • VolumniaHis mother, proud architect of his martial identity and the most powerful speaker in the final appeal.
  • MeneniusPatrician mediator whose fable of the belly tries to turn social hierarchy into a natural body.
  • Tullus AufidiusVolscian commander, rival and uneasy ally, drawn to Coriolanus even while preparing his destruction.
  • Sicinius and BrutusTribunes who defend plebeian political power and know how quickly public opinion can turn.
  • VirgiliaCoriolanus’s wife, whose quieter language of fear and affection differs sharply from Volumnia’s pride.

    • Questions worth carrying through the text

    Not answers to memorise, but problems the writing keeps alive.

  • DemocracyDoes the play distrust the crowd, the patricians, their political managers—or all three?
  • PerformanceWhy can Coriolanus act courage under fire but not humility in the marketplace?
  • TragedyIs the final catastrophe caused by an unchanging nature, by political institutions or by the roles others demand?

    • Six political tests

    Coriolanus repeatedly succeeds where force is enough and fails where citizenship requires listening, negotiation or restraint.

  • The grain protestThe opening citizens speak from hunger, not from abstract envy. Their argument begins with stores, prices and survival; Menenius answers by turning the state into a body whose parts should trust the belly.
  • The gates of CorioliMarcius enters the city almost alone and returns bloodied. The action establishes extraordinary courage, but it also confirms his dangerous preference for isolation over coordinated public life.
  • The new name‘Coriolanus’ records a conquest by attaching another city to his body. The honour is real, yet the name also keeps him permanently inside the military act that civilian office will require him to leave behind.
  • The gown of humilityCustom asks the candidate for consul to appear before citizens, request their voices and show his wounds. Coriolanus sees theatre and degradation; the citizens see a necessary acknowledgement of their political standing.
  • BanishmentHis answer—banishing Rome from himself—tries to make exclusion an act of command. The words are magnificent and futile. The city continues without him while he must find a new identity among former enemies.
  • Volumnia kneelsThe mother who trained him to value Roman honour now asks him to spare Rome. His surrender is humane, filial and politically fatal, which is why the scene refuses a simple division between victory and defeat.

    • What the play refuses to settle

    Its politics remain difficult because no class or institution is allowed a clean monopoly on wisdom.

    The citizens

    A crowd can reason and turn

    The citizens disagree, reconsider and respond to pressure. Their changes of mind are easy to mock, but patrician contempt and tribune strategy also help to produce the volatility they fear.

    The tribunes

    Representation includes calculation

    Sicinius and Brutus defend an office created to protect plebeian power. They also read crowds tactically and provoke Coriolanus towards the outburst that will destroy his candidacy. Principle and management occupy the same action.

    The hero

    Integrity can become incapacity

    Coriolanus hates saying what he does not feel. That refusal can look like moral consistency, yet public office depends upon forms he treats as beneath him. The tragedy asks when uncompromising truth becomes a refusal to live with other people.

    Aufidius and the rival self

    Coriolanus understands his enemy more readily than he understands his fellow citizens. The relationship gives military honour an intimacy that politics cannot safely contain.

    Recognition

    The enemy knows his value

    Aufidius can praise Coriolanus without asking him to soften, explain or request permission. Their rivalry rests on a shared military language in which courage is proved directly against another body.

    That recognition is deeply attractive after banishment. Among the Volscians, Coriolanus can imagine that an honoured enemy offers a truer home than a city of negotiation and changing voices.

    Alliance

    Admiration does not remove competition

    When Coriolanus arrives at Antium, the embrace is intense, but the alliance immediately unsettles rank. Volscian soldiers begin to prefer the Roman hero, and Aufidius discovers that welcoming greatness may reduce his own command.

    The play does not turn rivalry into simple friendship. Each man needs the other as a measure of martial worth, which means admiration always carries the possibility of replacement.

    The last scene

    Language prepares the killing

    Aufidius cannot defeat Coriolanus by meeting his public image of invulnerable courage. He attacks the role instead, presenting the decision to spare Rome as childish weakness and naming his rival a traitor.

    Coriolanus answers by returning to the capture of Corioli. The old deed and old name are all he can offer against the new charge. His attempt to inhabit military identity one final time helps make the murder possible.

    Four words that keep changing sides

    The political argument lives in repeated terms whose meaning depends on who is entitled to use them.

  • VoicesThe citizens give Coriolanus their votes—their ‘voices’—then withdraw them. He treats the word as noise and dependency; the constitution treats it as a share in public authority.
  • ServiceMilitary service gives Coriolanus his claim to office, but Rome also demands service in forms he despises: speaking, listening and accepting the judgement of people who did not fight beside him.
  • TraitorThe charge moves between factions and cities. It does not merely identify disloyalty; it reveals which community a speaker believes has the right to demand loyalty.
  • Name‘Coriolanus’ is reward, public memory and trap. Once his identity has been made from victory over Corioli, compromise can be made to look like the loss of the self that Rome created.
