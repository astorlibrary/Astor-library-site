Records worth knowing

Brief factual points that help frame the novel before reading, teaching or comparing adaptations.

  • Documentary form The novel is assembled from documents: journals, letters, telegrams, a ship log, newspaper cuttings, memoranda and phonograph diaries.
  • Professional witnesses Harker, Seward, Van Helsing and Mina bring legal, medical, academic and clerical habits of recording to the pursuit of Dracula.
  • Vampire sources Stoker did not invent the literary vampire. Polidori, Rymer and Le Fanu all sit behind the novel’s vampire tradition.
  • Vlad Dracula Stoker borrowed the name and fragments of historical material. The Count is not a biography of Vlad III.
  • Whitby The English coastal setting matters to the novel’s movement from foreign threat to domestic crisis.
  • Technology The novel uses late-Victorian systems of communication and storage: shorthand, typewriting, telegrams, trains, phonograph recording and medical case notes.

    Dracula belongs to the 1890s, but its materials reach back through vampire folklore, earlier Gothic fiction, travel writing and Stoker’s theatrical working life.

    May 1897

    First edition: Archibald Constable and Company

    • Dracula first appeared in London in 1897.
    • The novel was issued as a late-Victorian Gothic text, not as the beginning of a settled modern horror franchise.
    • Its form is epistolary and documentary rather than single-narrator confession.
    • The documents give the story a record-keeping surface: evidence is gathered, copied, sorted and compared.

    1847-1912

    Bram Stoker and the Lyceum Theatre

    • Stoker was born in Dublin and later worked in London.
    • His long association with Henry Irving and the Lyceum Theatre shaped his knowledge of performance, publicity, timing and theatrical effect.
    • Dracula draws on theatre indirectly through entrances, spectacle, concealment, role-playing and managed revelation.
    • The novel’s documentary structure is also practical: characters act like clerks, reporters, doctors, lawyers and archivists.

    1819-1872

    Earlier vampire fiction

    • John Polidori’s The Vampyre helped establish the aristocratic literary vampire in English prose.
    • Varney the Vampire carried vampire fiction into serial popular print.
    • J. Sheridan Le Fanu’s Carmilla gave Stoker an important earlier model for seduction, secrecy and vampire threat.
    • Stoker’s novel reorganises these materials into a larger pursuit narrative with multiple witnesses.

    Sources and folklore

    Vlad, Transylvania and vampire tradition

    • Stoker’s working notes show an interest in travel books, folklore, naming and regional detail.
    • The name Dracula connects the Count to historical Wallachian material, but the novel does not simply retell the life of Vlad III.
    • Transylvania gives the opening chapters distance, superstition, border-crossing and unfamiliar law.
    • The English sections test what happens when the same threat enters railway timetables, newspapers, hospitals and suburban households.

    Victorian context

    The novel is built from late-Victorian institutions and anxieties: work, class, gender, empire, religion, medicine, new recording technologies and older supernatural beliefs.

    Work and records

    Clerks, doctors, solicitors and typists

    • Jonathan Harker begins as a solicitor’s clerk on business abroad.
    • Mina’s typing, shorthand and organisation turn scattered documents into usable evidence.
    • Seward records his medical diary on a phonograph, making the asylum part of the novel’s documentary system.
    • The group’s strength comes from coordinated work as much as individual bravery.

    Medicine

    Blood, transfusion and the asylum

    • Lucy’s illness is handled through diagnosis, observation and repeated blood transfusions.
    • The novel predates modern blood typing, which makes the transfusion scenes historically specific.
    • Seward’s asylum creates a second Gothic space inside modern medical authority.
    • Renfield’s case notes show how professional language can both observe and misread.

    Gender and status

    Mina, Lucy and late-Victorian womanhood

    • Lucy is watched through desire, diagnosis and protection.
    • Mina combines sympathy, literacy, organisation and technical skill.
    • The novel repeatedly tests the boundary between admiration for women’s competence and anxiety about female independence.
    • Marriage, inheritance, reputation and bodily control all sit inside the vampire plot.

    Religion and ritual

    Modern evidence and older protections

    • The group uses modern records, but also older religious and folk protections.
    • Crucifixes, consecrated wafers, garlic, burial practice and threshold rules belong to the novel’s supernatural grammar.
    • Stoker does not remove religion from modernity. He places it inside the same evidence-gathering campaign.

    Characters and documentary method

    The main characters matter not only for plot, but for the kinds of record they produce and trust.

    Jonathan Harker

    Legal errand, captivity and witness

    • Harker begins with professional confidence and a traveller’s notebook.
    • Castle Dracula turns legal business into imprisonment.
    • His early diary gives the reader the first sustained evidence that ordinary categories are failing.

    Mina Harker

    Typist, editor and organiser

    • Mina gathers, copies and arranges the group’s documents.
    • Her work makes the hunt coherent.
    • She is repeatedly treated as someone to protect, yet the men depend on her intellectual and practical labour.

    Lucy Westenra

    Sleep, illness and transformation

    • Lucy’s plot moves through sleepwalking, medical care, transfusion and violation.
    • Her transformation into the “Bloofer Lady” changes illness into public threat.
    • The handling of Lucy’s body is one of the novel’s sharpest Gothic episodes.

    Van Helsing

    Professor, doctor and interpreter

    • Van Helsing brings medical knowledge, folklore and religious confidence together.
    • He is the first major figure to read the signs as vampirism rather than illness alone.
    • His authority depends on crossing boundaries between science and older belief.

    Count Dracula

    Aristocrat, invader and archive problem

    • Dracula is introduced through hospitality, property, law and language before open violence.
    • His movement to England turns the plot into a problem of documents, boxes, shipping, addresses and pursuit.
    • The Count is powerful partly because he is difficult to classify by modern institutions.

    Stage history and adaptations

    Dracula’s theatre afterlife changed the Count’s public image before sound cinema fixed it for a wider audience.

    1897

    Lyceum copyright reading

    • Stoker arranged a dramatic reading at the Lyceum Theatre shortly before publication to secure stage rights.
    • The reading was a practical legal and theatrical step rather than a full production history.
    • It shows how closely the novel’s first public life sits beside London theatre.

    1924

    Hamilton Deane at the Grand Theatre, Derby

    • Hamilton Deane’s authorised version opened at the Grand Theatre, Derby, in 1924.
    • The play reshaped the novel for touring theatre and drawing-room suspense.
    • Raymond Huntley played Dracula in the early production record.
    • The stage Count began to move towards evening dress, controlled entrances and recognisable theatrical glamour.

    1927-1928

    London and Broadway

    • Deane’s version reached London before John L. Balderston adapted it for Broadway.
    • The Broadway production opened at the Fulton Theatre in October 1927.
    • Bela Lugosi played Dracula on stage before playing him for Universal in 1931.
    • The stage version tightened the story around interiors, doors, visitors, doctors and confrontation.

    1977-1980

    Edward Gorey and the Broadway revival

    • The 1977 Broadway revival used Edward Gorey’s distinctive designs.
    • Frank Langella played Dracula and later returned to the role in the 1979 film.
    • The production made the Count visually elegant and highly stylised for a late twentieth-century audience.

    Screen versions

    Film versions usually simplify the documents and concentrate on Dracula’s body, voice, costume, castle, victims and pursuit.

    1922

    Nosferatu

    • F. W. Murnau’s silent German film was an unauthorised adaptation of Stoker’s novel.
    • Names and details were changed, but the connection to Dracula was clear enough to lead to legal action by Florence Stoker.
    • Max Schreck’s Count Orlok created a separate visual tradition: rat-like, plague-bearing and corpse-thin.

    1931

    Universal: English and Spanish-language films

    • Tod Browning’s English-language Dracula starred Bela Lugosi.
    • It drew heavily on the Deane and Balderston stage tradition.
    • A Spanish-language version directed by George Melford was shot in the same period on Universal sets.
    • The two films show how early sound cinema adapted theatre practice, studio space and vampire imagery.

    1958

    Hammer: Dracula / Horror of Dracula

    • Terence Fisher’s Hammer film starred Christopher Lee as Dracula and Peter Cushing as Van Helsing.
    • The film intensified colour, blood, physical threat and pace.
    • Hammer’s series built a separate British horror cycle around Lee’s Count.

    1979 / 1992 / later

    Romance, spectacle and return to Stoker

    • John Badham’s 1979 film starred Frank Langella and drew on his stage success.
    • Francis Ford Coppola’s 1992 Bram Stoker’s Dracula foregrounded romance, visual design and claims of fidelity to the novel.
    • Later adaptations continue to choose between Stoker’s documents, the stage Count and the cinematic vampire image.
