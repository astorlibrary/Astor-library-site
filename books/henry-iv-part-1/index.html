Henry IV, Part 1, William Shakespeare

King Henry IV is trying to hold a divided kingdom together. His son, Prince Hal, is wasting his reputation in the taverns of Eastcheap. Hotspur, the rebel warrior Henry wishes were his own heir, is preparing for war.

The play moves between court, tavern, road and battlefield. At Shrewsbury, private reputation and national crisis meet: Hal must face Hotspur, Henry must confront the instability of his rule, and Falstaff must test what honour is worth when bodies are dying around him.

The edition follows the whole play while giving its crowded political and theatrical history space to breathe. Notes on the quartos, early performance, Holinshed and Hall sit alongside close attention to Henry, Hal, Falstaff, Hotspur and the people drawn into their competing versions of loyalty.

Longer readings return to Hal’s soliloquy, Hotspur’s idea of honour, Falstaff’s catechism and the battle of Shrewsbury. Together they ask how legitimacy is performed, what fathers demand of sons and why tavern prose can cut through martial rhetoric.

Three worlds in collision

Court, tavern and battlefield give honour a different meaning before Shrewsbury brings them together.

  • Quarto title The 1598 quarto title foregrounds Shrewsbury, Hotspur and the “humorous conceits” of Sir John Falstaff.
  • Stationers’ Register Andrew Wise entered the play on 25 February 1598.
  • Print popularity The play was reprinted repeatedly before the First Folio.
  • Oldcastle trace Evidence suggests Falstaff was first connected with the name Oldcastle before pressure made the safer name Falstaff necessary.
  • Henriad position The play follows Richard II and leads into Henry IV, Part 2 and Henry V.
  • Genre mixture Court history, tavern comedy and battle narrative are not separate strands; each changes the meaning of the others.

    • Text and publication history

    The early printed record shows how strongly the play was associated with Hotspur, Shrewsbury and Falstaff from the start.

    1598

    First quarto

    • Printed by Peter Short for Andrew Wise.
    • The title names the battle at Shrewsbury, Hotspur and Sir John Falstaff.
    • A possible earlier quarto fragment also survives in the Folger Shakespeare Library.
    • The play’s printed popularity is unusual among Shakespeare’s works before 1623.

    1599-1622

    Repeated quartos

    • Further quarto editions appeared in 1599, 1604, 1608, 1613 and 1622 before the First Folio.
    • The 1599 quarto presents the play as newly corrected by Shakespeare.
    • This repeated printing record is evidence of strong commercial demand.

    1623

    First Folio

    • The Folio includes the play as The First Part of Henry the Fourth.
    • Folio publication places the play within the printed sequence of Shakespeare’s histories.
    • The Folio helps fix the play as part of a larger dynastic arc from Richard II to Henry V.

    Oldcastle / Falstaff

    A name with political risk

    • Several traces suggest the comic knight was first associated with Oldcastle.
    • The historical Sir John Oldcastle was a Protestant martyr with powerful descendants.
    • The change to Falstaff left behind jokes and textual oddities that still matter to editors.

    Sources and historical frame

    Shakespeare builds the play from chronicle history, but he reorganises that material around theatrical contrast: king, rebel, prince and tavern companion.

    Holinshed

    Chronicle history

    Raphael Holinshed’s Chronicles supplied major material for the English history plays. In Henry IV, Part 1, that material provides the political frame of rebellion, royal instability and Shrewsbury. Shakespeare changes the story's pressure by giving Hal a theatrical education beside Falstaff.

    Edward Hall

    Lancaster and York

    Hall’s chronicle tradition shaped Tudor accounts of dynastic conflict. Although the play is set before the Wars of the Roses, it already thinks about legitimacy and succession. Henry IV’s crown can never be politically neutral because he took it from Richard II.

    1403

    Shrewsbury

    The historical battle of Shrewsbury took place on 21 July 1403, when Henry IV defeated the Percy rebellion. Shakespeare turns the battle into a moral theatre in which Hal’s reputation can be publicly rewritten.

    Elizabethan pressure

    Succession and stability

    The play was written near the end of Elizabeth I’s reign, when succession anxiety mattered. Its questions about heirs, rebellion and public image would not have felt remote. Hal’s transformation is political theatre as well as personal reform.

    Court, tavern and battlefield

    The play works by cutting between spaces. Each space has its own language, rhythm and test of value.

    Court

    Legitimacy under strain

    Henry wants order, but the kingdom remembers how he gained the crown. The court scenes make political authority anxious rather than settled.

    Eastcheap

    Performance and delay

    Hal, Falstaff and Poins rehearse roles, lies and mock trials. Tavern comedy becomes a school of public performance.

    Rebel camp

    Honour and fracture

    Hotspur, Worcester, Northumberland and Glendower do not form a simple heroic opposition. Their coalition is energetic but unstable.

    Road

    Gadshill

    The robbery plot links comic deception to questions of report, reputation and evidence.

    Wales

    Language and myth

    Glendower brings prophecy, music and cultural difference into the play’s political geography.

    Shrewsbury

    War and exposure

    The battle tests every performance. Hal fights Hotspur; Falstaff stages death; Henry survives without resolving the larger disorder.

    Visual history

    Portraits and stage imagery helped turn Falstaff and Hotspur into enduring cultural types.

    Falstaff image tradition

    The comic body

    • Falstaff’s body became a major visual shorthand for appetite, wit and evasion.
    • Many later images flatten him into joviality.
    • The play itself is sharper: Falstaff is comic, exploitative, theatrical and morally evasive at once.

    Hotspur

    The rebel ideal

    • Hotspur is politically dangerous but theatrically magnetic.
    • Henry IV praises him because he seems to embody the martial value Hal lacks.
    • Shakespeare makes that admiration unstable by showing Hotspur’s impatience and strategic weakness.

    1403

    Shrewsbury

    • Shrewsbury is the public test toward which the play moves.
    • The battle gives Hal the chance to replace tavern disgrace with martial service.
    • The ending is a victory, not national peace. Rebellion continues into Henry IV, Part 2.

    Characters in the drama

    The major characters are arranged around competing versions of honour, inheritance and public identity.

  • King Henry IV A ruler who has the crown but not peace. His anxiety comes from rebellion and from the memory of Richard II.
  • Prince Hal A prince staging his own bad reputation so that reform will look more dramatic when it comes.
  • Sir John Falstaff Comic knight, tavern actor, liar and anti-hero. He exposes honour as language, costume and risk.
  • Hotspur Martial energy and impatience. He is heroic, reckless and politically exploitable.
  • Worcester Political calculation inside rebellion. His withholding of Henry’s offer helps produce catastrophe.
  • Glendower Welsh power, prophecy and cultural difference. He makes England’s politics feel geographically larger.

    • Language and interpretation

    The play’s shifts in language are part of its political design.

  • Prose and verse Eastcheap prose, court rhetoric and rebel verse create different social worlds.
  • Honour Hotspur treats honour as martial value; Falstaff treats it as a dangerous word that cannot heal wounds.
  • Role-play Hal and Falstaff rehearse kingship before Hal faces his father and the battlefield.
  • Fathers and sons Henry compares Hal with Hotspur, turning national conflict into family failure.
  • Reports and lies The play repeatedly asks who controls the story after an event.
  • History as theatre Hal’s public transformation works because he understands timing, spectators and expectation.

    • Stage and screen adaptations

    Later versions often decide the play by how they handle Falstaff: comic abundance, moral corruption, substitute father or political liability.

    1600

    Court performance record

    • The earliest recorded performance took place at court on 6 March 1600 before the Flemish ambassador.
    • Further court performances are recorded in 1612 and 1625.

    1965

    Orson Welles, Chimes at Midnight

    • Welles combined material from both Henry IV plays with parts of Henry V, Richard II and The Merry Wives of Windsor.
    • Welles played Falstaff; John Gielgud played King Henry IV; Keith Baxter played Hal; Norman Rodway played Hotspur.
    • The film makes the Falstaff-Hal relationship central.

    2012

    The Hollow Crown

    • Richard Eyre directed the BBC version for The Hollow Crown.
    • Jeremy Irons played Henry IV, Tom Hiddleston played Hal, Simon Russell Beale played Falstaff and Joe Armstrong played Hotspur.
    • The screen version places the play within a continuous filmed Henriad.

    2024

    Player Kings

    • Robert Icke adapted and directed a version combining the two Henry IV plays.
    • Ian McKellen played Falstaff, with Toheeb Jimoh as Hal and Richard Coyle as Henry IV.
    • The production showed how strongly modern versions still organise the plays around Falstaff’s pull on Hal.
