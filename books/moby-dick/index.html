Complete commentary edition

Moby-Dick; or, The Whale, Herman Melville

First published in 1851, Melville’s novel recounts the final voyage of the whaling ship Pequod, narrated by Ishmael. What begins as a commercial whaling expedition becomes Ahab’s private hunt for the white whale that maimed him.

The commentary edition travels with the complete novel, chapter by chapter, without pretending that all of its detours need to be made tidy. Its account of the 1851 British and American editions sits beside the history of whaling, global trade and maritime labour that fills the Pequod with more than Ahab’s private obsession.

Notes on biblical, Shakespearean and classical allusion lead into close readings of Father Mapple’s sermon, the Quarter-Deck, whiteness, the doubloon and the final chase. Ishmael, Ahab, Queequeg, Starbuck and Pip are followed across those changing forms, with a glossary and bibliography available when the novel sends a reader elsewhere.

At a glance

Essential context for reading and discussion.

  • British first Richard Bentley issued the novel in London in October 1851 as The Whale, in three volumes.
  • American title Harper & Brothers issued Moby-Dick; or, The Whale in New York in November 1851.
  • Epilogue problem The British first edition omitted the epilogue, leaving early reviewers with a first-person narrator who seemed to have died with the ship.
  • Front matter The American edition places “Etymology” and “Extracts” before the narrative; the British edition misplaced them at the end of volume three.
  • Essex source The whaleship Essex was struck by a sperm whale in the Pacific in 1820; Owen Chase’s account became central source material.
  • Mocha Dick J. N. Reynolds’s 1839 article on a white sperm whale called Mocha Dick supplied a named public precedent for Melville’s whale.

    • Text and publication

    The 1851 publication record matters because the first readers did not all meet the same book.

    November 1851

    Harper & Brothers: Moby-Dick; or, The Whale

    • The American edition appeared after the London edition but preserves the title now used for the book.
    • It includes the epilogue, which identifies Ishmael as the survivor of the Pequod’s destruction.
    • Its one-volume format differs from Bentley’s three-volume British issue.
    • The title’s hyphen matters: modern usage generally treats Moby-Dick as the book and Moby Dick as the whale.

    October 1851

    Richard Bentley: The Whale

    • Bentley published the British edition first, under the title The Whale.
    • The text was copy-edited for British publication and expurgated in many places.
    • The omission of the epilogue created a formal problem for early readers: Ishmael narrates a wreck he appears not to survive.
    • The British edition records the novel’s transatlantic publication pressures, including copyright and piracy concerns.

    1850-51

    Arrowhead and composition

    • Melville worked on the novel while living at Arrowhead in Pittsfield, Massachusetts.
    • His materials include maritime experience, whaling print sources, Shakespeare, the Bible, natural history and travel writing.
    • The finished book keeps its sources visible by mixing documents, quotations, speeches, lists, explanations and reported experience.

    Whaling history and literary sources

    The novel is built from real maritime labour and print sources, then pushed beyond documentary realism by Ahab’s pursuit.

    1819-1891

    Melville and sea writing

    Melville sailed on the whaleship Acushnet in 1841. Earlier books including Typee, Omoo, Redburn and White-Jacket had already drawn on travel, labour and maritime experience. Moby-Dick enlarges that material into a book about knowledge, obsession and record-making.

    1841

    A surviving whaleship

    The Charles W. Morgan gives a concrete visual record of the shipboard world close to Melville’s era. Whaling was industrial work at sea, encompassing pursuit, killing, cutting-in, boiling, stowing and sale. The Pequod is fictional, but the labour system behind it was historical and global.

    Whaling image record

    Boats, lines and impact

    Whales were pursued from small boats with lines, harpoons and lances. Melville repeatedly returns to the bodily danger of this labour, before the final chase turns working practice into catastrophe.

    1820 / 1839

    The Essex and Mocha Dick

    The Essex supplied the record of a sperm whale destroying a whaleship, while Reynolds’s “Mocha Dick” supplied a named white whale already famous in maritime print. Melville uses both without simply turning the novel into documentary reconstruction.

    Pittsfield

    Arrowhead

    • Melville wrote much of Moby-Dick while living at Arrowhead.
    • The inland writing place sits at a distance from the sea-world the novel reconstructs.

    Animal record

    Sperm whale

    • The hunted animal is also a physical fact: large, deep-diving and powerful.
    • Melville’s whale is never only symbolic; the ship is destroyed by impact.

    Classification

    Cetology and error

    • Ishmael’s whale classification is learned, comic and unstable.
    • The novel repeatedly tests whether knowledge systems can contain the animal.

    1930

    Rockwell Kent

    • Kent’s black-and-white illustrations became closely associated with the novel’s twentieth-century recovery.
    • His stark line work shaped how many readers pictured the book before film versions took over that function.

    Passages that carry the book

    Useful anchors for study, quotation and close reading.

  • Chapter 9 Father Mapple’s sermon turns Jonah into a pattern for flight, punishment and obedience before the Pequod sails.
  • Chapter 36 The Quarter-Deck speech changes a whaling voyage into Ahab’s private revenge.
  • Chapter 42 “The Whiteness of the Whale” refuses one fixed meaning for whiteness and makes interpretation unstable.
  • Chapter 99 The doubloon becomes a test object: each viewer reads the same coin differently.
  • Chapter 96 “The Try-Works” fuses industrial fire, night labour and moral disorientation.
  • Chapters 133-135 The final chase turns pursuit into wreckage and leaves Ishmael as witness.

    • Characters

    The central figures are also forms of knowledge, labour and authority.

  • Ishmael Narrator, survivor and collector of forms: sailor, reader, comic observer and philosophical witness.
  • Ahab Captain of the Pequod. His wound becomes a metaphysical grievance forced onto the whole crew.
  • Queequeg Harpooner from Kokovoko. Ishmael first misreads him, then discovers skill, loyalty and fellowship.
  • Starbuck First mate, Quaker and moral counterweight. He sees Ahab’s error but cannot stop the voyage.
  • Pip Black cabin boy whose abandonment at sea changes his relation to Ahab and to the novel’s language of sanity.
  • Fedallah Ahab’s hidden harpooner and prophetic double, attached to the fatal logic of the chase.

    • Stage and screen adaptations

    Adaptations usually simplify Ishmael’s documentary range and place Ahab, the ship and the whale at the centre.

    1926

    The Sea Beast

    • Warner Bros. silent film starring John Barrymore.
    • A loose adaptation that turns Melville’s structure towards romance and melodrama.

    1930

    Moby Dick

    • Another Warner Bros. version starring John Barrymore.
    • The film belongs to the early sound period and keeps the Ahab-centred screen tradition alive.

    1955

    Orson Welles, Moby Dick - Rehearsed

    • Welles’s stage version opened at the Duke of York’s Theatre, London.
    • A company of actors rehearses Shakespeare before transforming into Melville’s whaling world.

    1956

    John Huston’s Moby Dick

    • Directed by John Huston and written by Huston with Ray Bradbury.
    • Gregory Peck played Ahab, Richard Basehart played Ishmael and Leo Genn played Starbuck.

    2010

    Jake Heggie’s opera

    • Moby-Dick, by Jake Heggie with libretto by Gene Scheer, premiered at Dallas Opera on 30 April 2010.
    • The opera makes the ship, chorus and Ahab’s command central to the adaptation.

    Ways into the text

    Follow the novel through form, labour, authority, race and the meanings imposed on the whale.

  • Edition history Compare the British and American first editions, especially title, epilogue and front matter.
  • Form Track how chapters move between story, sermon, drama, taxonomy, encyclopaedia and stage speech.
  • Labour Read the whaling chapters with image records of whaleboats, try-works and sperm-whale anatomy.
  • Authority Treat Ahab’s Quarter-Deck speech as command, performance and coercion.
  • Race and fellowship Map the Pequod’s crew while keeping the book’s stereotyping and violence visible.
  • Nature and meaning Use the whiteness chapter to test the difference between symbol and physical animal.
