William Shakespeare

Antony and Cleopatra

Antony is divided between Roman command and Cleopatra’s Alexandria, but the play refuses to make either world simple. News, messengers and shifting alliances cross the Mediterranean while the lovers continually perform versions of themselves. Their political defeat becomes a struggle over how their story will be told.

Tragedy

Antony and Cleopatra, William Shakespeare

Probably written in 1606–07, Antony and Cleopatra is Shakespeare’s epic tragedy of love, political power and imperial conflict.

Fourteen years after Julius Caesar’s assassination, Rome is ruled by Mark Antony, Octavius Caesar and Lepidus. While Antony remains at Cleopatra’s court, his Roman allies accuse him of surrendering discipline to pleasure. Crisis forces him back to Rome, where an uneasy settlement and marriage to Caesar’s sister temporarily restore order. Antony nevertheless remains divided between Roman duty and the Egyptian queen whose influence neither distance nor calculation can contain.

How the book works

Three ways to see the structure before getting lost in the detail.

Geography

The play keeps moving

Short scenes and arriving messengers make political space feel unstable. Rome talks about Egypt; Egypt receives Roman news; the lovers rarely control the whole field.

Performance

Everyone manages an image

Cleopatra stages moods, Antony stages command and Caesar carefully prepares public victory. Political power depends upon who controls the scene and the report.

Ending

Defeat becomes theatre

Cleopatra imagines the Roman triumph that would display her, then arranges a death Caesar cannot fully possess. The final monument is both love story and political claim.

People to keep in view

The figures who carry the main pressures of the work.

  • Mark AntonyTriumvir and soldier, pulled between several ideas of Roman honour and his life with Cleopatra.
  • CleopatraQueen of Egypt, ruler, lover and commanding performer of her own identity.
  • Octavius CaesarAntony’s rival, patient and controlled, turning political survival into imperial authority.
  • EnobarbusAntony’s follower and commentator, whose famous descriptions do not save him from a crisis of loyalty.
  • OctaviaCaesar’s sister, married to Antony as a political settlement and made to carry the cost of its failure.
  • CharmianCleopatra’s attendant, sharing the queen’s private wit and her final public act.
