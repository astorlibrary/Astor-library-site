The Merry Wives of Windsor, William Shakespeare

Falstaff plans to improve his fortunes by seducing Mistress Ford and Mistress Page. The wives discover he has sent them identical letters and answer with punishments: the buck-basket, the disguise as the fat woman of Brentford, and the final trick at Herne’s Oak.

Around this run Ford’s jealousy and Anne Page’s fight to choose her own husband. The play turns English domestic space into theatrical machinery: letters, doors, servants, laundry, disguises, marriage contracts and neighbourhood gossip all become comic instruments.

At a glance

Essential context for reading and discussion.

  • 1602 quarto The first printed text is short, rough and often treated as a bad quarto.
  • 1623 Folio The First Folio gives a much fuller version and is the usual basis for modern editions.
  • Stationers’ Register John Busby entered the play on 18 January 1602.
  • Falstaff after Henry IV The play reuses Falstaff, Pistol, Nym, Bardolph and Shallow outside the political world of the history plays.
  • Garter tradition Some scholars connect the play with a possible 1597 Garter occasion, partly because of the fairy pageant and Windsor setting.
  • Only modern-English comedy Unlike the Italian or ancient settings of many comedies, this one stays in contemporary Windsor.

    • Text and publication history

    The textual record is unusually important because the quarto and Folio versions are sharply different in length and quality.

    1602

    First quarto

    • Printed by Thomas Creede for Arthur Johnson.
    • The title foregrounds Sir John Falstaff, the merry wives, Sir Hugh Evans, Shallow, Slender, Pistol and Nym.
    • The text is much shorter than the Folio and has long been discussed as a “bad quarto”.
    • Its title page says the play had been acted by the Lord Chamberlain’s Men before the Queen and elsewhere.

    1619

    Second quarto and False Folio

    • A second quarto appeared in 1619 as part of William Jaggard’s Pavier / False Folio group.
    • It reprints the quarto tradition rather than giving the fuller Folio text.
    • The episode matters because it shows Shakespeare’s plays being gathered and marketed before the official Folio.

    1623

    First Folio text

    • The First Folio supplies the longer and more coherent version used by most modern editions.
    • It expands the comic network and gives the Windsor community a fuller shape.
    • Editors must decide how to handle differences between the quarto and Folio traditions.

    Bad quarto debate

    Memory, performance and print

    • The 1602 quarto became one of the key examples in Alfred W. Pollard’s bad-quarto theory.
    • W. W. Greg treated it as a text possibly reconstructed from memory.
    • The category is debated, but the quarto remains central to how scholars think about theatrical publication.

    Windsor and local England

    The play’s setting matters. This is not Rome, Venice, Illyria or Athens. It is an English town of households, neighbours, servants, social ambition and public embarrassment.

    Domestic space

    Houses as comic machinery

    • Ford’s house becomes a trap, a stage and an imagined crime scene.
    • The buck-basket depends on servants, laundry and household routine.
    • The comedy is practical: doors, rooms, baskets, errands and disguises do the work.

    The wives

    Collaboration and control

    • Mistress Ford and Mistress Page are not passive targets.
    • They recognise Falstaff’s duplicated letters and turn the seduction plot into a punishment plot.
    • Their alliance drives the play’s strongest comic structure.

    Anne Page

    Marriage choice

    • Anne is pulled between her parents’ different choices: Slender and Caius.
    • She chooses Fenton instead.
    • The subplot connects domestic comedy with control over women’s marriage and property.

    Language

    Local accents and social friction

    • Evans and Caius bring comic accent, quarrel and professional rivalry.
    • Shakespeare uses linguistic difference to build neighbourhood texture.
    • The joke is social as well as verbal: Windsor is full of people watching, correcting and misreading one another.

    The three punishments of Falstaff

    Falstaff is not defeated once. The wives make the punishment serial, theatrical and communal.

    First

    The buck-basket

    Falstaff hides in dirty laundry and is carried away before being dumped into the Thames. The joke turns erotic plotting into household waste.

    Second

    The fat woman of Brentford

    Falstaff escapes in female disguise and is beaten by Ford, who believes the disguised woman to be a witch-like figure he hates.

    Third

    Herne’s Oak

    Falstaff is lured into a folklore scene, dressed with horns, pinched by children disguised as fairies, and exposed before Windsor.

    Visual history

    Quarto pages, portraits and Windsor landmarks reveal the play’s most durable visual traditions.

    Falstaff image tradition

    Falstaff outside history

    • In the Henry IV plays, Falstaff is tied to Hal and national history.
    • In Windsor, he becomes an ageing comic intruder into married household space.
    • Images often make him jovial; the play makes him greedy, exposed and repeatedly outplanned.

    Windsor

    A named English town

    • The Windsor setting links the play to English locality and royal association.
    • The comedy itself is not courtly; it belongs to households, streets, inns and local gossip.
    • This is one reason the play is often described as Shakespeare’s most English comedy.

    Herne’s Oak

    Folklore as social theatre

    • The final scene uses local folklore to organise a public punishment.
    • Children dressed as fairies become agents of communal correction.
    • Falstaff’s horns make sexual boasting into visible ridicule.

    Characters in the drama

    The play is a community comedy. Character is often defined by who knows what, who overhears what, and who is allowed to manage the plot.

  • Sir John Falstaff A broke, ageing opportunist trying to turn lust into income. He underestimates the wives and misreads Windsor.
  • Mistress Ford One of Falstaff’s targets and one of the play’s chief plotters. Her intelligence exposes both Falstaff and Ford.
  • Mistress Page Co-plotter and social manager. She helps turn private insult into controlled public comedy.
  • Ford Jealous husband who creates a disguise, becomes Brook, and humiliates himself through suspicion.
  • Anne Page Marriage-choice figure. Her decision against her parents’ plans gives the comedy its final domestic settlement.
  • Quickly Messenger and go-between whose language and promises keep competing plots moving.

    • Language and interpretation

    The play’s comedy is unusually verbal and practical at once: letters, accents, reports, disguises and household objects all carry meaning.

  • Prose comedy Much of the play works through fast social prose rather than high poetic rhetoric.
  • Identical letters Falstaff’s laziness exposes him immediately because the wives compare evidence.
  • Jealous interpretation Ford sees signs of adultery because he is already committed to suspicion.
  • Disguise Disguise is not romantic escape. It is practical, humiliating and often bodily.
  • Folklore Herne’s Oak turns local legend into theatrical discipline.
  • Domestic authority The wives control the comic rhythm more successfully than the men who try to direct them.

    • Stage, music and screen

    Falstaff’s punishments, Ford’s jealousy and Herne’s Oak have made the play attractive to stage comedy, opera and television.

    1604

    Whitehall performance

    • The earliest definitely dated performance is recorded at Whitehall Palace on 4 November 1604.
    • The 1602 quarto title page already claims performance before the Queen and elsewhere.

    1849

    Otto Nicolai, Die lustigen Weiber von Windsor

    • Nicolai’s German opera helped carry the play into nineteenth-century musical afterlife.
    • It focuses strongly on the comic energy of the wives, Falstaff and Windsor plotting.

    1874

    Gaiety Theatre and Arthur Sullivan

    • John Hollingshead staged the play at the Gaiety Theatre with Samuel Phelps as Falstaff.
    • Arthur Sullivan composed incidental music for the production.
    • A. C. Swinburne supplied words for an additional song for Anne Page.

    1982

    BBC Television Shakespeare

    • David Jones directed the BBC version, first transmitted in the UK on 28 December 1982.
    • Richard Griffiths played Falstaff, Prunella Scales played Mistress Page and Judy Davis played Mistress Ford.
    • The production built its studio Windsor from Tudor-house references connected with Shakespeare’s family and Stratford.
