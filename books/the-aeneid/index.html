The Aeneid, Virgil

Aeneas flees the fall of Troy and leads the surviving Trojans towards Italy. His journey passes through storm, Carthage, Dido’s tragic love, funeral games, the Sibyl’s cave, the underworld and the war against Turnus.

This edition uses John Dryden’s 1697 translation and gives its language room to move, with explanation where the Roman world needs opening up. The accompanying notes keep pietas, furor, fate, exile and empire tied to the people who bear their cost, from Creusa and Dido to Pallas and Turnus. The fall of Troy, Dido’s curse, the underworld, Aeneas’s shield and the final killing can then be read as parts of one unsettled foundation story.

At a glance

The poem, its Roman setting and Dryden’s English translation.

  • Unfinished poem Ancient tradition says Virgil wanted the poem destroyed because it had not received final revision. Augustus ordered publication.
  • Epic architecture Books 1-6 echo wandering and return narratives; Books 7-12 turn to Italian war.
  • Homeric pressure The poem works after the Iliad and Odyssey, but it makes Trojan defeat the beginning of Roman destiny.
  • Augustan frame The poem was written under Augustus, after decades of Roman civil war.
  • Dido matters Carthage is not a side episode. Dido’s story gives Rome’s future enemy a tragic human history.
  • Dryden’s version Dryden’s Works of Virgil appeared in 1697 and helped make Virgil central to English literary education.

    • Virgil, Augustus and Roman memory

    The poem gives Rome an origin story shadowed by the suffering through which myth is converted into history.

    70-19 BCE

    Virgil

    Publius Vergilius Maro was born in 70 BCE and died in 19 BCE. Before the Aeneid, he wrote the Eclogues and Georgics. He composed this poem while Augustus was consolidating power after civil war, giving Rome a Trojan genealogy that connects Roman history with Greek epic tradition.

    Augustan Rome

    Empire after civil war

    The poem’s public future is Augustan: Rome, law, rule and imperial order. Its immediate story is anything but orderly, moving through storm, shipwreck, desertion, funeral, prophecy and war. That tension is one reason the poem can be read both as a founding epic and as a study of the violence needed to found.

    Roman ancestry

    Aeneas, Ascanius and the Julian line

    Aeneas carries Troy into Italy. His son Ascanius, also called Iulus, links Trojan survival to later Roman foundation myth, and the poem connects that mythic line to the Julian family and the political present of Augustus.

    Book-by-book structure

    The twelve-book structure moves from survival towards settlement, then from settlement into violence.

    Book 1

    Storm and Carthage

    Juno’s hostility drives the Trojans into storm and shipwreck. Aeneas reaches Dido’s Carthage.

    Book 2

    The fall of Troy

    Aeneas recounts the wooden horse, the burning city, Creusa’s loss and the escape with Anchises and Ascanius.

    Book 3

    Wandering

    The Trojans search for a destination through failed settlements, omens and inherited prophecies.

    Book 4

    Dido

    Love, political duty and divine command collide. Aeneas leaves Carthage; Dido dies and curses his descendants.

    Book 5

    Games and mourning

    Funeral games for Anchises turn grief into communal ritual before the journey resumes.

    Book 6

    The underworld

    Aeneas enters the underworld, meets Anchises and sees the future Roman story he must serve.

    Books 7-8

    Italy and alliance

    Latinus, Lavinia and Turnus bring the Trojans into Italian politics. Evander and the shield of Aeneas widen the poem’s Roman frame.

    Books 9-10

    War expands

    Nisus and Euryalus die; Pallas dies; Aeneas’s anger grows harder to separate from his duty.

    Books 11-12

    Camilla and Turnus

    Camilla’s death and the final duel narrow the war to Aeneas and Turnus. The poem ends with Turnus killed, not with a settled peace.

    The epic’s divided loyalties

    The poem announces Rome’s future, but it rarely lets promised greatness erase the people and places lost on the way.

    Fate

    Knowing the destination does not simplify the road

    Readers know that Aeneas will reach Italy and that Rome will rise from his line. Prophecy supplies scale, but it does not tell characters how to endure the immediate cost of fulfilling it.

    Jupiter’s long view can make suffering look like a stage in history. At ground level, storms, deaths, broken alliances and abandoned homes remain fully felt.

    Pietas

    Duty pulls in several directions

    Aeneas must answer gods, family, followers, the dead and a future people who do not yet exist. These duties do not always agree. Leaving Carthage serves one command while devastating Dido; entering war in Italy creates the settlement destiny promised.

    The poem makes duty difficult precisely because Aeneas is not choosing between simple selfishness and simple virtue.

    Memory

    Troy is carried and rebuilt

    The survivors save household gods, tell stories and reproduce names from the lost city. Memory protects identity, but it can also turn every new landscape into a measure of what has gone.

    Italy cannot become a home by returning to the past. The final people will emerge from Trojans and Latins, and even the victorious name is subject to negotiation.

    Six scenes that change the poem

    These moments place the public story of foundation beside private grief, uncertainty and violence.

  • Aeneas carries AnchisesAs Troy burns, the family leaves in a chain: the father carried, the son led by the hand and Creusa following behind. The image gathers past and future, while Creusa’s loss shows that even exemplary escape is incomplete.
  • Dido receives the TrojansCarthage first appears as a city being built, a possible counterpart to Rome. Dido understands exile and offers hospitality before she knows how fatally her story will become entangled with Aeneas’s mission.
  • The cave and its meaningsDido understands the union as marriage; the narrator, omens and later action make its status uncertain. The difference between private meaning and public recognition drives the tragedy that follows.
  • The games for AnchisesAeneas turns mourning into ritual, competition and shared memory. The games briefly show leadership without conquest, though disorder and divine interference remain close.
  • The underworldAnchises reveals future Roman heroes and the work of empire. The pageant gives Aeneas historical purpose, but the exit through the gate of false dreams leaves the revelation with an unease readers cannot simply dismiss.
  • Turnus at Aeneas’s feetThe defeated warrior appeals to a son’s understanding of a father. Aeneas pauses, then sees Pallas’s belt and kills him. The epic ends at the point where vengeance overtakes mercy, refusing a comfortable celebration.

    • Reading Dryden’s translation

    John Dryden’s couplets are not a transparent pane. They give Virgil an English Augustan music, argument and pace of their own.

    Couplets

    Sense arrives through balance

    Rhyme and paired lines encourage antithesis, completion and rhetorical turn. A long Latin sentence may become a sequence of firm English units, making political and moral contrast especially audible.

    Distance

    Translation joins two Augustan ages

    Dryden wrote under a monarchy and literary culture that understood Rome as a powerful language for modern politics. His Virgil belongs to antiquity and to the arguments of late seventeenth-century England.

    Method

    Compare a passage, not isolated words

    When another translation feels different, compare the movement of a whole speech or scene. Look for what Dryden makes more direct, more formal, more heroic or more emotionally explicit.

    People caught inside destiny

    Prophecy explains the scale of the journey, but the poem remains with characters who have to live through what history calls necessary.

  • AeneasSurvivor, father, leader and future ancestor. His defining duty is not obedience to one simple rule, but the attempt to hold divine command, family memory and responsibility for his people together.
  • DidoFounder and ruler of Carthage before Aeneas arrives. Her tragedy makes the cost of his departure political as well as personal: a city being built is interrupted by a destiny centred elsewhere.
  • AnchisesAt Troy he must be carried; in the underworld he becomes the interpreter of Rome’s future. Aeneas’s duty to his father gradually becomes duty to the history his father reveals.
  • AscaniusThe child for whom a future is being made. His presence joins domestic care to dynastic promise, while his part in the Italian hunt helps turn arrival into war.
  • TurnusItalian prince, rival and defender of a world altered by Trojan settlement. The poem gives him courage and grievance as well as destructive fury, making the final killing morally harder.
  • PallasEvander entrusts his young son to Aeneas. Pallas’s death turns public alliance into personal obligation and supplies the object—the captured belt—that decides Turnus’s fate.

    • How the poem slows history down

    Rome’s future may be certain, but Virgil repeatedly delays forward movement so that readers see what the destination costs.

    Storytelling

    Aeneas has to narrate Troy

    Books 2 and 3 place the hero inside his own retrospective account. He knows which losses came next, but his listeners hear a survivor rebuilding sequence from catastrophe. Public epic temporarily becomes testimony.

    Simile

    Comparison opens another world

    Epic similes interrupt action with farmers, animals, weather, crowds and landscapes. They do more than decorate violence: they ask readers to see a warrior or city through forms of life that do not share the poem’s political destiny.

    Objects

    Pictures know more than their viewers

    The images of Troy at Carthage make Aeneas encounter his suffering as public art. The shield forged for him reaches forward to Roman history he cannot yet understand. In both, looking becomes an encounter with time beyond the immediate scene.

    Questions the ending leaves open

    Has Aeneas acted from public duty, personal vengeance or both? Does the sight of Pallas’s belt reveal a responsibility he must fulfil, or does it destroy the moment in which mercy seemed possible? Why does the poem stop at Turnus’s death rather than continuing to marriage, settlement and Rome? A strong reading should not force these into one answer. The ending matters because the promised civilisation remains inseparable from the anger of its final act.
