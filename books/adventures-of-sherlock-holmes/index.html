The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes, Arthur Conan Doyle

First published as a collection in 1892, The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes contains twelve of Arthur Conan Doyle’s most famous detective stories.

Narrated by Dr Watson, the collection follows Holmes through cases of blackmail, fraud, disappearance, inheritance, disguise and murder. ‘A Scandal in Bohemia’, ‘The Red-Headed League’, ‘The Blue Carbuncle’, ‘The Speckled Band’ and ‘The Copper Beeches’ helped establish the method, voice and structure of modern detective fiction.

The complete Astor Library edition keeps the stories together with summaries and contextual notes. Further material follows Doyle’s life, publication history, Victorian London, deduction, language, themes, characters, key passages, criticism and possible routes for teaching.

How the book works

Three ways to see the structure before getting lost in the detail.

Form

A case in one sitting

Each story creates a client, a puzzle and a field of possible explanations quickly. Watson’s delayed understanding gives the final explanation its force.

Method

Observation is not magic

Holmes notices material details, but his conclusions also depend on specialist knowledge, interviews, experiments, disguise and networks of information.

Society

Private trouble at Baker Street

Clients bring domestic, financial and professional danger into the consulting room. The cases reveal how much Victorian security depends on money, status and secrecy.

A closer reading

The pleasure of the solution is only one part of these stories. Their narrators, streets and clients deserve equal attention.

Watson

The stories are built from limited knowledge

Watson is not merely the companion who fails to keep up. He decides what the reader sees, when a clue becomes important and how Holmes appears in memory. Admiration shapes the portrait as strongly as observation.

Holmes sometimes complains that Watson makes stories out of demonstrations, but the stories depend upon exactly that transformation. A bare chain of inference would not create suspense, character or the sense of returning to Baker Street after the case is over.

London

A modern city supplies both clue and cover

Railways, cabs, newspapers, banks, hotels and postal systems make rapid investigation possible. They also allow people, money and information to disappear into crowds and routines.

The collection moves easily between aristocratic rooms, suburban villas, professional offices and rented lodgings. Holmes reads this social geography as carefully as a footprint, but the city is never fully transparent: respectability can conceal coercion, fraud or violence.

Clients

The law and the just ending are not always the same

Many clients arrive because ordinary authority has failed them. Helen Stoner is endangered inside her family; Violet Hunter is asked to accept alarming employment; Irene Adler protects herself against a king who assumes he may control her.

Holmes does not always deliver a criminal to the police. He weighs motive, danger and consequence, sometimes allowing a person to leave. The endings therefore ask readers to judge the detective’s private authority as well as admire his method.

Reading the collection as a collection

The twelve cases can stand alone, but their order builds a larger portrait of Holmes, Watson and the world that arrives at Baker Street.

Opening

Irene Adler alters the terms

‘A Scandal in Bohemia’ begins the volume by denying Holmes an uncomplicated victory. Adler reads the situation, recognises the disguise and protects herself. The story establishes Holmes’s brilliance while warning readers not to mistake brilliance for total control.

It also places power under examination. The royal client has rank, money and male authority; Adler has performance, intelligence and the ability to leave.

Return

Baker Street creates continuity

The cases vary in place and danger, yet the repeated return to 221B gives the collection its home. A knock at the door, an unusual visitor and Holmes’s first observation become familiar pleasures. Repetition makes small changes visible: Watson’s confidence grows, and Holmes’s habits become part of the reader’s evidence.

The consulting room is both refuge and threshold. Private troubles enter it, are turned into narratives and send the two men back into the city.

Ending

The final case refuses tidiness

‘The Copper Beeches’ ends with Violet Hunter escaping an alarming household, but the solution does not produce the marriage Watson briefly imagines. Holmes’s interest belongs to the problem rather than the sentimental future of the client.

That coolness leaves a useful question at the end of the book: how much does the detective understand about people once the pattern of the crime has been explained?

People to keep in view

The figures who carry the main pressures of the work.

  • Sherlock HolmesConsulting detective: exacting, theatrical and sometimes too pleased with the effect of his own reasoning.
  • Dr John WatsonFriend, witness and narrator. His partial knowledge gives the stories both warmth and suspense.
  • Irene AdlerThe woman at the centre of ‘A Scandal in Bohemia’, remembered because she defeats Holmes’s plan.
  • Jabez WilsonThe pawnbroker whose unlikely employment opens ‘The Red-Headed League’.
  • Helen StonerThe frightened client in ‘The Speckled Band’, attempting to escape the danger surrounding her sister’s death.
  • Violet HunterThe governess in ‘The Copper Beeches’, alert enough to recognise that strange conditions conceal a crime.

    Not answers to memorise, but problems the writing keeps alive.

  • EvidenceSeparate what Holmes sees from what he infers. At what point does the explanation become convincing?
  • NarrationWhat does Watson misunderstand, withhold or admire, and how does that guide the reader?
  • JusticeThe legal ending and Holmes’s moral judgement are not always the same. Where do they part company?

    • How a Holmes story handles evidence

    The solution may be brilliant, but Doyle usually makes the route to it visible: a client tells a story, Holmes notices what has been misread and Watson controls what the reader learns.

    The client

    A problem arrives already shaped

    Clients bring facts, but they also bring assumptions about class, gender, money and respectability. Holmes listens for the point at which an ordinary explanation has been ruled out too quickly or a strange detail has been treated as irrelevant.

    The opening interview therefore gives the reader two cases at once: what happened, and why the person telling it cannot yet see what happened.

    Watson

    The witness is not a camera

    Watson selects, arranges and delays. He records Holmes’s observations without always understanding their weight, allowing readers to possess the clue before they possess the method.

    His admiration gives the stories warmth, but his bafflement also protects the final reveal. A case is not simply solved; it is edited into an account of Holmes solving it.

    The ending

    Justice and law can separate

    Not every conclusion ends in arrest. Holmes sometimes lets an offender go, protects a client’s privacy or recognises that the formal law would produce a poorer result than his own judgement.

    Those decisions make him more than a machine of deduction. They also ask who has given a private detective the authority to decide which truth becomes public.

    Six cases, six kinds of problem

    The collection is broader than murder. Doyle tests the same method against blackmail, organised fraud, family control and apparently impossible violence.

  • A Scandal in BohemiaHolmes reads disguises and intentions but misjudges Irene Adler. The case establishes his method by showing its limit: another person can observe, plan and perform just as well as he can.
  • The Red-Headed LeagueAn absurd advertisement conceals a practical crime. The comedy of Jabez Wilson’s employment distracts from the geography of his shop and the reason anyone wants him absent.
  • The Blue CarbuncleA hat and a Christmas goose connect poverty, chance and theft. Holmes reconstructs a person from an object, then decides that solving the chain does not require handing its weakest participant to the police.
  • The Speckled BandThreat is hidden inside a country house, a will and a doctor’s authority. The locked room becomes less mysterious once Holmes asks who controls the building and who profits from fear.
  • The Man with the Twisted LipRespectable identity and street disguise occupy the same man. The story turns the city into an economy of appearances in which the apparently ruined body may be a successful professional performance.
  • The Copper BeechesViolet Hunter’s employment asks her to surrender hair, clothing and freedom of movement. Her precise report makes her a strong observer in her own right, while the house reveals how domestic authority can become imprisonment.
