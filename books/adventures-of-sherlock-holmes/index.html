Across twelve cases, Holmes and Watson move through a London of lodgings, clubs, banks, railway timetables, newspaper advertisements and locked rooms. The mysteries are compact, but their subjects are wide: money, marriage, class, reputation, violence and the power to read another person’s life from a trace.

The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes, Arthur Conan Doyle

First published as a collection in 1892, The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes contains twelve of Arthur Conan Doyle’s most famous detective stories.

Narrated by Dr Watson, the collection follows Holmes through cases of blackmail, fraud, disappearance, inheritance, disguise and murder. ‘A Scandal in Bohemia’, ‘The Red-Headed League’, ‘The Blue Carbuncle’, ‘The Speckled Band’ and ‘The Copper Beeches’ helped establish the method, voice and structure of modern detective fiction.

The complete Astor Library edition keeps the stories together with summaries and contextual notes. Further material follows Doyle’s life, publication history, Victorian London, deduction, language, themes, characters, key passages, criticism and possible routes for teaching.

How the book works

Three ways to see the structure before getting lost in the detail.

Form

A case in one sitting

Each story creates a client, a puzzle and a field of possible explanations quickly. Watson’s delayed understanding gives the final explanation its force.

Method

Observation is not magic

Holmes notices material details, but his conclusions also depend on specialist knowledge, interviews, experiments, disguise and networks of information.

Society

Private trouble at Baker Street

Clients bring domestic, financial and professional danger into the consulting room. The cases reveal how much Victorian security depends on money, status and secrecy.

People to keep in view

The figures who carry the main pressures of the work.

  • Sherlock HolmesConsulting detective: exacting, theatrical and sometimes too pleased with the effect of his own reasoning.
  • Dr John WatsonFriend, witness and narrator. His partial knowledge gives the stories both warmth and suspense.
  • Irene AdlerThe woman at the centre of ‘A Scandal in Bohemia’, remembered because she defeats Holmes’s plan.
  • Jabez WilsonThe pawnbroker whose unlikely employment opens ‘The Red-Headed League’.
  • Helen StonerThe frightened client in ‘The Speckled Band’, attempting to escape the danger surrounding her sister’s death.
  • Violet HunterThe governess in ‘The Copper Beeches’, alert enough to recognise that strange conditions conceal a crime.

    • Questions worth carrying through the text

    Not answers to memorise, but problems the writing keeps alive.

  • EvidenceSeparate what Holmes sees from what he infers. At what point does the explanation become convincing?
  • NarrationWhat does Watson misunderstand, withhold or admire, and how does that guide the reader?
  • JusticeThe legal ending and Holmes’s moral judgement are not always the same. Where do they part company?
