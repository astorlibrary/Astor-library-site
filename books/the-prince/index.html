The Prince, Niccolò Machiavelli

Written in 1513 and first published in 1532, The Prince is Niccolò Machiavelli's analysis of political rule in a fragmented Renaissance Italy. Drawing on examples from Cesare Borgia, the papacy and classical Rome, Machiavelli examines principalities, military organisation, fortune and the qualities required of a successful ruler. Virtù and fortuna structure the argument; appearance, fear and necessity replace ideal moral theory.

Often read as a foundational text of modern political thought, the treatise separates effective governance from conventional ethical expectations.

Records worth knowing

Short factual records before the longer context.

  • Original title The work circulated under the Latin title De Principatibus, meaning Of Principalities.
  • Dedication The printed text is dedicated to Lorenzo di Piero de' Medici.
  • Posthumous publication Machiavelli died in 1527; the first printed edition appeared in 1532.
  • Borgia case study Cesare Borgia, called Duke Valentino, is one of the central modern examples in the treatise.
  • Citizen arms Machiavelli distrusts mercenaries and auxiliaries; he links state security to its own arms.
  • Not a neutral manual The book is practical, but it is also shaped by Machiavelli's Florentine experience and his desire to be politically useful again.

    • Text and publication history

    The first publication did not begin the book's life. The Prince circulated in manuscript before print, and its printed form appeared only after Machiavelli's death.

    1532 and after

    First print and later editions

    • The first edition was printed in Rome by Antonio Blado in 1532.
    • A Florentine edition by Bernardo Giunta also appeared in 1532.
    • Later editions often gathered The Prince with other Machiavellian writings, including The Life of Castruccio Castracani and texts on Duke Valentino.
    • The title-page tradition makes the Medici dedication visible as part of the book's political presentation.

    1513

    Composition after political defeat

    • In 1512 the Medici returned to Florence and the republican regime fell.
    • Machiavelli lost his office in the Second Chancery.
    • In 1513 he was imprisoned and tortured after being suspected in an anti-Medici conspiracy.
    • Later that year he wrote from enforced retirement about his reading and the composition of a small work on principalities.

    Manuscript circulation

    A book known before print

    • The treatise circulated in manuscript before the first printed edition.
    • Its first readers therefore encountered it as a courtly and political document before it became a printed classic.
    • That early circulation helps explain why controversy gathered around the work before print fixed the text for wider circulation.

    1532

    Medici permission and papal privilege

    • The first printed edition appeared with permission linked to Pope Clement VII, a Medici pope.
    • Publication came five years after Machiavelli's death.
    • The book reached print in the same year as Machiavelli's Florentine Histories, another posthumous publication.

    Machiavelli and Florence

    The treatise cannot be separated from Machiavelli's years in Florentine service, the fall of the republic, the return of the Medici and the pressure of foreign war in Italy.

    1469-1527

    Niccolò Machiavelli

    • Born in Florence in 1469.
    • Served the Florentine Republic as secretary and diplomat after 1498.
    • Observed courts, armies, papal politics and French power at close range.
    • After 1512 he wrote from political exclusion, trying to convert experience into analysis.

    Florence

    Republic, Medici and civic office

    • Machiavelli's political career belonged to the Florentine Republic between Savonarola's fall and the Medici restoration.
    • His work involved diplomacy, correspondence and military organisation.
    • The Prince is written by someone who knew government as paperwork, negotiation and risk, not only as theory.

    1527 / 1787

    Death and later commemoration

    • Machiavelli died in 1527, soon after the Sack of Rome and another upheaval in Florentine politics.
    • His Santa Croce monument was made much later, in 1787.
    • The inscription, often translated as saying no praise equals so great a name, shows the later transformation of a controversial writer into an Italian cultural monument.

    Italian political background

    The Prince is a book of Italian crisis: city-states, papal territories, mercenary armies, French invasions, Spanish pressure and fragile dynastic rule.

    1494

    French intervention

    • Charles VIII of France invaded Italy in 1494.
    • The invasion destabilised older balances between Italian states.
    • Machiavelli's political world is one in which foreign armies could decide the fate of cities.

    1512

    Medici return

    • The Medici returned to Florence in 1512 after years of republican government.
    • Machiavelli was dismissed from office because of his association with the previous regime.
    • The dedication to a Medici ruler has to be read against that loss of position.

    Papacy

    Church power as Italian power

    • The papacy is treated as a political force, not simply a religious authority.
    • Alexander VI, Julius II and Leo X matter because papal power affected territory, war, patronage and alliances.
    • Machiavelli's analysis repeatedly treats religion through its political effects.

    Mercenary war

    Condottieri and insecurity

    • Italian war depended heavily on hired captains and mercenary forces.
    • Machiavelli treats mercenary arms as dangerous because they serve pay, reputation and private interest.
    • His preference for a ruler's own arms is one of the book's most concrete policy arguments.

    Examples and political figures

    Machiavelli argues through examples: ancient Rome, recent Italy, papal politics, new princes and failed rulers.

    Cesare Borgia

    Duke Valentino as case study

    • Cesare Borgia is the major recent example of a new prince in the treatise.
    • Machiavelli praises the severity and planning of Borgia's Romagna policy while noting the role of fortune in his fall.
    • Chapter 7 turns Borgia into a practical example of acquisition, consolidation and vulnerability.

    Agathocles

    Cruelty, crime and rule

    • Machiavelli distinguishes between gaining power through wickedness and achieving political glory.
    • The Agathocles example is deliberately uncomfortable: effective seizure of power does not automatically create honour.
    • This keeps the book more complex than a simple endorsement of cruelty.

    Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, Theseus

    Founders and armed prophets

    • Chapter 6 uses legendary and ancient founders to think about new orders.
    • The contrast between armed and unarmed prophets is one of the book's sharpest claims about reform.
    • New political orders require belief, force and institutional follow-through.

    Political terms and arguments

    Key terms should be read in context. Virtù is not simple moral virtue, and fortuna is not mere luck.

    Virtù

    Capacity, force and political intelligence

    • Virtù names energy, skill, command, adaptability and the capacity to act effectively.
    • It can include qualities that conventional moral language would not call virtuous.
    • The word is central because Machiavelli judges political action by effect under pressure.

    Fortuna

    Chance, timing and instability

    • Fortuna names the unstable conditions that no ruler fully controls.
    • Machiavelli does not simply say fortune rules everything.
    • His argument asks how far preparation, boldness and adaptability can resist fortune's damage.

    Fear and love

    Chapter 17

    • The famous question is whether it is better to be loved or feared.
    • Machiavelli's answer is conditional: both would be best, but fear is more reliable where one must choose.
    • He still warns against being hated, which limits the argument more than popular summaries suggest.

    Appearance

    Being and seeming

    • Chapter 18 argues that rulers must know how to appear merciful, faithful, humane, upright and religious.
    • The passage does not make hypocrisy a private vice only; it makes appearance a technique of rule.
    • Public judgement is shaped by effects, surfaces and success.

    Arms and laws

    The state as force and order

    • Machiavelli joins arms and laws rather than treating war as separate from politics.
    • Good laws depend on good arms, and insecure arms corrupt political order.
    • This argument makes military policy central to the book's political theory.

    Necessity

    Action under constraint

    • Necessity is not an excuse for every act.
    • It describes situations in which rulers must choose under danger, shortage of time and competing harms.
    • The book's hard tone comes from taking those conditions as politically normal, not exceptional.

    Chronological record

    Dated points for students, teachers and cross-reference.

    1469

    Machiavelli born

    • Niccolò Machiavelli was born in Florence on 3 May 1469.

    1498

    Second Chancery

    • Machiavelli entered Florentine government service after Savonarola's fall.
    • His diplomatic and administrative work shaped the practical basis of his political writing.

    1502

    Encounter with Borgia politics

    • Machiavelli observed Cesare Borgia's campaign and methods at close range during diplomatic service.
    • Borgia later becomes one of the treatise's most important examples.

    1512-13

    Medici return, dismissal, prison

    • The republic fell, the Medici returned and Machiavelli lost office.
    • In 1513 he was imprisoned and tortured, then released under amnesty.

    1513

    The Prince composed

    • Machiavelli completed or substantially drafted the treatise in this period.
    • The work belongs to enforced political distance rather than active office.

    1527

    Death

    • Machiavelli died on 21 June 1527.
    • The first printed edition of The Prince appeared five years later.

    1532

    First publication

    • Il Principe was first printed in Rome by Antonio Blado.
    • The book then entered a wider European history of admiration, suspicion, translation and attack.

    1559

    Index of Prohibited Books

    • Machiavelli's works were placed on the Roman Index.
    • The ban shaped Catholic reception and helped make his name a byword for dangerous political counsel.

    Reception and later afterlives

    The word Machiavellian has often done less careful work than Machiavelli's text. Reception history matters because the book quickly became a symbol as well as a treatise.

    Sixteenth century

    Anti-Machiavel tradition

    • Humanist, religious and political readers attacked Machiavelli as a teacher of tyranny.
    • Gentillet's Discours contre Machiavel helped form the French anti-Machiavel tradition.
    • In England, the stage Machiavel became a figure of cunning, atheism and political fraud.

    Republican Machiavelli

    Discourses beside The Prince

    • The Prince is not the whole Machiavelli.
    • The Discourses on Livy develop republican arguments about law, conflict, citizenship and liberty.
    • Reading the two works together complicates the idea that Machiavelli only praises princely domination.

    Modern political thought

    Realism and the state

    • Modern readers often treat The Prince as a founding text of political realism.
    • Its force lies in separating political effect from inherited moral vocabulary.
    • The book remains teachable because its examples are old but its questions about power, image, force and stability remain usable.
