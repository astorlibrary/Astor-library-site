Utopia, Thomas More

First published in Latin at Louvain in 1516, Utopia sets Thomas More in conversation with the traveller Raphael Hythloday, who describes the laws, religion and social order of an imagined island. Private property is abolished, work is regulated and religious toleration is practised. Yet the framing dialogue keeps asking whether any of this is desirable, possible, satirical or ironic.

The notes stay with that uncertainty rather than tidying it away. The 1516 print history, More’s career under Henry VIII, Plato, humanist dialogue and travel writing give the argument its setting; section guides, a glossary and a bibliography help when More’s jokes and political questions begin to overlap.

At a glance

The language, speakers and printed objects that make More’s argument slippery.

Form

A dialogue, not a simple blueprint

  • Book I is framed as a conversation involving More, Peter Giles and Raphael Hythloday.
  • Book II gives Hythloday’s extended account of the island.
  • More’s fictional self does not simply endorse Hythloday’s claims.
  • The closing exchange leaves several Utopian customs under question rather than offering a settled political programme.

Title

No-place and good-place

  • The title draws on Greek wordplay: ou-topos, no-place, sits beside eu-topos, good-place.
  • More’s invented names keep signalling that the book is playful as well as serious.
  • Hythloday’s name has often been read as another warning, since it points towards both healing and nonsense-speaking.

Politics

Property, counsel and punishment

  • Book I discusses counsel to princes, war, theft, enclosure and punishment.
  • Book II presents a society without private property, with regulated labour and communal institutions.
  • The text should be read through its frame: the island is reported by Hythloday, not directly narrated by More.

Text, sources and early print history

The printed book was built through Latin text, letters, paratexts, maps, alphabets and later translations.

1516

Louvain first edition

  • Utopia was first printed in Latin at Louvain in 1516.
  • The first edition included a woodcut map of the island, a Utopian alphabet, prefatory verse and More’s epistle to Peter Giles.
  • The paratexts encourage readers to treat the island as both a plausible travel report and an invented literary device.
  • This is why textual history matters: the supporting material helps shape the joke, the argument and the uncertainty.

1516-1518

Early editions and added material

  • Four early editions were printed between 1516 and 1518.
  • Across those editions, letters, poems and commendatory matter were added, removed and rearranged.
  • The 1518 Basel edition is often treated as a major early witness because it gathers more of the supporting humanist apparatus around the text.
  • More’s circle, including Erasmus and Peter Giles, matters because the book presents itself as part of a learned international exchange.

1551 / 1684

Robinson, Burnet and English Utopia

  • Ralph Robinson’s 1551 translation was the first English-language translation.
  • Gilbert Burnet’s 1684 translation became a major later English version.
  • Many public-domain online texts descend through the Burnet and Henry Morley tradition.
  • Modern editions often need to decide how much of the early paratextual material to include.

Historical context

More writes inside early Tudor politics, Renaissance humanism and European travel literature.

1478-1535

More’s career under Henry VIII

  • More was a lawyer, writer, humanist and statesman.
  • He served as Lord Chancellor from 1529 until 1532.
  • He was imprisoned during the final part of his life and executed for high treason on 6 July 1535.
  • His later religious polemics and political career complicate any simple reading of Utopia as a modern liberal text.

Sources

Plato, Cicero, Lucian and travel writing

  • The dialogue form looks back to classical models, especially Plato and Cicero.
  • The comic and satirical handling of names, speakers and arguments owes something to Lucianic dialogue.
  • The traveller frame draws on contemporary European interest in voyages, especially reports associated with Amerigo Vespucci.
  • These influences allow More to combine philosophical debate with apparent ethnographic description.

Tudor England

Enclosure, labour and punishment

  • Book I links social disorder to poverty, land use, war and harsh punishment.
  • The famous discussion of sheep and enclosure sits inside a larger debate about whether political counsel can improve flawed states.
  • Book II then turns those pressures into a counter-model: fixed working hours, shared goods, public meals and regulated movement.
  • The uncomfortable presence of slavery and coercion prevents a clean idealising reading.

Timeline

From More’s Low Countries mission to the major early English translations.

1515

Low Countries mission and Antwerp frame

  • More travelled on diplomatic business to the Low Countries.
  • The fiction places More in Antwerp, where he is introduced to Raphael Hythloday by Peter Giles.
  • The setting lets the book move between English political argument and wider European humanist conversation.

1516

Latin publication at Louvain

  • The work appeared in Latin, aimed at learned readers across Europe.
  • Its full title presents it as a discussion of the best state of a commonwealth and the new island Utopia.
  • The book’s early apparatus makes the fiction look documentary while also inviting scepticism.

1518

Basel edition and humanist apparatus

  • The Basel edition helped stabilise the work’s early printed identity.
  • More’s epigrams and associated humanist material sit beside the political fiction.
  • The printed object is therefore more than Book I and Book II alone.

1551

First English translation

  • Ralph Robinson’s translation brought Utopia into English after More’s death.
  • The delay matters: the English text appears after More’s execution, the Henrician break with Rome and the early English Reformation.

1684

Burnet’s translation

  • Gilbert Burnet’s translation gave later readers a widely circulated English Utopia.
  • This version became part of the public-domain route through which many modern online readers encounter the text.

Afterlives and adaptations

The book’s strongest afterlife lies in political vocabulary, later utopian fiction, design culture and stage works about More.

1590s / 1922 / 1954 / 2005

Sir Thomas More on stage

  • The collaborative play Sir Thomas More is an early modern dramatic afterlife of More rather than an adaptation of Utopia.
  • Its performance record is patchier than Shakespeare’s major plays.
  • Modern revivals and scholarly attention have made it part of the wider theatre history of More’s public image.

1960 / 1966

A Man for All Seasons

  • Robert Bolt’s A Man for All Seasons opened in London in 1960.
  • The play concentrates on More’s conscience, Henry VIII’s divorce crisis and the political pressure leading to More’s execution.
  • Fred Zinnemann’s 1966 film adaptation starred Paul Scofield as More.
  • This is a More afterlife, not a direct adaptation of Utopia.

1968

R. A. Lafferty’s Past Master

  • Lafferty’s science-fiction novel brings Thomas More into a future utopian society called Astrobe.
  • The premise turns More into a judge of a later world built around utopian claims.
  • The book shows how Utopia became available as a framework for modern speculative fiction.

2016

Five-hundred-year anniversary culture

  • The first London Design Biennale used the theme “Utopia by Design” in 2016.
  • The theme marked the 500th anniversary of More’s book.
  • Such events show the word “utopia” moving beyond literary history into design, politics and public culture.

More on screen

The best-known screen dramas centre on More’s life, trial and execution rather than directly adapting the island dialogue.

1966 / 1988

More on screen

  • A Man for All Seasons was adapted for cinema in 1966, directed by Fred Zinnemann.
  • Paul Scofield reprised his stage role as More.
  • A later television adaptation appeared in 1988, directed by and starring Charlton Heston.
  • These films are biographical and historical afterlives of More, rather than screen adaptations of the island dialogue.
