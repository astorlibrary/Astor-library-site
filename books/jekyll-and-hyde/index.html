Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde, Robert Louis Stevenson

First published in 1886, Robert Louis Stevenson’s Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde follows the investigation of lawyer Gabriel Utterson into the connection between his friend Dr Henry Jekyll and the violent, secretive Edward Hyde. As disturbing events unfold, Utterson tries to understand Hyde’s influence over Jekyll and the nature of their relationship.

Set in London, the novella presents a divided urban environment in which public respectability coexists with hidden transgression. The gradual revelation of Jekyll’s experiment, and its consequences, exposes tensions between scientific ambition, moral responsibility and the limits of self-control.

Often associated with Gothic fiction and early science fiction, the text reflects contemporary interest in psychology, identity and the dual nature of human behaviour. Its structure is built around documents, testimonies and delayed disclosure, so the central mystery is uncovered gradually rather than announced.

Records worth knowing

Short factual records for teaching, revision and source-led reading.

  • Title The first title is Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde, without an initial The. The missing article helps make the title sound like a legal or medical file.
  • Publication The novella appeared in January 1886 in Britain and the United States. It was quickly read beyond the normal market for short fiction.
  • Length The first London edition was short. Its compact plot made it unusually adaptable for theatre, silent film and later horror cinema.
  • Narrative The transformation is not staged directly until the explanatory documents near the end. Most of the book is investigative delay.
  • Utterson The central observer is not Jekyll. Stevenson makes the lawyer’s restraint, loyalty and partial knowledge part of the form.
  • Adaptation drift Later versions often add romance, expand Hyde’s violence and put the transformation at the centre much earlier than the novella does.

    • Text and publication

    The novella’s first form matters: it is short, legal-medical in tone, and built around controlled disclosure.

    1886

    First London edition

    • Published by Longmans, Green and Co.
    • The title page gives the original form: Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde.
    • The title presents the narrative as a case: legal, medical, documentary and unresolved.
    • Later posters and films make Hyde visually dominant; the first title page is plain, restrained and almost clinical.

    Textual note

    Case, not confession

    • The novella is not told as Jekyll’s continuous confession.
    • Utterson receives the story in fragments before the final explanatory packets.
    • That sequence matters: the reader is asked to interpret evidence before being allowed to know the mechanism.
    • This makes the book useful for teaching narrative perspective, withheld information and form.

    Robert Louis Stevenson and source history

    Stevenson’s novella draws on older double-life stories, Edinburgh memory, Gothic convention and the pressure of late-Victorian moral respectability.

    1850-1894

    Stevenson before and after Jekyll

    • Stevenson was born in Edinburgh on 13 November 1850.
    • Treasure Island had already made him widely known before Jekyll and Hyde.
    • In 1886 he also published Kidnapped, another sign of the range and pace of his work in the mid-1880s.
    • He died at Vailima, Samoa, on 3 December 1894.

    Source context

    Deacon Brodie and the public double life

    • William Brodie was remembered as a respectable Edinburgh tradesman and civic figure who also led a criminal life.
    • Stevenson had already worked with W. E. Henley on Deacon Brodie, or The Double Life.
    • Brodie is a context for double identity and respectability, not a one-to-one original for Jekyll.
    • The connection helps explain why the novella’s horror is social as well as supernatural.

    Bournemouth, 1885

    Dream, draft and revision

    • Stevenson wrote the novella while living at Skerryvore in Bournemouth.
    • Family accounts connect the story with a dream of transformation.
    • The famous story that Stevenson burned an earlier draft is part of the book’s composition history, though modern accounts treat the details carefully rather than as a simple proven scene.
    • The surviving result is unusually tight: the plot depends on compression, not sprawl.

    Victorian London, science and identity

    The novella uses a modern professional city, not a remote castle. Its Gothic energy comes from doors, papers, reputations, laboratories and men who do not say what they know.

    Urban form

    Front doors, back doors and hidden rooms

    • The opening story turns on a damaged-looking door in an otherwise respectable street.
    • Jekyll’s house and laboratory create a divided architecture: one face public, one route concealed.
    • Soho is used as a space of obscurity, fog, suspicion and social descent.
    • The city is not just setting; it is a system of partial access.

    Professional men

    Law, medicine and silence

    • Utterson is a lawyer; Lanyon and Jekyll are doctors; Enfield is a gentleman observer.
    • Their authority depends on restraint, reputation and male professional codes.
    • Those same codes slow the investigation because scandal is treated as dangerous knowledge.
    • The plot works because respectable men avoid direct speech until avoidance becomes impossible.

    Science

    Experiment and moral responsibility

    • Jekyll’s experiment is revealed retrospectively, through his written statement.
    • Lanyon’s horror marks a boundary between acceptable professional science and Jekyll’s hidden research.
    • The novella does not simply attack science. It attacks secrecy, self-exemption and the refusal to accept responsibility for experiment.
    • The failed supply of salt at the end turns the mystery into a problem of material conditions as well as morality.

    Identity

    Before modern diagnostic shorthand

    • The phrase “Jekyll and Hyde” became a cultural shorthand after the novella.
    • Stevenson’s own language is moral, legal, religious and medical rather than modern diagnostic vocabulary.
    • Hyde is not simply a second personality in a modern clinical sense. He is Jekyll’s experiment in separating and indulging what Jekyll wants to disown.

    The document sequence

    The book is arranged as a file of evidence. These sections are useful for students because they show how Stevenson controls knowledge.

    Chapter 1

    Story of the Door

    Enfield’s account gives Hyde a first public record before the reader knows why the door matters.

    Will

    Jekyll’s legal paper

    The will turns Hyde into a problem of inheritance, disappearance and legal authority.

    Witness

    The Carew murder

    A maid’s testimony makes Hyde visible as public violence rather than private rumour.

    Handwriting

    The note problem

    Mr Guest’s comparison of handwriting quietly shifts suspicion back towards Jekyll.

    Packet

    Lanyon’s narrative

    Lanyon supplies the physical proof of transformation, but only after delay, illness and death.

    Confession

    Jekyll’s statement

    Jekyll finally explains the experiment, but he also shapes his own defence.

    Silence

    Utterson’s limits

    Utterson is loyal and observant, but the story repeatedly shows what he cannot ask or admit.

    Effect

    Delayed disclosure

    The plot makes the reader experience investigation before explanation.

    Chronological record

    Dated reference points for reading and teaching.

    1850

    Stevenson born in Edinburgh

    • Born Robert Lewis Balfour Stevenson; he later used Robert Louis Stevenson.
    • Edinburgh’s divided social and architectural identity remains a useful background for the novella’s double-life motif.

    1880

    Deacon Brodie

    • Stevenson and W. E. Henley developed the Brodie material before Jekyll and Hyde.
    • The double-life pattern was therefore not a sudden invention in 1885.

    1883

    Treasure Island

    • Stevenson’s adventure fiction had already reached a wide audience before the later Gothic novella.

    1885

    Composition at Bournemouth

    • Stevenson was living at Skerryvore.
    • The tale was drafted and revised with unusual speed according to family accounts, then refined for publication.

    1886

    Publication and rapid success

    • The book appeared in January 1886.
    • Its early reception was closely tied to moral discussion as well as literary interest.

    1894

    Death in Samoa

    • Stevenson died on 3 December 1894.
    • By then Jekyll and Hyde had already moved decisively into stage culture.

    Stage afterlives

    Theatre quickly changed the book. Utterson’s investigation became less central, while the dual role of Jekyll and Hyde became a star actor’s opportunity.

    1887-1888

    Thomas Russell Sullivan and Richard Mansfield

    • The authorised adaptation opened at the Boston Museum on 9 May 1887.
    • Richard Mansfield played both Jekyll and Hyde.
    • The London Lyceum run began in August 1888.
    • Stage make-up, lighting and performance made visible what Stevenson had delayed on the page.

    1888

    Daniel E. Bandmann and rival versions

    • Bandmann worked with John McKinney on an unauthorised rival adaptation.
    • His version opened in New York before reaching London.
    • The rivalry shows how fast the novella became commercially valuable as performance property.
    • Adaptation competition also explains why the stage Jekyll quickly developed features not in Stevenson’s structure.

    1888 London climate

    Jekyll, Hyde and the Ripper months

    • Mansfield’s London performances took place in the same period as the Whitechapel murders.
    • Press and public discussion linked the visible stage monster with contemporary fears about hidden urban violence.
    • This connection belongs to reception history, not to Stevenson’s original composition.

    1990 / 1997

    Wildhorn, Bricusse and Cuden musical

    • Jekyll & Hyde was developed as a musical by Frank Wildhorn, Leslie Bricusse and Steve Cuden.
    • The Broadway production opened in 1997.
    • The musical turns the story further towards romance, spectacle and sung self-division.

    Screen versions

    Cinema usually foregrounds transformation. The page becomes face, body, make-up, camera trick and split performance.

    1908-1913

    Early screen versions

    • Short silent versions appeared before the major 1920 feature tradition.
    • These early films already show the attraction of the transformation scene as a screen effect.
    • The novella’s document trail is difficult to preserve in short film, so adaptation naturally shifts towards visible change.

    1920

    John Barrymore and the silent feature

    • John S. Robertson directed the Paramount version.
    • John Barrymore played Jekyll and Hyde.
    • The film drew on the Sullivan stage tradition as well as Stevenson’s novella.
    • Barrymore’s physical performance made the transformation a showcase for bodily control and screen close-up.

    1931

    Rouben Mamoulian and Fredric March

    • Mamoulian directed the Paramount pre-Code film.
    • Fredric March played the dual role opposite Miriam Hopkins and Rose Hobart.
    • March won the Academy Award for Best Actor, shared with Wallace Beery for The Champ.
    • The film’s point-of-view camera, dissolves, make-up and sexual frankness make it one of the major screen afterlives.

    Image record

    Hyde becomes a face

    • Film stills and posters helped fix Hyde as a visual object.
    • This is different from Stevenson’s prose, where witnesses struggle to describe what is wrong with Hyde.
    • The adaptation history turns indescribability into make-up, posture and publicity art.

    1941

    Victor Fleming, Spencer Tracy, Ingrid Bergman and Lana Turner

    • MGM remade the story in 1941.
    • Spencer Tracy played Jekyll and Hyde.
    • Ingrid Bergman played Ivy and Lana Turner played Beatrix.
    • The remake shifts the material into prestige studio melodrama as well as horror.

    1996 / 2023

    Later returns and inversions

    • Mary Reilly retold the story through a domestic servant’s viewpoint.
    • Later adaptations continue to test whether the story is about science, desire, addiction, class, gender, violence or performance.
    • Hammer’s Doctor Jekyll continued the tradition of rewriting Stevenson through new bodies, settings and forms of identity.

    Teaching and reading pathways

    Useful routes through the text for students and teachers.

    Structure

    Read it as a case file

    • Track who knows what at the end of each chapter.
    • Separate witness statements from interpretation.
    • Ask why Stevenson delays Jekyll’s own account until the end.

    Language

    Describe Hyde by failed description

    • Collect moments where characters cannot describe Hyde precisely.
    • Compare that vagueness with the very precise documents around him.
    • This contrast is one of the novella’s strongest Gothic techniques.

    Context

    Respectability under pressure

    • Study doors, wills, letters, dinner parties, servants and professional networks.
    • The horror depends on respectable systems being compromised from inside.

    Adaptation

    Compare page and performance

    • Ask what changes when one actor visibly plays both parts.
    • Compare Stevenson’s delayed disclosure with film posters that reveal the split immediately.
