Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde, Robert Louis Stevenson

First published in 1886, Robert Louis Stevenson’s Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde follows the investigation of lawyer Gabriel Utterson into the connection between his friend Dr Henry Jekyll and the violent, secretive Edward Hyde. As a series of disturbing events unfolds, Utterson seeks to understand Hyde’s influence over Jekyll and the nature of their relationship.

Set in London, the novella presents a divided urban environment in which public respectability coexists with hidden transgression. The gradual revelation of Jekyll’s experiment exposes tensions between scientific ambition, moral responsibility and the limits of self-control.

Records worth knowing

Small records first. These prevent the novella being flattened into a simple monster story.

  • Case file title The title presents the story as a case: legal, medical, documentary and moral.
  • Utterson’s filter The first reading experience depends on what Utterson can infer, delay, misread or refuse to imagine.
  • Late confession Jekyll’s account comes near the end. The transformation is not the opening spectacle of the book.
  • Documents The plot moves through a will, letters, witness testimony, sealed statements and retrospective confession.
  • Urban split Respectable doors, back entrances, fog, Soho and locked rooms give the city a divided shape.
  • Adaptation shift Stage and film versions usually move attention from Utterson’s investigation to the actor’s visible double performance.

    • Text and publication history

    The novella’s first form was brief, cheap, portable and quickly successful. Its later fame often hides the precision of Stevenson’s original structure.

    1850-1894

    Robert Louis Stevenson

    • Stevenson was born in Edinburgh on 13 November 1850.
    • Before Jekyll and Hyde, he had already reached a wide readership with Treasure Island.
    • His work crosses adventure fiction, essays, travel writing, Scottish fiction, Gothic fiction and Pacific writing.
    • The later portrait is not a publication image for the novella, but it gives the page a clear author record.

    January 1886

    First London edition

    • Longmans, Green and Co. published the London edition.
    • The title page gives the original title without an initial “The”.
    • The book was short enough to circulate as a compact shocker and serious moral fable at once.
    • Later versions often advertise transformation and Hyde up front; Stevenson’s text withholds full explanation.

    US publication

    Scribner and early circulation

    • Charles Scribner’s Sons issued the American edition in New York.
    • The book spread quickly through Britain and the United States.
    • Its early reception crossed fiction review, religious comment, stage interest and popular sensation.
    • That mix explains why the novella entered public vocabulary unusually fast.

    Sources and origins

    The novella is not reducible to one source. It gathers Edinburgh stories of double life, Victorian science, Gothic convention and Stevenson’s own formal discipline.

    Deacon Brodie

    Respectability and night crime

    • William Brodie was an Edinburgh tradesman and town councillor who also lived a criminal double life.
    • Stevenson had already treated Brodie in Deacon Brodie, or The Double Life, written with W. E. Henley.
    • The Brodie connection is best used as source context: respectable surface, hidden appetite, divided public and private conduct.
    • Jekyll is not simply Brodie in scientific dress; Stevenson turns the double life into a tighter Gothic case.

    Dream and revision

    The famous composition story

    • Stevenson later became associated with the story that the central idea came through a dream.
    • The important publication fact is the revision: the novella is tightly shaped, delayed and controlled.
    • Whatever its originating image, the finished text works through legal and medical documents rather than dream logic.

    Gothic inheritance

    Doors, doubles and withheld knowledge

    • The text uses recognisable Gothic materials: night streets, locked rooms, hidden rooms, horror at the body and secrecy around the house.
    • It also borrows investigative habits from legal and detective fiction.
    • Hyde is frightening partly because he resists stable description: witnesses react strongly, but struggle to say exactly what is wrong.

    Victorian context

    The novella belongs to the 1880s: urban respectability, professional secrecy, scientific prestige, religious language and new ways of thinking about mind and conduct.

    London

    Respectable frontage and back-door geography

    • Jekyll’s house has a socially respectable face and a separate laboratory entrance.
    • The building turns moral division into architecture.
    • Soho, fog, night walking and anonymous streets create a city of partial sight.
    • The text does not need a full street map; it needs contrasts between polish and concealment.

    Science

    Experiment without public accountability

    • Jekyll’s experiment is not presented as ordinary medical progress.
    • It is private, secretive and finally self-excusing.
    • Lanyon’s horror marks a boundary between respectable science and Jekyll’s transgressive practice.
    • The novella is less anti-science than anti-secrecy, anti-vanity and anti-moral evasion.

    Identity

    Divided self before modern labels

    • Modern readers often attach later psychological terms to the story.
    • Stevenson’s language is more strongly moral, legal, religious and physiological.
    • The text speaks about divided conduct before it becomes a convenient modern clinical shorthand.
    • That difference matters when teaching the novella: Jekyll is responsible for choices, not merely a diagnosis.

    Class and violence

    Hyde as social disturbance

    • Hyde first appears through an assault on a child.
    • His violence crosses social categories: street witness, servant, doctor, lawyer, MP and household all become involved.
    • Carew’s murder moves the case from disturbing incident to public criminal crisis.
    • The novella repeatedly asks what polite society refuses to see until violence becomes impossible to absorb.

    Narrative method

    Stevenson builds suspense by restricting knowledge. The story is a file assembled too late.

    Utterson

    The lawyer as reader

    • Utterson reads wills, letters, social signs and silences.
    • His caution slows the story and preserves the mystery.
    • He is loyal enough to investigate, but conventional enough to miss the impossible explanation.

    Enfield

    The opening testimony

    • Enfield’s account of Hyde trampling the girl begins the case at street level.
    • The story is already mediated before Utterson acts.
    • The opening scene teaches readers to notice doors, witnesses, money, signatures and social pressure.

    Lanyon

    Scientific witness and collapse

    • Lanyon provides the first direct account of transformation.
    • His narrative arrives sealed, withheld and posthumous in effect.
    • His collapse gives the revelation physical cost before Jekyll explains it in words.

    Jekyll

    Confession and self-defence

    • Jekyll’s final statement explains the experiment but also attempts to control interpretation.
    • He admits division while shifting blame towards Hyde.
    • The reader must judge the confession, not just accept it as the final truth.

    Chronological record

    Dated reference points for students, teachers and readers.

    1850

    Stevenson born in Edinburgh

    • Born Robert Lewis Balfour Stevenson on 13 November 1850.
    • Edinburgh’s social divisions and stories of hidden conduct remain useful background for the later novella.

    1880

    Deacon Brodie

    • Stevenson and W. E. Henley’s play about Brodie belongs directly to the double-life source tradition.

    1883

    Treasure Island

    • Stevenson’s adventure novel had already made him a widely read author before Jekyll and Hyde.

    1885

    Composition and revision

    • The novella was composed and revised during Stevenson’s Bournemouth period.
    • Its compact scale helped it move quickly into stage performance.

    1886

    Publication

    • The book appeared in Britain and the United States in January 1886.
    • It became one of Stevenson’s most famous works within his lifetime.

    1894

    Stevenson dies in Samoa

    • Stevenson died at Vailima, Samoa, on 3 December 1894.
    • By then Jekyll and Hyde already had a major theatrical identity.

    Stage afterlives

    The stage tradition changed the novella quickly. Utterson’s file became a star vehicle: one actor, two bodies, visible transformation.

    1887-1888

    Richard Mansfield and Thomas Russell Sullivan

    • Sullivan’s authorised adaptation opened at the Boston Museum on 9 May 1887.
    • Richard Mansfield played both Jekyll and Hyde.
    • The play gave audiences a visible transformation scene rather than Stevenson’s delayed written revelation.
    • The London Lyceum programme is a surviving object from the early stage tradition.

    1888

    London, sensation and Ripper-year pressure

    • Mansfield brought the play to London in 1888.
    • The stage Hyde appeared in the same year as the Whitechapel murders.
    • That coincidence sharpened public attention around performance, violence and urban fear.
    • It also shows how quickly Stevenson’s fiction became part of public discussion beyond literary reviewing.

    Rival adaptations

    Bandmann and unauthorised performance

    • Daniel E. Bandmann appeared in a rival version associated with John McKinney.
    • Competing adaptations show the commercial speed of the novella’s afterlife.
    • They also show what stage versions wanted most: transformation, conflict and recognisable moral spectacle.

    1990 / 1997

    Musical afterlife

    • Frank Wildhorn, Leslie Bricusse and Steve Cuden developed the musical Jekyll & Hyde.
    • The Broadway production opened in 1997.
    • The musical moves the story further from Utterson’s documentary mystery towards romance, spectacle and sung confession.

    Screen versions

    Cinema usually abandons the novella’s delayed revelation because film can show the body changing. The camera turns mystery into performance.

    1920

    John Barrymore and silent-film Hyde

    • John S. Robertson directed the Paramount version.
    • John Barrymore played Jekyll and Hyde.
    • The film draws on the stage tradition as well as Stevenson’s text.
    • Barrymore’s performance helped make bodily transformation central to screen versions.

    1931

    Rouben Mamoulian and Fredric March

    • Mamoulian’s Paramount film starred Fredric March, Miriam Hopkins and Rose Hobart.
    • March won the Academy Award for Best Actor, sharing the award with Wallace Beery.
    • The film’s transformation effects and Hyde make-up strongly shaped later visual traditions.
    • Its pre-Code sexual frankness also moves the story far from Stevenson’s all-male documentary frame.

    1931 image record

    Hyde as make-up tradition

    • Stevenson’s Hyde is difficult to describe precisely.
    • Film versions must choose a visible face and body.
    • March’s Hyde helped establish the simian, physically altered version that later popular culture often remembers.

    1941 / 1960 / later

    Remakes and variation

    • Victor Fleming’s 1941 MGM version starred Spencer Tracy, Ingrid Bergman and Lana Turner.
    • Hammer’s The Two Faces of Dr. Jekyll appeared in 1960.
    • Later versions often use Jekyll and Hyde as a loose cultural framework for split identity rather than a strict adaptation of the novella.

    What adaptations usually change

    These changes are useful when teaching the novella beside stage or screen versions.

  • They reveal earlier Adaptations often show Jekyll becoming Hyde long before Stevenson explains it.
  • They add women Stage and film versions frequently add fiancées, victims or romantic plots absent from the original structure.
  • They centre Jekyll Utterson often loses his role as investigator because the star part is the double role.
  • They show the body Hyde becomes make-up, posture, lighting, editing and special effects.
  • They simplify documents Wills, letters and sealed statements are often reduced or removed.
  • They moralise visually The novella’s problem of hidden self-control becomes visible degeneration.
