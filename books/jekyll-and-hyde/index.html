Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde, Robert Louis Stevenson

First published in 1886, Robert Louis Stevenson’s Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde follows lawyer Gabriel Utterson as he investigates the link between Dr Henry Jekyll and Edward Hyde.

The novella is built as a case file: doors, wills, letters, statements, withheld testimony and a final confession. Its Gothic pressure comes from professional respectability, scientific secrecy and the divided spaces of London.

Records worth knowing

Short factual records for teaching, revision and source-led reading.

  • Title The first title is Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde, without an initial The.
  • Publication The novella appeared in January 1886 in Britain and the United States.
  • Narrative The transformation is delayed until the explanatory documents near the end.
  • Utterson The central observer is a lawyer whose restraint and partial knowledge shape the form.
  • Adaptation drift Later versions often add romance, expand Hyde’s violence and foreground the transformation earlier than the book does.

    • Text and publication

    The novella’s first form matters: it is short, legal-medical in tone, and built around controlled disclosure.

    1886

    First London edition

    • Published by Longmans, Green and Co.
    • The title page gives the original form: Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde.
    • The title presents the narrative as a case: legal, medical, documentary and unresolved.

    Textual note

    Case, not confession

    • The novella is not told as Jekyll’s continuous confession.
    • Utterson receives the story in fragments before the final explanatory packets.
    • The reader interprets evidence before being allowed to know the mechanism.

    Robert Louis Stevenson and source history

    Stevenson’s novella draws on older double-life stories, Edinburgh memory, Gothic convention and late-Victorian respectability.

    1850-1894

    Stevenson before and after Jekyll

    • Stevenson was born in Edinburgh on 13 November 1850.
    • Treasure Island had already made him widely known before Jekyll and Hyde.
    • He died at Vailima, Samoa, on 3 December 1894.

    Source context

    Deacon Brodie and the public double life

    • William Brodie was remembered as a respectable Edinburgh tradesman and civic figure who also led a criminal life.
    • Stevenson had already worked with W. E. Henley on Deacon Brodie, or The Double Life.
    • Brodie is a context for double identity and respectability, not a one-to-one original for Jekyll.

    Bournemouth, 1885

    Dream, draft and revision

    • Stevenson wrote the novella while living at Skerryvore in Bournemouth.
    • Family accounts connect the story with a dream of transformation.
    • The surviving result is unusually tight: the plot depends on compression.

    Victorian London, science and identity

    The novella uses a modern professional city. Its Gothic energy comes from doors, papers, reputations, laboratories and men who do not say what they know.

    Urban form

    Front doors, back doors and hidden rooms

    • The opening story turns on a damaged-looking door in an otherwise respectable street.
    • Jekyll’s house and laboratory create a divided architecture: one face public, one route concealed.

    Professional men

    Law, medicine and silence

    • Utterson is a lawyer; Lanyon and Jekyll are doctors; Enfield is a gentleman observer.
    • Their authority depends on restraint, reputation and male professional codes.

    Science

    Experiment and moral responsibility

    • Jekyll’s experiment is revealed retrospectively, through his written statement.
    • The novella attacks secrecy, self-exemption and refusal of responsibility.

    Identity

    Before modern diagnostic shorthand

    • Stevenson’s own language is moral, legal, religious and medical rather than modern diagnostic vocabulary.
    • Hyde is Jekyll’s experiment in separating and indulging what Jekyll wants to disown.

    Stage afterlives

    Theatre quickly changed the book. Utterson’s investigation became less central, while the dual role became a star actor’s opportunity.

    1887-1888

    Thomas Russell Sullivan and Richard Mansfield

    • The authorised adaptation opened at the Boston Museum on 9 May 1887.
    • Richard Mansfield played both Jekyll and Hyde.
    • The London Lyceum run began in August 1888.

    1888

    Daniel E. Bandmann and rival versions

    • Bandmann worked with John McKinney on an unauthorised rival adaptation.
    • The rivalry shows how fast the novella became commercially valuable as performance property.

    1990 / 1997

    Wildhorn, Bricusse and Cuden musical

    • Jekyll & Hyde was developed as a musical by Frank Wildhorn, Leslie Bricusse and Steve Cuden.
    • The Broadway production opened in 1997.

    Screen versions

    Cinema usually foregrounds transformation. The page becomes face, body, make-up, camera trick and split performance.

    1908-1913

    Early screen versions

    • Short silent versions appeared before the major 1920 feature tradition.
    • These early films already show the attraction of the transformation scene as a screen effect.

    1920

    John Barrymore and the silent feature

    • John S. Robertson directed the Paramount version.
    • John Barrymore played Jekyll and Hyde.
    • The film drew on the Sullivan stage tradition as well as Stevenson’s novella.

    1931

    Rouben Mamoulian and Fredric March

    • Mamoulian directed the Paramount pre-Code film.
    • Fredric March played the dual role opposite Miriam Hopkins and Rose Hobart.
    • March won the Academy Award for Best Actor, shared with Wallace Beery for The Champ.

    1941

    Victor Fleming, Spencer Tracy, Ingrid Bergman and Lana Turner

    • MGM remade the story in 1941.
    • Spencer Tracy played Jekyll and Hyde.
    • The remake shifts the material into prestige studio melodrama as well as horror.
