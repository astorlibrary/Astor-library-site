Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde, Robert Louis Stevenson

First published in 1886, Stevenson’s novella follows the lawyer Gabriel Utterson as he investigates the connection between his friend Dr Henry Jekyll and the violent, secretive Edward Hyde. The case develops through wills, letters, witness accounts, sealed statements and delayed disclosure.

Set in London, the novella presents a divided urban world in which public respectability sits beside concealed transgression. The gradual revelation of Jekyll’s experiment exposes tensions between scientific ambition, moral responsibility and self-control. This edition presents the complete text with structured scholarly support, including publication history, historical context, chapter companions, key-passage study, thematic discussion, glossary, bibliography and teaching pathways.

Records worth knowing

Useful factual details before reading or teaching the novella.

Title

No initial “The” in Stevenson’s title

  • The first title is Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde.
  • Later references often add “The”, but the shorter title is the form associated with the first edition.
  • The title frames the book as a case: a legal, medical and documentary puzzle rather than a simple monster story.

Narrative

Utterson, documents and delayed disclosure

  • Most of the story is filtered through Utterson’s investigation.
  • The final explanations arrive through Lanyon’s narrative and Jekyll’s written statement.
  • The famous transformation is withheld until late in the book, so the first reading experience depends on mystery and misdirection.

Genre

Gothic, detective fiction and scientific romance

  • The novella uses Gothic settings and secrecy, but also uses a lawyer’s investigation and written evidence.
  • Jekyll’s experiment links the text to late-Victorian scientific speculation.
  • The book is often discussed beside Gothic fiction, early crime fiction and early science fiction.

Text, sources and early publication

The novella’s print history is closely tied to price, format, speed of circulation and the immediate appeal of its central secret.

Writing

Dream, draft and revision

  • Stevenson later connected the story to a dream of transformation.
  • Accounts of the first draft vary; the burnt-manuscript story is part of the book’s legend and should be treated carefully.
  • What is secure is the concentrated period of composition and revision at Bournemouth, where Stevenson was living at Skerryvore.

Sources

Deacon Brodie and double lives

  • Stevenson had earlier written about William Brodie, the respectable Edinburgh figure with a hidden criminal life.
  • The Brodie material predates Jekyll and Hyde and gives a clear precedent for the divided public and private self.
  • The novella turns that social double life into a chemical, bodily and moral division.

Reception

Fast circulation

  • The novella spread quickly in Britain and the United States.
  • Its brevity and strong central premise made it easy to review, discuss, stage and adapt.
  • It soon became one of Stevenson’s most widely read works.

Historical context

The text belongs to late-Victorian debates around science, urban life, professional status, secrecy and the unstable boundary between respectability and transgression.

Victorian London

Respectable streets and hidden doors

  • The novella’s London is not mapped with precise realism, but it relies on recognisable urban contrasts.
  • Jekyll’s house and laboratory create a physical split between respectable frontage and concealed experiment.
  • Soho, fog, night walks and locked rooms help turn the city into a system of partial knowledge.

Science

Experiment and moral responsibility

  • Jekyll’s experiment is presented through retrospective confession rather than open scientific demonstration.
  • Lanyon’s reaction marks the boundary between respectable medical science and Jekyll’s transgressive research.
  • The story does not reject science as a whole; it examines secrecy, ambition and the ethical failure of the experimenter.

Psychology

Identity before modern diagnostic language

  • The novella predates many later clinical categories now loosely attached to it in popular culture.
  • Its language is moral, religious, legal and medical rather than modern psychiatric description.
  • That historical gap matters: “Jekyll and Hyde” became a cultural shorthand after the book, not the term Stevenson inherited.

Chronological record

Dated points for reading, teaching and cross-referencing.

1850

Stevenson born in Edinburgh

  • Robert Louis Stevenson was born in Edinburgh on 13 November 1850.
  • Edinburgh’s divided social geography and the story of Deacon Brodie remain useful contexts for the later novella.

1883

Treasure Island

  • Treasure Island established Stevenson’s popular reputation shortly before Jekyll and Hyde.
  • The two works show the range of his fiction: adventure romance on one side, Gothic psychological mystery on the other.

1885

Composition period

  • Stevenson composed and revised the novella while living at Skerryvore in Bournemouth.
  • The story’s compactness helps explain its strong stage and screen afterlife.

1886

Publication

  • The book appeared in January 1886 in both Britain and the United States.
  • It became one of Stevenson’s best-known works almost immediately.

1894

Stevenson dies in Samoa

  • Stevenson died at Vailima, Samoa, on 3 December 1894.
  • By then Jekyll and Hyde had already become a major stage property.

Stage afterlives and adaptations

The stage tradition quickly changed the novella. Most adaptations reduce Utterson’s investigation, expand Jekyll and Hyde as a dual star role, and add romantic or domestic material not present in the book.

1887-1888

Richard Mansfield and Thomas Russell Sullivan

  • Sullivan’s authorised adaptation opened at the Boston Museum on 9 May 1887.
  • Richard Mansfield played both Jekyll and Hyde.
  • The play added stage material that later films often inherited, including a stronger domestic and romantic framework.

1888

Bandmann and rival stage versions

  • Daniel E. Bandmann worked with John McKinney on a rival adaptation.
  • Rival versions show how quickly the novella became theatre property.
  • They also show how nineteenth-century adaptation could compete directly with authorised performance.

1990 / 1997

Musical Jekyll and Hyde

  • Frank Wildhorn, Leslie Bricusse and Steve Cuden developed the musical Jekyll & Hyde.
  • The Broadway production opened in 1997.
  • The musical tradition moves the story further from Utterson’s documentary mystery and towards spectacle, romance and sung confession.

Screen versions

Film versions often abandon the book’s delayed revelation because cinema can foreground the visible transformation and the actor’s dual performance.

1908 / 1912 / 1920

Silent-film versions

  • Early screen adaptations appeared within the first decades of cinema.
  • The 1908 Selig version is usually identified as an early film adaptation and is now lost.
  • The 1920 Paramount version, directed by John S. Robertson, starred John Barrymore.
  • J. Charles Haydon also released a 1920 version starring Sheldon Lewis.

1931

Rouben Mamoulian and Fredric March

  • Mamoulian’s Paramount film starred Fredric March as Jekyll and Hyde.
  • March won the Academy Award for Best Actor, shared with Wallace Beery for The Champ.
  • The film’s transformation effects helped shape the visual tradition of Hyde on screen.
  • Like many adaptations, it owes as much to stage tradition as to Stevenson’s exact narrative structure.

1941 / 1996 / 2023

Later screen afterlives

  • Victor Fleming’s 1941 MGM film starred Spencer Tracy, Ingrid Bergman and Lana Turner.
  • Mary Reilly reworked the story from the viewpoint of a woman in Jekyll’s household.
  • Doctor Jekyll, released in the 2020s, shows the story still being refitted to new Gothic and psychological frameworks.
