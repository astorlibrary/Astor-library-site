Comedy

As You Like It, William Shakespeare

As You Like It is Shakespeare’s celebrated comedy of exile, disguise, love and transformation.

After Duke Frederick banishes Rosalind, she escapes with Celia and Touchstone. Disguised as Ganymede, Rosalind enters the Forest of Arden, where exiled nobles, shepherds, lovers and fools have formed an alternative world beyond the corruption of court. When Orlando arrives and covers the trees with poems to Rosalind, she uses disguise to test his devotion and direct their courtship herself.

How the book works

Three ways to see the structure before getting lost in the detail.

Disguise

Rosalind writes the scene

As Ganymede, Rosalind can question Orlando, rehearse love and control the pace of revelation. The audience sees several identities at once even when the other characters cannot.

Pastoral

A forest with weather

Arden offers freedom from court, but it is not effortless paradise. Corin works, Audrey tends goats and the exiles feel winter.

Ending

Four marriages and a return

The final scene gathers couples, restores the dukedom and calls upon Hymen. Jaques chooses not to join the celebration, leaving a small space outside its order.

A closer reading

The comedy opens its questions gradually. These three threads are worth following from court to forest and back again.

Court and forest

Arden changes the rules, but not every condition

Duke Frederick’s court is governed by seizure, suspicion and the threat of banishment. Arden appears to offer another social order: identities loosen, rank matters less and conversation can replace command.

Yet the forest contains hunger, cold and labour. Corin’s knowledge of sheep and land is practical rather than decorative, while Jaques can afford to turn other people’s lives into material for melancholy. Shakespeare keeps the pastoral dream in contact with economic fact.

Rosalind

Disguise creates room to think aloud

Ganymede gives Rosalind a freedom that court life denied her. She can teach, tease and test Orlando, turning the conventional suffering lover into a participant in a courtship she largely directs.

The disguise does not simply conceal a fixed self. It allows Rosalind to try out several voices at once: woman, young man, lover, critic of love and stage manager. Performance becomes a way of discovering what desire can survive.

Return

The ending restores more than it explains

Four marriages and the conversion of Duke Frederick create the shape of comic settlement. Property, inheritance and political authority can now return to their expected places.

Jaques declines the dance and leaves for the converted duke. His refusal matters. The play ends in celebration, but it keeps one spectator outside the restored order and lets Rosalind’s epilogue cross the boundary between character, actor and audience one last time.

People to keep in view

The figures who carry the main pressures of the work.

  • RosalindBanished heroine whose performance as Ganymede gives her control over courtship and comic timing.
  • CeliaRosalind’s cousin and chosen companion, who leaves privilege behind to share her exile.
  • OrlandoYoungest son of Sir Rowland de Boys, denied his inheritance and learning how to speak love.
  • TouchstoneCourt fool who tests pastoral idealism through appetite, wordplay and deliberate foolishness.
  • JaquesMelancholy observer drawn to complaint, performance and the spectacle of other people’s lives.
  • Phebe and SilviusA shepherdess and her persistent lover, caught inside Rosalind’s lesson about unequal desire.

    • Questions worth carrying through the text

    Not answers to memorise, but problems the writing keeps alive.

  • GenderWhat can Rosalind say or arrange as Ganymede that she could not attempt openly at court?
  • PastoralWho experiences Arden as liberty, who works there, and who merely visits?
  • PerformanceHow does the play change when audiences remember that the original Rosalind was played by a boy actor?

    • From the wrestling match to the epilogue

    These turning points show Rosalind learning how much can be changed by performance—and what must still be restored by law, inheritance and recognition.

  • Orlando wrestles CharlesThe bout makes physical courage public while family injustice remains private. Rosalind and Orlando are drawn together before either can speak freely about the dispossession shaping their lives.
  • Rosalind is banishedDuke Frederick gives no stable reason for his decision. Celia’s choice to leave with Rosalind turns cousinship into deliberate loyalty and exposes how little security rank provides at court.
  • The exiles meet ArdenDuke Senior praises adversity, yet the cold and the killing of deer complicate his pastoral confidence. Arden is freer than court, but it is still a place where bodies need food and shelter.
  • Ganymede meets OrlandoRosalind does not abandon romance; she gives it a timetable and a test. The invented cure for love lets her hear Orlando’s answers while protecting the knowledge he lacks.
  • Phebe desires GanymedeThe disguise creates consequences Rosalind cannot simply talk away. Phebe’s sudden attraction repeats the imbalance of Silvius’s devotion and forces Rosalind to manage another person’s expectations.
  • Rosalind speaks the epilogueThe settled character steps forward as a performer. Marriage has closed the plot, but the epilogue reopens questions of gender, acting and the audience’s part in believing what it has seen.

    • Arden is not one thing

    Different characters find different forests because they bring different needs and privileges into it.

    Work

    Corin knows the ground

    Corin’s life depends on wages, pasture and the health of a flock owned by someone else. His plain account of labour checks the courtly habit of treating shepherd life as a pleasing literary pose.

    Observation

    Jaques turns life into material

    Jaques watches the wounded deer, Touchstone and the lovers, but observation is not the same as understanding. His melancholy can sharpen a scene, yet it also lets him remain outside the obligations that bind other people.

    Time

    The forest interrupts ordinary sequence

    Love poems hang from trees, meetings happen by chance and conversions arrive with comic speed. Even so, clocks and promises matter to Rosalind. She uses time to distinguish Orlando’s performed passion from the discipline of turning up.
