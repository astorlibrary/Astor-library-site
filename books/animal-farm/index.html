Animal Farm, George Orwell

The animals of Manor Farm drive out Mr Jones and begin a new society in the name of equality. The pigs take control, Napoleon removes Snowball, Squealer explains away every contradiction, Boxer works until he is destroyed, and the Seven Commandments are altered until the revolution has become a new tyranny.

Orwell's fable keeps its language plain and its structure exact. The simplicity is part of the attack: slogans, songs, commandments and official explanations show how political language can train a population to accept what it once rejected.

Records worth knowing

Brief factual points that frame the book before reading, teaching or comparing adaptations.

  • Original title The book was first published as Animal Farm: A Fairy Story.
  • Publication problem Several publishers refused or avoided the manuscript during the wartime alliance with the Soviet Union.
  • Secker & Warburg Fredric Warburg's firm published the first British edition after delays caused by politics and paper shortage.
  • US publication Harcourt, Brace published the first American edition in 1946.
  • Suppressed preface Orwell's proposed preface, later known as The Freedom of the Press, was not printed in the first edition.
  • Political fable The book uses talking animals, repeated slogans and simple story shape to make propaganda visible.
  • Spanish Civil War Orwell's experience of Stalinist conduct in Spain fed directly into his anti-totalitarian argument.
  • Language plot The changing commandments are not decoration. They are the farm's political history being rewritten in public.
  • Boxer matters Boxer's loyalty and disposal turn the labouring body into one of the book's starkest political records.

    • Text and publication history

    Animal Farm reached print through a political bottleneck. Its early history is part of its meaning: a book about censorship, loyalty and truth was itself delayed by wartime literary caution.

    1903-1950

    George Orwell

    • George Orwell was the pen name of Eric Arthur Blair.
    • His earlier work included reportage on poverty, imperial policing, the North of England and the Spanish Civil War.
    • Animal Farm and Nineteen Eighty-Four made his late work central to modern arguments about propaganda, state power and political language.

    1936-37

    Spain and anti-Stalinism

    • Orwell fought in the Spanish Civil War with the POUM militia.
    • His experience of Republican factional conflict and Stalinist suppression shaped Homage to Catalonia.
    • That experience also fed the political anger behind Animal Farm.

    1943-44

    Writing the fable

    • Orwell developed the story during the Second World War.
    • He had recently worked for the BBC and became literary editor of Tribune in 1943.
    • The animal fable allowed him to compress revolutionary history into a narrative that could be read quickly and remembered easily.

    1944

    Manuscript difficulties

    • The manuscript was hard to place because open criticism of Stalin's Soviet Union was politically awkward during the wartime alliance.
    • Faber and Faber rejected it after T. S. Eliot read it for the firm.
    • Jonathan Cape first moved toward publication, then withdrew after advice linked to the Ministry of Information.

    17 August 1945

    First British edition

    • Secker & Warburg published the first British edition.
    • The timing mattered: the European war had ended and the wartime political taboo around criticising the Soviet Union was weakening.
    • The book's shortness, plain style and clear allegorical form helped it move quickly beyond specialist political readerships.

    1972

    The Freedom of the Press

    • Orwell's proposed preface was rediscovered by Ian Angus in 1972 and published in the Times Literary Supplement with an introduction by Bernard Crick.
    • The preface argues that censorship can work without a formal ban when editors, publishers and intellectuals accept a prevailing orthodoxy.
    • It is one of the best companion texts for reading the publication history of Animal Farm.

    Russian Revolution and Stalinism

    The book is not a footnoted history of Russia. It is an allegorical compression of revolution, civil conflict, propaganda, forced labour, rewritten memory and dictatorship.

    1917

    Revolution and founding myth

    • The animals' rebellion is usually read against the Russian Revolution of 1917.
    • Old Major's speech gathers grievance, song and promise into a political origin story.
    • The later regime survives by repeatedly claiming ownership of that origin story.

    Napoleon / Snowball

    Power struggle after revolution

    • Napoleon and Snowball are usually read in relation to Stalin and Trotsky.
    • The debate over the windmill becomes a struggle over authority, planning, violence and credit.
    • Snowball's expulsion gives Napoleon a permanent enemy who can be blamed for every failure.

    Stalinism

    Dictatorship and command

    • Napoleon's rule develops through dogs, surveillance, staged confessions, executions, production targets and revision of the past.
    • The animals' language narrows as the pigs' control expands.
    • The final scene makes the pigs and humans visually indistinguishable to the animals watching through the window.

    Propaganda

    Squealer and official explanation

    • Squealer's job is not merely to lie. He converts memory into something negotiable.
    • He uses statistics, fear of Jones, false alternatives and appeals to sacrifice.
    • His success shows how power can work through explanation as much as through force.

    Language, fable and form

    Orwell's form is plain by design. The book is short, linear and memorable, but the language plot is exact.

    Beast fable

    Simple form, severe argument

    • The talking animals make the story accessible, but the form also strips politics to visible relations of labour, appetite, command and fear.
    • The farm is small enough for every change to be noticed.
    • That compression is why the book works both as school text and political warning.

    Seven Commandments

    Law as rewriteable text

    • The commandments begin as a public constitution.
    • The pigs alter them gradually, usually at night and usually after a breach has already happened.
    • The final version reduces the original revolutionary programme to a single contradiction about equality.

    Songs and slogans

    Memory under pressure

    • Beasts of England gives the rebellion emotional force before it is banned.
    • Later slogans are shorter and more coercive.
    • Language moves from hope to discipline, then to absurd loyalty.

    Narrative voice

    Plainness and irony

    • The narrator often reports impossible contradictions in calm, simple sentences.
    • That restraint lets the reader register the gap between what is said and what is true.
    • The book's violence is sharper because the style rarely announces itself as shocked.

    Character and allegory guide

    These correspondences are useful, but they should not flatten the book into a one-word key. Orwell's characters also work as fable figures and as studies in political behaviour.

  • Mr Jones Usually read against Tsar Nicholas II, but also against negligent ownership and exploitative pre-revolutionary power.
  • Old Major A revolutionary source figure linked to Marx, Lenin and socialist promise rather than one single historical person.
  • Napoleon The Stalin figure: tactical, patient, violent and skilled at turning institutions into personal power.
  • Snowball The Trotsky figure: energetic, theoretical and exiled, then converted into a permanent scapegoat.
  • Squealer Propaganda as character: a voice that makes contradiction sound like policy.
  • Boxer Labour, loyalty and political exploitation in one body. His slogans help the regime that destroys him.
  • Clover Memory, unease and limited literacy. She feels betrayal before she can fully state it.
  • Benjamin Cynical memory. He can read, but his detachment does not save Boxer.
  • The dogs Coercive force trained privately before it appears publicly.

    • Chronological record

    Dated points for teaching, revision and cross-reference.

    1903

    Eric Arthur Blair born

    • Orwell was born in Motihari, Bengal Presidency, British India.
    • His later writing often returns to class, empire, poverty, power and the abuse of language.

    1933

    Down and Out in Paris and London

    • Published under the name George Orwell.
    • The book established his documentary interest in poverty, work and social observation.

    1937

    The Road to Wigan Pier

    • Orwell wrote about poverty, industrial labour and English socialism.
    • The book's second half also records his discomfort with some middle-class socialist habits.

    1938

    Homage to Catalonia

    • Orwell's Spanish Civil War account became a major source for understanding his anti-Stalinism.
    • The book sold poorly at first but gained later authority in Orwell studies.

    1943

    BBC resignation and Tribune

    • Orwell left the BBC and became literary editor of Tribune.
    • The weekly column "As I Please" began in December 1943.

    1945

    Animal Farm published

    • First British publication by Secker & Warburg.
    • Orwell's wife Eileen died earlier that year, before the book appeared.

    1946

    American edition and "Why I Write"

    • Harcourt, Brace published the American edition.
    • In "Why I Write", Orwell identified Animal Farm as the book in which he most consciously joined political and artistic purpose.

    1949

    Nineteen Eighty-Four

    • Orwell's final novel extended his attack on propaganda, surveillance, political language and state control.
    • It is often taught beside Animal Farm, but the two books work through very different forms.

    1950

    Orwell's death

    • Orwell died in London at the age of forty-six.
    • The posthumous handling of his work became part of Cold War publishing, education and adaptation history.

    Stage afterlives

    On stage, Animal Farm usually has to solve the same problem: how to make animals theatrical without reducing the politics to costume.

    1984

    National Theatre, London

    • A theatrical version with music by Richard Peaslee and lyrics by Adrian Mitchell was staged at the National Theatre.
    • Peter Hall directed.
    • The date matters: the production appeared in the year named by Orwell's later novel.

    1995

    Guy Masterson solo version

    • Guy Masterson adapted and performed a one-person version.
    • The solo format makes narration, animal voices and political shift depend on the performer's physical and vocal precision.

    2021

    National Youth Theatre

    • The National Youth Theatre toured a version during pandemic-era performance conditions.
    • The run included outdoor performances at Soulton Hall.
    • The production history shows how the fable can move into non-traditional spaces.

    2022

    Robert Icke adaptation

    • Robert Icke's adaptation opened at Birmingham Rep before touring.
    • Bunny Christie designed the production.
    • Toby Olié designed and directed the puppetry, placing animal bodies and political machinery at the centre of the staging problem.

    2023

    Alexander Raskatov opera

    • Alexander Raskatov's opera premiered at Dutch National Opera in Amsterdam.
    • Opera changes the function of slogans and songs: political language becomes literal musical material.

    Screen versions

    Film versions often reveal what later periods want from Orwell: anti-Stalinist warning, Cold War propaganda, family spectacle, or broad parable about power.

    1954

    Halas and Batchelor animation

    • Directed by John Halas and Joy Batchelor.
    • It was the first British animated feature-length film.
    • The film famously changes the ending so that the animals rise again against the pigs.
    • Later scholarship traced the film's production to covert CIA involvement in the Cold War cultural campaign.

    1999

    Television film

    • Directed by John Stephenson.
    • The film used live animals, animatronics and voice performances.
    • Its ending reflects a post-Soviet moment, with Napoleon's regime collapsing and new human owners arriving.

    2026

    Andy Serkis animated film

    • Directed by Andy Serkis from a screenplay by Nicholas Stoller.
    • The adaptation substantially loosens Orwell's political fable for a modern animated audience.
    • Its reception has been strongly shaped by arguments over tone, audience and fidelity to Orwell's ending.

    Key passages for study

    These passages carry most of the book's classroom and exam weight.

  • Old Major's speech Revolution begins with shared grievance, song and a promise of equality.
  • The Seven Commandments Political ideals become written law, then written law becomes editable property.
  • The Battle of the Cowshed The farm's early heroism creates the memory later used and distorted by the pigs.
  • Snowball's expulsion Napoleon replaces argument with dogs, then turns Snowball into a permanent explanation for failure.
  • The executions Confession and terror enter the farm openly, destroying the idea that animal rule is naturally just.
  • Boxer's removal The regime consumes its most loyal worker and sells the lie through Squealer's explanation.
  • The final commandment Equality survives only as a grammatical trap.
  • The farmhouse window The animals can see power clearly, but too late to separate pigs from men.
  • Changing songs The movement from Beasts of England to official verses charts the loss of revolutionary imagination.

    • Teaching pathways

    Useful routes through the text for study, revision and comparison.

    Language

    Propaganda and rewritten truth

    • Track every alteration to the commandments.
    • Mark Squealer's recurring methods: fear, statistics, denial, false memory and false choice.
    • Compare the animals' memories with the official version of events.

    Character

    Boxer, Clover and Benjamin

    • Read Boxer as labour rather than just innocence.
    • Read Clover as emotional memory under limited literacy.
    • Read Benjamin as intelligence without intervention.

    Context

    Russia and beyond Russia

    • Start with the Russian Revolution and Stalinism, then widen the question.
    • Ask how far the book is about one revolution and how far it is about repeated political behaviour.
    • Keep Orwell's democratic socialism in view so the book is not misread as a simple attack on equality itself.
