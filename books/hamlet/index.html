Hamlet, William Shakespeare

A ghost walks the battlements of Elsinore. Denmark has a new king, a hurried royal marriage, and a prince who cannot accept the official story of his father’s death. When the Ghost tells Hamlet that he was murdered by his own brother, the play becomes a tragedy of revenge, grief, suspicion and delay.

Hamlet must decide whether the voice from the grave is truthful or devilish, whether revenge is justice or damnation, and whether action is possible in a court built on spying, performance and deceit.

At a glance

Key details about the play’s texts, sources and performance history.

  • Three early texts Hamlet survives in Q1, Q2 and the First Folio, and the three witnesses differ significantly.
  • Q1 is short The 1603 First Quarto is much shorter than Q2 and F1 and has a different order in places.
  • Q2 is longest The 1604/5 Second Quarto is the longest early printed version.
  • Folio differs The First Folio includes some material absent from Q2 and omits some Q2 passages.
  • Source tradition The play draws on older Amleth material and revenge-tragedy convention, not one simple source text.
  • Performance text Every staged Hamlet is a cut, arranged or edited version of a play too long for most performance conditions.

    • Text and publication

    Hamlet is a textual case as well as a revenge tragedy. Its early printed history gives editors and performers no single uncontested text.

    1603

    First Quarto: Q1

    • Published by Nicholas Ling and John Trundle and printed by Valentine Simmes.
    • Q1 is much shorter than Q2 and F1.
    • Its title page says the play had been acted in London and at the universities of Cambridge and Oxford.
    • The text is often called a “bad quarto”, but modern scholarship also treats it as evidence of performance, touring and textual circulation.

    1604/5

    Second Quarto: Q2

    • Printed by James Roberts for Nicholas Ling.
    • The title page presents the text as newly enlarged according to the “true and perfect copy”.
    • Q2 is the longest early version of the play.
    • Many modern editions use Q2 as a major base text, while also attending to Folio readings.

    1623

    First Folio

    • The First Folio prints the play as The Tragedie of Hamlet, Prince of Denmarke.
    • F1 differs from Q2 in wording, arrangement and omissions.
    • Editors often discuss Q2 and F1 together because each preserves material the other lacks.
    • The opening on the cold battlements immediately makes Denmark a place of watchfulness, fear and political uncertainty.

    Sources and revenge tragedy

    The play belongs to older revenge narratives and to the Elizabethan revenge-tragedy tradition. Shakespeare turns revenge into a problem of proof, conscience and performance.

    Saxo / Belleforest

    Amleth source tradition

    • The older Amleth story appears in Saxo Grammaticus and later in Belleforest’s Histoires tragiques.
    • The source tradition includes feigned madness, murder within a royal family and a son forced into dangerous concealment.
    • Shakespeare transforms the material by making inner conflict, theatre and theological uncertainty central.

    Revenge tragedy

    Ghost, murder and delay

    • The Ghost demands revenge, but Hamlet has to test what kind of spirit he has encountered.
    • The play repeatedly delays direct revenge through prayer, performance, spying, accidental killing and exile.
    • The delay is not simply indecision: it grows from uncertainty about truth, salvation, damnation and political consequence.

    The Mousetrap

    Theatre as investigation

    • Hamlet uses performance to test Claudius’s conscience.
    • The play-within-the-play turns theatrical illusion into a method of evidence gathering.
    • It also reminds the audience that the whole tragedy is built from watching, acting and interpretation.

    Elsinore, spying and court politics

    Elsinore is not just a castle. It is a system of surveillance: guards watch the battlements, Polonius manages informants, Claudius monitors Hamlet and language itself becomes tactical.

    The battlements

    Ghost and succession crisis

    • The first scene takes place in darkness, cold and military anxiety.
    • The Ghost appears before Hamlet does, so the political world is disturbed before the protagonist enters.
    • The watchmen and Horatio establish the first major question: is the Ghost a true witness, a bad omen, or a dangerous spirit?

    The court

    Performance and public language

    • Claudius opens with fluent public rhetoric, managing mourning, marriage and state business in one political performance.
    • Hamlet resists that performance through grief, black clothing and verbal refusal.
    • The court repeatedly turns private feeling into something to be watched, diagnosed or exploited.

    Polonius

    Spying as habit

    • Polonius sends Reynaldo to spy on Laertes.
    • He uses Ophelia as bait to observe Hamlet.
    • His death behind the arras literalises the danger of a court where listening from concealment has become normal.

    Images, memory and afterlife

    Paintings and theatrical photographs repeatedly try to fix the play’s unstable scenes—grief, hesitation and performance—in a single image.

    1851-52

    Ophelia and Victorian memory

    • Millais turns Ophelia’s reported drowning into a still, intensely detailed image.
    • The painting helped shape later visual memory of Ophelia as beautiful, passive and surrounded by symbolic flowers.
    • It also risks making the violence of the court disappear behind aesthetic beauty.

    Nineteenth century

    Hamlet as actor’s test

    • By the nineteenth century, Hamlet had become one of the defining roles for major actors.
    • Booth’s Hamlet belongs to a long tradition of star interpretations rather than ensemble textual reconstruction.
    • The image records Hamlet as a theatrical identity: black clothing, inwardness, posture and skull-associated memory.

    1899

    Sarah Bernhardt and cross-gender Hamlet

    • Bernhardt played Hamlet on stage and later in an early film record of the duel scene.
    • Her performance is a reminder that Hamlet has not been a fixed male theatrical property in performance history.
    • The role’s afterlife includes questions of gender, voice, body and star image.

    Key dates

    A concise timeline of publication, reception and adaptation.

    c.1599-1601

    Probable composition period

    • The usual dating places Hamlet around the turn of the seventeenth century.
    • The play belongs near Julius Caesar, Twelfth Night and the great tragedies that follow.

    1602

    Stationers’ Register

    • The intended publication of Hamlet was entered in the Stationers’ Register by James Roberts.
    • The entry precedes the 1603 First Quarto.

    1603

    First Quarto

    • Published by Ling and Trundle.
    • Shorter, textually unstable and dramatically revealing.

    1604/5

    Second Quarto

    • Printed by James Roberts for Nicholas Ling.
    • The longest early text.

    1623

    First Folio

    • Printed in the Tragedies section of the First Folio.
    • F1 provides an alternative early witness, not merely a correction of Q2.

    Stage and screen adaptations

    Modern Hamlets often reveal what a period thinks the play is about: psychology, politics, performance, surveillance, youth, melancholy, corruption or media culture.

    1661 onward

    Restoration stage

    • Hamlet returned to the stage after the theatres reopened.
    • Thomas Betterton became one of the major early post-Restoration Hamlets.
    • The role’s actor-centred prestige grows from this long performance tradition.

    1964

    Richard Burton and Broadway

    • Richard Burton played Hamlet in John Gielgud’s Broadway production.
    • The production was filmed through the Electronovision process and released as a screen record.
    • It stands between theatre and film: a commercial attempt to preserve a stage event for cinema audiences.

    1965

    David Warner and Peter Hall at the RSC

    • Peter Hall directed David Warner as Hamlet for the Royal Shakespeare Company.
    • The production became associated with a younger, anti-heroic Hamlet for the 1960s.
    • It helped move the role away from purely noble melancholy towards modern disaffection.

    1948 / 1996 / 2009

    Major screen records

    • Laurence Olivier’s 1948 film won Academy Awards for Best Picture and Best Actor.
    • Kenneth Branagh’s 1996 film presented a complete-text screen version on a large scale.
    • Gregory Doran’s 2009 television film preserved the RSC’s David Tennant / Patrick Stewart production for broadcast.

    2000 / 2014

    Modern transpositions

    • Michael Almereyda’s 2000 film relocates the story into corporate New York and video culture.
    • Vishal Bhardwaj’s Haider reworks the play through Kashmir, militarisation and family politics.
    • These versions show how the revenge plot can become a structure for modern state power and surveillance.
