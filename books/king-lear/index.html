William Shakespeare

King Lear

Lear gives away his kingdom but expects to keep the authority of a king, while Gloucester makes an equally ruinous judgement about his sons. From the love test onwards, the tragedy asks what remains of power, family and human obligation once their public forms have failed.

Astor Library edition. The complete play is accompanied by scene introductions and notes that respect the differences between its quarto and Folio forms. Further essays follow the storm, the two families and the radically different endings found in performance.

Power

Division as catastrophe

Lear gives away the machinery of rule while trying to keep the honours of kingship. The opening scene turns family speech into a public test of obedience.

Text

Quarto against Folio

The early texts differ in title, wording, lineation, scene material and ending emphasis. Modern editors cannot treat the Folio as a small correction of the quarto.

Stage

One play, many Lears

Performance history runs from the King’s Men to Tate’s happy ending, Macready’s restoration, Brook’s austerity, McKellen’s exposed king and Sher’s ritualised pagan court.

King Lear, William Shakespeare

Lear divides his kingdom between Goneril, Regan and Cordelia. When Cordelia refuses to flatter him, he disowns her and gives authority to the daughters whose words best serve the ceremony. The decision removes the bond between power and judgement, and leaves Lear dependent on those he has just empowered.

Alongside Lear’s story, Gloucester is deceived by Edmund into rejecting Edgar. The two plots mirror each other without simply repeating one another: Lear cannot read speech; Gloucester cannot read evidence. Both fathers misjudge the child who remains loyal.

The edition keeps the complete play in view while its notes move between the Lear and Gloucester families. Lear, Cordelia, Goneril, Regan, Gloucester, Edmund, Edgar, Kent and the Fool are read through what they say and do, rather than reduced to fixed character labels.

Longer pieces examine the quarto and Folio differences, the storm, Poor Tom, blindness, old age and kingship. The performance history includes Nahum Tate’s adaptation, whose altered ending makes clear just how uncompromising Shakespeare’s tragedy can be.

A kingdom divided

The two families, two early texts and one disastrous ceremony shape everything that follows.

  • Two early forms King Lear survives through a 1608 quarto, a 1619 quarto reprint and the 1623 First Folio. The quarto and Folio are not interchangeable witnesses.
  • Q1 title The first quarto calls the play a True Chronicle Historie and names Edgar’s assumed identity as Tom of Bedlam on the title page.
  • Folio title The First Folio prints the play as The Tragedie of King Lear, moving it clearly into the Folio’s tragic grouping.
  • Source range The Lear story reaches Shakespeare through chronicle, romance, drama, legend and moral writing, not through one single source.
  • Gloucester plot The blinded father and wronged son material is drawn from Sir Philip Sidney’s Arcadia.
  • Stage interruption Nahum Tate’s 1681 adaptation, with a happy ending and no Fool, held the stage until Macready’s Shakespeare-based restoration in 1838.

    • Text and publication

    King Lear is one of Shakespeare’s major textual problems. The quarto and Folio differ in hundreds of readings, and editorial choice changes what kind of play a reader meets.

    1608

    First Quarto: True Chronicle Historie

    • Printed for Nathaniel Butter.
    • The title page records performance before King James at Whitehall on St Stephen’s night during the Christmas holidays.
    • The same title page links the play to the King’s Men at the Globe on the Bankside.
    • Folger editors note confused verse and prose setting, press corrections and some introduced errors.

    1619

    Second Quarto: Pavier reprint

    • Q2 was mostly a reprint of Q1, though it made corrections, changes and fresh errors.
    • Its true date of 1619 was not recognised until the twentieth century.
    • Because Q2 appears to have influenced parts of the Folio text, it matters even though it is not the first printed witness.

    1623

    First Folio: The Tragedie of King Lear

    • The Folio version contains more than 100 lines absent from Q1.
    • It omits about 300 Q1 lines, including a whole scene, now numbered 4.3 in many editions.
    • Folger records more than 800 word-level differences between Q1 and the Folio.
    • Modern editions may conflate Q1 and F1, use one as base text, or present them separately.

    Source history

    Shakespeare inherited a known story and changed its pressure points. The older Leir tradition gives him family division and restoration; Shakespeare makes suffering, sight and collapse harder to repair.

    Chronicle and legend

    Geoffrey, Holinshed and the Leir story

    The 1587 second edition of Holinshed’s Chronicles tells of ancient Britain’s King Leir and his three daughters, drawing on Geoffrey of Monmouth’s twelfth-century account. In the older story, Cordeilla and the King of the Franks restore Leir to his throne before catastrophe later falls on Cordeilla. Shakespeare removes that secure restoration: Lear’s reconciliation with Cordelia is real, but politically too late.

    1574 and 1590

    Mirror for Magistrates and Spenser

    Mirror for Magistrates offered readers moralised accounts of fallen rulers, while Spenser’s The Faerie Queene includes a version in which Cordelia is hanged. These materials help explain why the play moves between family tragedy, political warning and exemplary history.

    1590

    Sidney’s Arcadia and Gloucester

    Sidney supplies a story of a father deceived by one son, blinded, and aided by the son he has wronged. Shakespeare expands this material into a full counter-plot rather than a brief parallel. Gloucester’s literal blinding forces the play’s repeated language of sight, judgement and recognition into action.

    1603

    Harsnett, Poor Tom and the fiends

    Samuel Harsnett’s A Declaration of Egregious Popish Impostures appeared in 1603. Edgar, disguised as Poor Tom, borrows from it the names of fiends including Obidicut, Hobbididence, Mahu, Modo and Flibbertigibbet. Those names make his disguise sound socially, religiously and theatrically unstable.

    Visual history

    The play’s visual memory often fixes moments the text leaves emotionally unstable: the love test, the storm, Gloucester’s recognition, Lear with Cordelia’s body.

    1793

    Benjamin West: Lear and Cordelia

    • West pictures the reunion rather than the final death scene.
    • The image belongs to the period when Tate’s adaptation still shaped public stage memory of King Lear.
    • Its focus on reconciliation records a softer visual tradition than Shakespeare’s closing scene permits.

    1858

    Poole: the tragic ending restored to sight

    • The V&A record identifies the painting as The Death of Cordelia, painted in 1858.
    • Its subject belongs to the post-Macready stage culture in which Shakespeare’s tragic ending had returned.
    • The painting turns the last scene into a single tableau of grief, recognition and irreversible loss.

    Heath scene

    The storm as stage image

    • The storm is not background. It externalises Lear’s loss of shelter, office and mental security.
    • Visual artists repeatedly return to Act 3 because it allows the king to be shown without a working kingdom around him.
    • The Fool’s presence keeps the scene from becoming only natural spectacle.

    Performance timeline

    Dated points that show how the play moved from court, Globe and print to adaptation, restoration and modern Shakespearean production.

    1594 / 1605

    Earlier King Leir

    • The anonymous True Chronicle History of King Leir had been performed in 1594 and printed in 1605.
    • RSC notes that some scholars believe Shakespeare knew the printed form closely.
    • The earlier play is more overtly Christian and less bleak in its movement toward restoration.

    26 December 1606

    Court performance

    • The title page and register evidence point to performance before King James at Whitehall on St Stephen’s night.
    • The performing company was the King’s Men.
    • Richard Burbage is identified in RSC stage-history material as the early Lear for whom the role was written.

    1681-1838

    Nahum Tate’s History of King Lear

    • Tate removed the Fool, added a Cordelia-Edgar love plot and gave the play a happy ending.
    • For more than a century, this was the dominant stage form of the story.
    • David Garrick played Lear in Tate’s version during the eighteenth century.

    1838

    Macready and the Fool restored

    • William Charles Macready returned to a shortened Shakespeare-based text.
    • Priscilla Horton played the restored Fool.
    • Victorian staging still often softened or displaced the full brutality of Gloucester’s blinding.

    1940

    John Gielgud at the Old Vic

    • Gielgud first played Lear in 1931 and returned to the role across his career.
    • His 1940 Old Vic production was directed by Harley Granville Barker and Lewis Casson.
    • Barker’s influence helped support a return to fluid staging rather than heavy scenic reconstruction.

    1968

    Trevor Nunn’s RSC Lear

    • The archive image records the opening rupture as a stage arrangement, not only a speech scene.
    • The production is part of a later twentieth-century return to the physical and political harshness of the play.

    2007

    Trevor Nunn and Ian McKellen

    • RSC describes the production as Ruritania-inspired, stormy and built around a thrust stage.
    • McKellen played Lear as father, violent master and vulnerable old man.
    • The production included an on-stage hanging of the Fool.

    2016 / 2018

    Gregory Doran and Antony Sher

    • Doran’s production played the Royal Shakespeare Theatre and the Barbican in 2016 and was broadcast to cinemas worldwide.
    • Niki Turner’s design emphasised a pagan setting and a copper-disc sun image.
    • The production was revived in 2018 for New York and Stratford, with Sher returning as Lear.

    Stage and screen adaptations

    Modern afterlives usually choose between political scale, family intimacy, ritual austerity and filmed theatre.

  • Tate’s ending The 1681 adaptation preserves Lear and Gloucester, marries Cordelia to Edgar, removes the Fool and turns the ending toward moral reward.
  • Macready’s return The 1838 restoration did not produce a full modern scholarly text, but it did return the Fool and the tragic ending to public stage memory.
  • Kathryn Hunter Hunter played Lear at Leicester Haymarket in 1997, directed by Helena Kaut-Howson, with the action framed through a nursing-home hallucination.
  • Kozintsev, 1970 Grigori Kozintsev directed a Russian-language film in a Boris Pasternak translation.
  • Brook, 1971 Peter Brook filmed a stylised version based on his 1962 Stratford production, with Paul Scofield again as Lear.
  • BBC, 1982 Michael Hordern played Lear for the BBC Television Shakespeare, directed by Jonathan Miller.

    • Language and interpretation

    The play is especially teachable because its repeated words are also dramatic mechanisms. Speech creates power, destroys trust and fails at recognition.

  • Nothing Cordelia’s “nothing” exposes the gap between honest language and performative flattery.
  • Seeing Lear and Gloucester repeatedly misread people. Gloucester’s physical blinding makes literal what the play has already staged morally.
  • Animals Animal language reduces court, family and state to predation, appetite and exposure.
  • Clothing Robes, rags and nakedness mark shifts in status. The storm strips rank from the king.
  • Law Lear’s imagined trial of Goneril and Regan turns judgement into breakdown.
  • Endurance The ending refuses easy consolation. Recognition arrives, but not in time to save Cordelia or restore the kingdom as it was.
