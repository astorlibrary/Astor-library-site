The Taming of the Shrew, William Shakespeare

In Padua, Baptista Minola refuses to allow Bianca to marry until a husband is found for her elder sister, Katherina. Petruchio arrives from Verona looking for a wealthy wife and agrees to marry Katherina for her dowry. Around their conflict, Bianca’s suitors use disguise, substituted identities and bargaining to pursue marriages of their own.

The play is one of Shakespeare’s most performed comedies and one of the hardest to stage without argument. Its comedy depends on courtship, money, household rule and public performance; its modern difficulty lies in Petruchio’s treatment of Katherina and the final speech on obedience. This page treats the play as a textual object, a stage problem and a record of changing theatrical values.

Records worth knowing

Core records for teaching, production context and textual discussion.

Text

First Folio text, later quarto

  • The Taming of the Shrew, in the form usually read today, first appears in the 1623 First Folio.
  • The Folio prints the play among the comedies.
  • A later quarto, A Wittie and Pleasant comedie called The Taming of the Shrew, appeared in 1631 and is based on the Folio text.
  • The absence of a pre-1623 quarto for Shakespeare’s Shrew makes the relationship with A Shrew central to the play’s textual history.

The A Shrew question

A similar play, different text

  • A Pleasant Conceited Historie, called The taming of a Shrew was printed in quarto in 1594, then again in 1596 and 1607.
  • It has a closely related plot but different names, structure and wording.
  • Scholars have treated it in several ways: source, memorial reconstruction, adaptation, early draft or evidence of a lost intermediate version.
  • Editors normally print the Folio Shrew, not the 1594 A Shrew, though the latter affects notes on Christopher Sly and the play’s ending.

Sources

Italian comedy and English translation

  • The Bianca subplot draws on Italian intrigue comedy.
  • George Gascoigne’s Supposes, performed in 1566 and printed in 1573, translated and adapted Ariosto’s I Suppositi.
  • Disguise, mistaken identity, substituted fathers and legal bargaining all belong to this comic source tradition.
  • The Katherina and Petruchio plot has older taming-story analogues, but no single direct source explains Shakespeare’s version.

Structure

The unfinished Christopher Sly frame

  • The play opens with Christopher Sly, a drunken tinker, tricked into believing that he is a lord.
  • The Padua story is presented as entertainment staged for Sly.
  • Unlike the 1594 A Shrew, Shakespeare’s Folio text does not return fully to Sly at the end.
  • Modern productions often decide whether to cut the Induction, restore a Sly ending from A Shrew, or make the frame a visible comment on gender and spectatorship.

Text, sources and first printing

The play’s early history is a problem of print evidence rather than a neat publication story.

1623

First Folio: the first secure printed text

  • The Folio title is The Taming of the Shrew.
  • The text opens with the Induction before moving to Baptista, Katherina, Bianca, Gremio, Hortensio, Lucentio and Tranio in Padua.
  • The Folio’s division between Sly’s frame and the Padua action has shaped modern arguments about whether the play undercuts or reinforces the taming plot.
  • Teachers should separate two questions: what the Folio text prints, and what productions choose to make of the incomplete frame.

1566 / 1573

Gascoigne’s Supposes

  • Gascoigne’s Supposes is one of the clearest source points for the Bianca plot.
  • The title refers to “supposes”, meaning assumptions, substitutions and mistaken identities.
  • Shakespeare turns this intrigue machinery into the Lucentio-Tranio-Cambio plot.
  • The source link is useful because the Bianca plot is not a minor distraction: it makes marriage a market of education, dowry, status and false documents.

1594 / 1596 / 1607

A Shrew in quarto

  • The 1594 quarto calls the play A Pleasant Conceited Historie, called The taming of a Shrew.
  • Names differ: for example, the equivalent of Katherina is usually called Kate, while the Bianca plot is reshaped with different names and details.
  • The Sly frame returns later in A Shrew, which is why some eighteenth-century editors imported parts of it into Shakespeare’s play.
  • Modern scholarly editions usually treat the relationship as uncertain and explain competing theories rather than claiming a settled answer.

Historical context

Facts that matter without turning the page into a generic theme guide.

Marriage

Dowry, household rule and public contract

  • Baptista’s household is organised around daughters, suitors and property negotiation.
  • Bianca’s marriage cannot proceed until Katherina’s does; this makes Katherina a social and financial obstacle before Petruchio even meets her.
  • Petruchio’s first description of marriage is openly economic: he has come to “wive it wealthily in Padua”.
  • The play’s repeated references to dowry, jointure, tutoring and paternal consent keep the marriage plot inside public bargaining rather than private romance alone.

Language

Names, roles and performance

  • Petruchio performs authority by renaming, contradiction and theatrical control.
  • Lucentio wins Bianca by changing roles with Tranio; Hortensio disguises himself as Litio; a Pedant is recruited to impersonate Vincentio.
  • Nearly every courtship depends on acting.
  • This is why modern productions often treat the play as a debate about performance itself: who acts, who watches, who controls the terms of the scene.

Controversy

The final speech cannot be ignored

  • Katherina’s final speech on wifely obedience is the play’s most contested passage.
  • Directors have played it straight, ironically, conspiratorially, angrily, sadly, or as a public script imposed by the wager scene.
  • The speech changes meaning according to staging: tone, physical distance, eye contact, reactions from Bianca and the Widow, and Petruchio’s response all matter.
  • A strong production normally makes the audience notice that the speech is being performed in front of men who have just made a bet on women’s conduct.

Image-bearing records

Visual material shows how often the play has been reduced to two bodies in conflict: Katherina resisting, Petruchio controlling, or both performing roles for an audience.

1898

Edward Robert Hughes: Katherina isolated

  • Hughes’s image presents Katherina apart from the bustle of the marriage market.
  • The picture belongs to a late-Victorian visual tradition that turns Shakespearean women into single, readable emotional figures.
  • For classroom use, it is useful because it removes Petruchio and asks what “shrewishness” looks like when no man is on the canvas.

1832

Charles Robert Leslie: domestic theatre

  • Leslie’s painting stages the couple as a scene of comic confrontation.
  • The image sits near the long stage dominance of Garrick’s shortened adaptation Catharine and Petruchio.
  • It is a useful reminder that many nineteenth-century spectators knew the play through adaptation, not through the full Folio structure.

1929

Pickford and Fairbanks on film

  • Sam Taylor’s film starred Mary Pickford as Katherina and Douglas Fairbanks as Petruchio.
  • It is widely cited as the first sound-film adaptation of Shakespeare.
  • Pickford’s Katherina famously winks during the final speech, a screen solution to the problem of obedience.

Chronological production history

Selected records showing how the play has repeatedly been adapted, shortened, reframed and argued with.

c.1609-10

Fletcher answers Shakespeare: The Woman’s Prize

  • John Fletcher’s The Woman’s Prize, or The Tamer Tamed is a sequel or reply to Shakespeare’s play.
  • Petruchio remarries after Katherina’s death and is resisted by his second wife, Maria.
  • The play turns the taming structure back against Petruchio.
  • Its early existence proves that the sexual politics of Shrew were already open to theatrical reply in the Jacobean period.

1667

John Lacy: Sauny the Scot

  • Lacy’s Restoration adaptation reworked Shakespeare’s play in prose.
  • Grumio was expanded into Sauny, a Scottish servant role played by Lacy himself.
  • The adaptation made Petruchio harsher and simplified the action for Restoration comic taste.
  • It also helped match Shakespeare’s play to Fletcher’s more popular sequel.

1754

David Garrick: Catharine and Petruchio

  • Garrick condensed the play to three acts and removed the Christopher Sly frame.
  • The Bianca subplot was drastically reduced or removed, depending on the version performed.
  • For much of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, Garrick’s version was performed more often than Shakespeare’s full text.
  • This is a key record for students: “Shakespeare in performance” often means Shakespeare rewritten.

1844

Return of Shakespeare’s fuller text in Britain

  • By the mid-nineteenth century, theatres began returning to versions closer to Shakespeare’s full play.
  • The shift belongs to a wider movement toward treating Shakespeare’s texts as literary and national documents rather than raw material for adaptation.
  • Even then, cuts and performance traditions remained strong.

1929

Sam Taylor film: Mary Pickford and Douglas Fairbanks

  • Pickford produced and starred as Katherina.
  • Fairbanks played Petruchio.
  • The film uses a reduced text and makes the final speech readable through gesture rather than textual change.
  • The wink became one of the best-known screen interventions in the play’s afterlife.

1948 / 1953

Kiss Me, Kate

  • Cole Porter’s Broadway musical opened in 1948.
  • The book by Bella and Samuel Spewack frames Shrew through backstage conflict between actors performing the play.
  • The original production starred Alfred Drake and Patricia Morison.
  • The 1953 MGM film carried the musical into Hollywood screen history.

1967

Zeffirelli film: Elizabeth Taylor and Richard Burton

  • Franco Zeffirelli directed the 1967 film.
  • Elizabeth Taylor played Katherina and Richard Burton played Petruchio.
  • The film foregrounds colour, costume, bustle and physical comedy.
  • It remains one of the best-known screen versions of the play.

1978

Michael Bogdanov at the RSC

  • Bogdanov’s RSC production starred Paola Dionisotti as Katherina and Jonathan Pryce as Petruchio.
  • The production used Christopher Sly aggressively: Pryce’s Sly disrupted the auditorium before the Padua story began.
  • It treated the taming plot as a social and theatrical problem rather than a harmless farce.
  • Bogdanov won the 1979 Olivier Award for Director of the Year for the production.

1980

BBC Television Shakespeare

  • Jonathan Miller directed the BBC version.
  • John Cleese played Petruchio and Sarah Badel played Katherina.
  • The production presented Petruchio less as a swaggering bully and more as a controlled social performer.
  • Its studio realism remains useful for comparing stage and screen approaches to the taming scenes.

1999

10 Things I Hate About You

  • Gil Junger’s film relocates the plot to an American high school.
  • Julia Stiles plays Kat Stratford; Heath Ledger plays Patrick Verona.
  • Bianca’s dating restriction becomes the equivalent of Baptista’s marriage rule.
  • The adaptation removes the obedience ending and turns the story into a late-1990s teen romance.

2003

RSC pairing with Fletcher

  • Gregory Doran’s RSC work paired The Taming of the Shrew with Fletcher’s The Tamer Tamed.
  • Alexandra Gilbreath and Jasper Britton played Katherina and Petruchio in Shrew.
  • The pairing made the response-play visible instead of leaving it as a footnote.
  • It gave audiences a historical way to see the play argued with from inside early modern drama itself.

2012

Shakespeare’s Globe: Toby Frow

  • Toby Frow directed the Globe production.
  • Samantha Spiro played Katherina and Simon Paisley Day played Petruchio.
  • The production restored a Christopher Sly disturbance before moving into the Padua action.
  • Reviews noted the production’s broad comedy and its refusal to soften the final speech heavily through irony.

2019

RSC gender-flipped production

  • Justin Audibert directed a reimagined RSC production set in a matriarchal version of Elizabethan England.
  • Claire Price played Petruchia and Joseph Arkley played Katherine.
  • The production changed the gendered power structure rather than leaving the original hierarchy intact.
  • It toured after the Stratford run and became one of the clearest recent examples of staging the play through structural reversal.

Stage afterlives and adaptations

The play has often survived by being rewritten.

Sequel

The Woman’s Prize, or The Tamer Tamed

  • Fletcher’s play is the major early modern answer to Shakespeare’s comedy.
  • It gives Petruchio a second wife, Maria, who organises resistance.
  • For modern teaching, it helps show that discomfort with Petruchio’s victory is not only a modern response.

Restoration and eighteenth century

Adaptation as normal practice

  • Sauny the Scot and Catharine and Petruchio show how freely Shakespeare was rewritten for later stages.
  • These adaptations reduce the textual complexity and sharpen the taming action.
  • They also helped create some “traditional” expectations that are not simply Shakespeare’s own.

Musical theatre

Kiss Me, Kate

  • Porter’s musical places actors’ offstage tensions beside the onstage Shrew plot.
  • That backstage frame is a twentieth-century equivalent of the Christopher Sly device: the audience watches people performing roles while private conflict leaks through.
  • The musical’s success made Shrew one of Shakespeare’s most recognisable sources for popular musical comedy.

Screen versions

Screen adaptations often solve the play’s final problem by changing genre.

1929

The Taming of the Shrew

  • Director: Sam Taylor.
  • Main cast: Mary Pickford as Katherina; Douglas Fairbanks as Petruchio.
  • Key record: early sound Shakespeare and the wink during Katherina’s final speech.

1967

The Taming of the Shrew

  • Director: Franco Zeffirelli.
  • Main cast: Elizabeth Taylor as Katherina; Richard Burton as Petruchio.
  • Production style: high-colour Italianate bustle, strong physical comedy and star casting.

1980

BBC Television Shakespeare

  • Director: Jonathan Miller.
  • Main cast: Sarah Badel as Katherina; John Cleese as Petruchio.
  • Production style: restrained, social, studio-based, with Petruchio’s method treated as controlled performance.

1999

10 Things I Hate About You

  • Director: Gil Junger.
  • Main cast: Julia Stiles, Heath Ledger, Larisa Oleynik and Joseph Gordon-Levitt.
  • Adaptation method: late-1990s American high school, with dating rules replacing marriage contracts.
