Verona

Four days, two houses, one failed message.

The play compresses inherited romance material into a tight civic disaster. Public brawls, private vows, family bargaining, banishment, plague quarantine and late arrival all press on the lovers. Their haste is fatal because the feud and the adults around them turn every delay, secret and mistake into danger.

Romeo and Juliet, William Shakespeare

In Verona, the Montagues and Capulets are locked in a feud nobody can end. Romeo meets Juliet at a Capulet feast, and the two fall in love before discovering they belong to enemy houses. Their secret marriage offers a possible escape, but Tybalt’s anger, Romeo’s banishment, Friar Lawrence’s failed message and Juliet’s desperate plan lead them towards the tomb.

Scene summaries and notes run beside the complete play rather than getting in its way. They follow the pressure of the feud through the quartos, the sonnet language, the speed of the action and the uneasy mixture of youthful desire, family power, medicine, fate and civic violence.

Form

Love speaks in public forms

The first shared speech between Romeo and Juliet is a sonnet. Their language begins as inherited courtship ritual and then becomes urgent, private, improvised and dangerous.

Text

A quarto problem

Q1 is short and unstable. Q2 is longer and became the basis for later quartos and the Folio line. Modern readers meet a play shaped by early print history.

Stage

Always a play about bodies

Directors must decide what Verona feels like: Renaissance city, divided modern state, schoolyard, street gang, religious society or overheated family machine.

At a glance

The dates, early texts and older story behind Shakespeare's tragedy.

  • Title page claim The 1597 quarto says the play had been often performed publicly and with applause by the Lord Hunsdon’s Servants.
  • Bad quarto label Q1 has long been classed among Shakespeare’s so-called bad quartos, though modern accounts treat the category with more caution than older editorial summaries did.
  • Q2 expansion The 1599 quarto is much longer than Q1 and became the main basis for later quarto and Folio transmission.
  • Source line Shakespeare’s main English source was Arthur Brooke’s 1562 poem The Tragical History of Romeus and Juliet.
  • Prose analogue William Painter printed a prose version in The Palace of Pleasure in 1567.
  • Italian chain The story runs through Luigi da Porto, Matteo Bandello and Pierre Boaistuau before Brooke and Painter.
  • Compressed time Brooke’s narrative unfolds over months; Shakespeare compresses the action into a few days.
  • New characters and pressure Shakespeare gives stronger theatrical life to Mercutio, Paris, the Nurse, Tybalt and the civil world around the lovers.
  • Stationers’ Register The play was not entered until 1607, after the first quartos had already appeared.
  • Afterlife range The play has produced operas, ballets, films, musicals, paintings, school editions and modern urban adaptations.

    • Text and publication

    Romeo and Juliet reached print well before the First Folio. Differences between the short 1597 Quarto and the fuller 1599 text still shape modern editions.

    1597

    First Quarto: An Excellent conceited Tragedie

    • Printed by John Danter.
    • The title page names the Lord Hunsdon’s Servants.
    • It is much shorter than Q2.
    • It preserves evidence of performance, abbreviation and textual disturbance.

    1599

    Second Quarto: corrected, augmented, amended

    • Printed by Thomas Creede for Cuthbert Burby.
    • The title page presents the text as newly corrected, augmented and amended.
    • Q2 is the fuller early text and became the major basis for later printed forms.
    • Editorial discussion often treats Q2 as closer to Shakespeare’s manuscript or pre-performance copy than Q1.

    1609 and 1622

    Later quartos

    • The play was reprinted in 1609 and again in early seventeenth-century quartos.
    • Later quartos continued the Q2 line of transmission.
    • These printings helped stabilise the form inherited by early readers.

    1623

    First Folio

    • The Folio prints the play as The Tragedie of Romeo and Juliet.
    • The Folio text is based chiefly on the later quarto tradition.
    • Folio placement confirms the play’s place among Shakespeare’s tragedies while preserving a text whose earliest forms were already layered.

    Sources and story history

    Shakespeare transformed a familiar tale of feuding families and feigned death by compressing it into an urgent drama of heat, misreading and late arrival.

    Classical pattern

    Pyramus and Thisbe

    • Ovid’s story of divided lovers, family obstruction, mistaken death and double suicide forms an ancient analogue.
    • Shakespeare also parodies Pyramus and Thisbe in A Midsummer Night’s Dream, written near the same period.
    • The shared pattern helps place Romeo and Juliet inside a long tradition of fatal love stories.

    Italian tale

    Da Porto, Bandello and Boaistuau

    • Luigi da Porto shaped an early version of the Romeo and Giulietta story in Italian prose.
    • Matteo Bandello retold the story in his Novelle.
    • Pierre Boaistuau adapted Bandello into French in Histoires tragiques.

    1562

    Arthur Brooke: Romeus and Juliet

    • Brooke’s poem is Shakespeare’s chief English source.
    • It supplies the broad plot, the lovers, the feud, the Friar, the Nurse, banishment, sleeping potion and tomb.
    • Shakespeare shortens the timescale and increases dramatic pressure.

    1567

    William Painter: Palace of Pleasure

    • Painter printed a prose version of the story in his collection of translated tales.
    • The collection reflects the popularity of Italian novelle in Elizabethan reading and theatre culture.
    • Shakespeare’s play belongs to that same traffic between Italian story, English prose, poetry and commercial drama.

    Language and structure

    Throughout the play, poetic form is strained by haste, violence and social pressure.

    Prologue

    Sonnet as plot machinery

    • The Chorus announces family feud, doomed love and death before the action begins.
    • The sonnet form gives the tragedy a closed shape from the start.
    • Students can track how foreknowledge changes audience response to haste, jokes and accidents.

    Act 1, Scene 5

    The shared sonnet

    • Romeo and Juliet’s first exchange builds a sonnet through dialogue.
    • Religious language turns touch into courtship ritual.
    • The form briefly creates order inside the Capulet feast.

    Mercutio

    Comedy and danger

    • Mercutio’s language moves through wit, fantasy, sexual joking and provocation.
    • His death changes the play’s temperature.
    • The line between play and violence collapses in the street.

    Juliet

    Time, speech and decision

    • Juliet often has to translate adult plans into immediate action.
    • Her soliloquies record fear, speed and self-command.
    • The potion scene is central for teaching agency and isolation.

    Productions in pictures

    Design transforms Verona: arches, ladders, cars, tombs and divided public spaces give the feud a different social world in every production.

    1941

    First meeting under painted arches

    • The RSC archive page identifies this image as Romeo and Juliet’s first meeting in the 1941 production.
    • The lovers are framed by a formal Renaissance-style stage world.
    • The image fixes the Capulet feast as a visual threshold between social performance and private recognition.

    1947

    Peter Brook’s post-war Romeo and Juliet

    • RSC teaching material notes that Brook’s production emphasised the youth of the lovers.
    • The balcony remains a height difference and a social barrier.
    • The photograph records the scene as one of vertical distance: Juliet above, Romeo exposed below.

    1961

    The tomb as final stage picture

    • RSC material identifies this image as the end of the 1961 production.
    • The tomb scene gathers private love, public feud and civic judgement into one tableau.
    • Productions often decide whether the final reconciliation feels credible, formal or hollow.

    1986

    Michael Bogdanov’s modern Verona

    • The RSC describes the 1986 Verona as a modern world influenced by 1980s yuppie culture.
    • Designer Chris Dyer used marble and chrome.
    • The red car shifts the feud from inherited Renaissance ritual to contemporary status, wealth and street display.

    2004

    Balcony, obstacle and touch

    • RSC teaching material notes that the balcony can be any raised place, not only a literal balcony.
    • Height, distance and access change how the relationship develops physically.
    • Production images across decades show the scene as a design problem as much as a famous speech sequence.

    1996

    Friar Laurence and the failed adult plan

    • The Friar is the third largest role after Romeo and Juliet.
    • His cell gives Romeo an alternative household and an alternative father figure.
    • The production record helps students track how consolation becomes intervention, and intervention becomes fatal timing.

    Music, dance and adaptation

    The play's swift sequence of feud, meeting, marriage, duel, exile, potion and tomb has proved especially powerful in music and dance.

    1748David Garrick restored more of Shakespeare’s text than earlier adaptations but rewrote and adjusted the ending for the eighteenth-century stage.
    1867Gounod’s opera Roméo et Juliette gave the lovers extended lyric duets and made the love plot the main musical engine.
    1935Prokofiev composed music for the ballet Romeo and Juliet, now one of the best-known ballet treatments of Shakespeare.
    1957West Side Story moved the feud into New York gang conflict, with music by Leonard Bernstein and lyrics by Stephen Sondheim.
    1965Kenneth MacMillan’s Royal Ballet version, set to Prokofiev, became a major modern dance afterlife for the play.

    Screen versions

    Film versions usually answer one question before anything else: what kind of youth culture will stand in for Verona?

    1936

    George Cukor

    • MGM feature film.
    • Leslie Howard played Romeo and Norma Shearer played Juliet.
    • The film framed the tragedy through studio-era spectacle and prestige Shakespeare.

    1954

    Renato Castellani

    • Italian-British film version.
    • Used Italian settings and an art-historical visual style.
    • Won the Golden Lion at Venice.

    1968

    Franco Zeffirelli

    • Leonard Whiting and Olivia Hussey were cast as young leads.
    • Zeffirelli’s earlier 1960 Old Vic production helped shape the film.
    • The film became one of the best-known classroom screen versions.

    1996

    Baz Luhrmann: Romeo + Juliet

    • Leonardo DiCaprio and Claire Danes played the lovers.
    • The film keeps Shakespeare’s language inside a violent modern Verona Beach.
    • Guns, television, religious iconography, cars and pop music remake the feud as a media-age spectacle.

    2013 and after

    Continuing screen life

    • Later films continue to alternate between period settings and contemporary updates.
    • The story also circulates through musicals, teen films and loose adaptations.
    • Most versions preserve the crossed houses, secret marriage and failed message even when the setting changes completely.
