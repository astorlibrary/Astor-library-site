Richard III, William Shakespeare

Richard, Duke of Gloucester, tells the audience that he has decided to "prove a villain", then makes us watch him clear a path to the throne. Clarence is sent to the Tower, Lady Anne is won beside the coffin of the king Richard helped murder, the young princes are imprisoned and yesterday's allies become today's obstacles. Politics turns into theatre, and theatre into tyranny.

Scene summaries and concise notes set the complete play beside its quarto and Folio texts, Tudor histories and long performance life. They make it easier to see how Richard recruits witnesses, rewrites events and draws the audience into his confidence without flattening the force of his language.

At a glance

The early texts, sources and political shape of the play.

  • Quarto success The play was printed repeatedly before the 1623 Folio, evidence of strong early demand.
  • Q1 title The 1597 quarto title advertises Clarence, the princes, usurpation and Richard's deserved death.
  • Q2 attribution The 1598 quarto adds Shakespeare's name to the title page.
  • Folio difference The Folio text is not simply a corrected reprint; it differs substantially from the quarto tradition.
  • History sequence The play completes the first tetralogy after the three Henry VI plays.
  • Cibber afterlife Colley Cibber's 1699 adaptation dominated the stage for nearly two centuries.

    • Text and publication

    Richard III has a busy early print history: quarto publication in 1597, repeated reprints, and a First Folio text in 1623. Modern editions must decide how to handle that record.

    1597

    First quarto

    • The first quarto was printed by Valentine Simmes for Andrew Wise.
    • The title page describes Richard's plots against Clarence, the murder of the princes, his usurpation and his death.
    • It says the play had been acted by the Lord Chamberlain's Men.
    • The title page does not name Shakespeare.

    1598-1634

    Further quartos

    • Q2 appeared in 1598 and names Shakespeare.
    • Further quartos appeared in 1602, 1605, 1612, 1622, 1629 and 1634.
    • The sequence shows continuing demand for the play in print.
    • Quarto frequency also makes Richard III one of the strongest examples of Shakespearean stage success becoming print success.

    1623

    First Folio

    • The Folio printed the play among Shakespeare's histories.
    • The Folio text is longer than the quarto and contains many readings absent from Q1.
    • Some passages in the quarto are absent from the Folio.
    • The relationship between quarto and Folio is an editorial problem, not a simple earlier/later correction.

    Sources and historical shaping

    Shakespeare inherits a Richard already shaped by hostile Tudor history, then turns that political memory into theatre.

    Thomas More

    The hostile Richard tradition

    • Thomas More's account helped establish the image of Richard as calculating, deformed, theatrical and murderous.
    • More's work did not circulate as a modern biography; it became source material through later chronicle history.
    • Shakespeare inherits a Richard already formed by moral and political hostility.

    Holinshed

    Chronicle source

    • Shakespeare used Raphael Holinshed's Chronicles, especially the 1587 edition.
    • Holinshed gathers material from earlier writers, including More, Hall and others.
    • The play converts chronicle sequence into a dramatic arc of persuasion, murder, coronation and collapse.

    Hall and Tudor memory

    History written after the victory

    • Edward Hall's chronicle belongs to the Tudor view of the Wars of the Roses.
    • Because Richmond becomes Henry VII, the ending carries dynastic justification as well as dramatic victory.
    • The play's final settlement is therefore historical, theatrical and ideological at once.

    Earlier Richard drama

    The True Tragedy of Richard III

    • An anonymous play on Richard III was printed in 1594.
    • It shows that Richard's story was already stage material before Shakespeare's quarto appeared.
    • Shakespeare's version outlived rival treatments because it made Richard's direct address and political acting central.

    Political and dramatic context

    The play treats power as performance: Richard scripts scenes, recruits witnesses and turns public language into a weapon.

    Opening soliloquy

    Self-fashioning before action

    • Richard begins by telling the audience who he intends to become.
    • His first speech turns physical description, resentment, performance and policy into one theatrical contract.
    • Audience complicity is part of the play's danger: we know his plan before many characters do.

    Lady Anne

    Wooing beside the coffin

    • Richard's courtship of Anne is one of Shakespeare's most disturbing persuasion scenes.
    • It takes place beside the corpse of Henry VI, whom Richard helped kill.
    • The scene turns grief, shock, sexual pressure and theatrical bravado into political momentum.

    The princes

    Dynasty as vulnerability

    • The young princes are central to the play's moral horror even when they are absent from view.
    • The Tower becomes a space of royal protection turned imprisonment.
    • The murder of the princes marks Richard's movement from intrigue into the public memory of tyranny.

    Buckingham

    The ally who becomes expendable

    • Buckingham helps turn Richard's ambition into political theatre.
    • The public scene of reluctant kingship depends on staged humility, citizens, clergy and managed silence.
    • Once Buckingham hesitates, Richard treats him as another obstacle.

    Characters

    Performance, witness, prophecy and family memory shape the principal roles.

    Richard

    Actor, strategist, tyrant

    • Richard writes himself as villain before the plot fully begins.
    • He thrives in direct address, improvisation and manipulation.
    • His public mask becomes less effective as suspicion hardens and ghosts replace applause.

    Queen Margaret

    Curse and memory

    • Margaret carries the memory of the previous civil wars into the play.
    • Her curses become a way of measuring political debt.
    • Productions decide whether to make her a prophetic outsider, a damaged survivor or both.

    Clarence

    Dream, guilt and execution

    • Clarence's Tower scene slows the early plot into dream and conscience.
    • The murderers' debate turns hired killing into dark moral comedy.
    • The malmsey-butt detail became one of the play's best-known murder images.

    Elizabeth

    Resistance after loss

    • Queen Elizabeth begins as a political target and becomes one of Richard's hardest opponents.
    • The later scene in which Richard tries to secure her daughter repeats the wooing structure under greater suspicion.
    • Her grief is political, maternal and dynastic.

    Buckingham

    Political broker

    • Buckingham is Richard's chief collaborator in the middle movement of the play.
    • He helps choreograph public consent.
    • His fall shows how quickly Richard consumes the men who make him king.

    Richmond

    Ending and Tudor settlement

    • Richmond enters late compared with Richard.
    • His function is less psychological than historical: he names peace after civil wounds.
    • Modern productions often have to work hard to make his victory feel dramatically alive.

    On stage

    Successive performers have reworked the role's mixture of disability, charisma, villainy and political ambition.

    c. 1600

    Burbage and early performance memory

    • Richard Burbage is strongly associated with the early role of Richard.
    • John Manningham's 1602 diary anecdote links Burbage's Richard to audience fascination and theatrical celebrity.
    • The play's direct address helps explain why the role became a vehicle for star performance.

    1745

    Garrick and the Cibber stage tradition

    • David Garrick made Richard one of his defining roles.
    • Hogarth's painting shows Garrick in the tent scene before Bosworth.
    • The image records not Shakespeare's full text in isolation, but the powerful stage tradition shaped by Colley Cibber's 1699 adaptation.
    • Cibber's version supplied famous stage business and helped fix Richard as a star part.

    nineteenth century

    Kean, Irving and the actor-manager line

    • Edmund Kean and Henry Irving belong to the nineteenth-century line of major Richards.
    • The role rewarded physical detail, sudden charm, vocal attack and visible menace.
    • Actor-manager productions often made Richard a controlling stage presence as well as a political figure inside the plot.

    1984

    Bill Alexander: Antony Sher

    • Bill Alexander directed the 1984 RSC production.
    • Antony Sher's Richard used black crutches and a highly physical performance style.
    • William Dudley's design drew on Gothic architecture and medieval spectacle.
    • Sher's performance became one of the landmark modern stage Richards.

    2007

    Michael Boyd: complete history cycle

    • Michael Boyd's modern-dress production came at the end of the RSC's eight-play history cycle.
    • Jonathan Slinger played Richard.
    • The same ensemble performed the English histories in repertoire at Stratford and later at the Roundhouse.
    • The cycle context made Richard the end product of civil war rather than a villain appearing from nowhere.

    2012

    Roxana Silbert: Jonjo O'Neill

    • Roxana Silbert directed the 2012 RSC production.
    • Jonjo O'Neill played Richard.
    • The production continued the RSC's recent habit of testing the play through contemporary theatrical speed and direct audience address.

    2022

    Gregory Doran: Arthur Hughes

    • Gregory Doran directed the 2022 Royal Shakespeare Theatre production.
    • Arthur Hughes played Richard, with Jamie Wilkes as Buckingham, Rosie Sheehy as Anne and Minnie Gale as Margaret.
    • The RSC described the production as the climax to Shakespeare's first great history cycle.
    • Its casting and reception were closely tied to debates about disability, representation and the play's inherited language.

    Adaptations

    Adaptations shift the balance between history play, revenge drama, villain comedy and political warning.

    1699

    Colley Cibber's adaptation

    • Cibber's Richard III opened in 1699 and became the dominant stage version.
    • It cut and rearranged Shakespeare, added material from other plays and sharpened Richard as a theatrical villain.
    • Many famous stage memories of Richard belong partly to Cibber's adaptation, not Shakespeare's text alone.

    Political theatre

    Richard as a tyrant for later ages

    • Modern productions often connect Richard to fascism, populism, propaganda or authoritarian theatre.
    • The play supports this because Richard's rise depends on staged humility, public fear and the manipulation of spectators.
    • It is a history play, but it repeatedly asks how political audiences are trained to accept violence.

    Casting and disability

    A changing performance question

    • Older productions often used make-up, costume or posture to mark Richard's body as sign of moral deformity.
    • Recent discussion has challenged that inherited equation.
    • The 2022 RSC production made this issue central by casting Arthur Hughes.

    Screen versions

    Screen adaptations usually choose a strong political visual frame. Film can turn Richard's asides into direct camera address, making the audience his private confidant.

    1955

    Laurence Olivier

    • Olivier directed and starred in the 1955 film.
    • The film uses colour, pageantry and direct address to build Richard as a theatrical villain for cinema.
    • Olivier's version remains one of the most widely recognised screen Richards.

    1995

    Richard Loncraine and Ian McKellen

    • Richard Loncraine directed the 1995 film, with Ian McKellen as Richard.
    • McKellen co-wrote the screenplay with Loncraine.
    • The setting relocates the story to a 1930s fascist visual world.
    • The cast includes Annette Bening, Jim Broadbent, Nigel Hawthorne, Kristin Scott Thomas, Maggie Smith and Robert Downey Jr.

    2016

    The Hollow Crown

    • The BBC's Hollow Crown cycle included Richard III as the conclusion of the second cycle.
    • Benedict Cumberbatch played Richard.
    • The television context restores the play's relationship to the Henry VI sequence.
