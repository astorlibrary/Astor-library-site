The Tempest, William Shakespeare

First printed in the 1623 First Folio, The Tempest finds Prospero, the rightful Duke of Milan, in exile on an island with his daughter Miranda. When the ship carrying his enemies passes nearby, he raises a storm and brings them ashore. Ariel seeks freedom, Caliban resists servitude, Miranda meets Ferdinand, and Prospero must decide what to do with the power he has spent years preparing to use.

The Folio text stays at the centre, with scene summaries and notes that move from the Sea Venture and Montaigne to the play’s masque, conspiracies and epilogue. They return especially to Caliban’s claim to the island, Gonzalo’s commonwealth and Prospero’s renunciation: passages where authority and freedom refuse easy answers.

At a glance

The Folio text, court record and sources of Shakespeare’s island.

  • First Folio The Tempest was first printed in the 1623 Folio, seven years after Shakespeare’s death.
  • Opening position In the Folio, it is placed first among the comedies, even though modern readers often class it with the late romances.
  • Court record The Revels Office records a performance of The Tempest before King James at Whitehall on 1 November 1611.
  • No single source Unlike some Shakespeare plays, The Tempest has no direct narrative source. Shakespeare combined travel writing, romance, masque, classical and essay material.
  • Bermuda context The wreck of the Sea Venture in 1609 and reports by Strachey, Jourdain and others are central to the play’s source history.
  • Montaigne Gonzalo’s imagined commonwealth echoes John Florio’s 1603 English translation of Montaigne’s essay ‘Of the Cannibals’.

    • Text and First Folio history

    The 1623 First Folio preserves the play’s first printed text and remains the basis of modern editions.

    1623

    First printed witness

    • The First Folio was prepared by Shakespeare’s fellow actors John Heminges and Henry Condell.
    • The Tempest had not appeared in quarto before 1623.
    • The Folio text includes act and scene divisions, unusually full stage directions and a cast list.
    • The play’s first place in the printed volume has influenced later ideas of order, closure and Shakespeare’s late career.

    c.1610-11

    Date and early performance

    • Most editors place composition around 1610 to 1611.
    • The court performance at Whitehall on 1 November 1611 shows the play was complete and performable by then.
    • The Sea Venture material gives a likely lower date boundary after accounts of the 1609 wreck reached England.

    Genre

    Comedy, romance, late play

    • The Folio classed the play as a comedy.
    • Modern categories often describe it as a late romance, alongside Pericles, Cymbeline and The Winter’s Tale.
    • The play ends with reconciliation and marriage arrangements, but it also leaves unresolved questions about Caliban, Ariel and Prospero’s rule.

    Source history and island context

    The play draws on shipwreck report, colonising ventures, humanist essays, court entertainment and older romance patterns.

    1609

    Sea Venture and Bermuda

    The Sea Venture was wrecked on Bermuda while sailing to the Virginia colony, and reports of survival, storm, wreck, island abundance and colonial rescue circulated in England. Shakespeare does not dramatise the wreck directly. He turns its public material into a controlled theatrical storm and a politically charged island.

    Strachey / Jourdain

    Reports behind the storm

    William Strachey wrote A True Reportory of the Wracke and Redemption of Sir Thomas Gates, while Silvester Jourdain published A Discovery of the Barmudas in 1610. The Bermuda material helps explain the play’s attention to shipwreck, survival, wonder, fear and contested land.

    1603

    Montaigne and Gonzalo

    John Florio’s translation of Montaigne was available in English before the play. Gonzalo imagines a commonwealth without commerce, magistrates, poverty, inheritance or organised labour. Antonio and Sebastian make the speech comic by mocking him, but it also raises serious questions about European power and so-called civilisation.

    Masque

    Court culture and spectacle

    The masque for Ferdinand and Miranda uses classical figures, music, dance and blessing. Prospero interrupts it when he remembers Caliban’s conspiracy, and the break exposes the limits of his control: pageantry cannot secure the island by itself.

    Language, power and theatrical form

    Prospero governs through books, commands, staged apparitions and delayed information. Other characters test that control.

    Prospero

    Rule as theatre

    Prospero designs most of the action, from storm to banquet to masque. His art is theatrical: he arranges entrances, illusions, music, costumes and revelations. His late decision to drown his book and break his staff turns that theatrical power into a problem, not a simple triumph.

    Ariel

    Service and promised liberty

    Ariel performs the storm, music, invisible labour and punishment scenes. Prospero repeatedly promises freedom but delays it. Ariel’s final liberty gives the play one kind of release, separate from dynastic marriage and political restoration.

    Caliban

    Island claim and imposed language

    Caliban insists that the island was his through Sycorax before Prospero arrived. His language is both beautiful and damaged by coercion. Modern productions often foreground colonial and racial readings, especially through the line ‘This island’s mine’.

    Miranda

    Wonder and confinement

    Miranda begins with pity for those caught in the storm. She has lived inside Prospero’s narrative control and must learn her own history from him. Her encounter with Ferdinand becomes a dynastic solution, but her wonder also marks the play’s pressure between innocence and instruction.

    Key passages

    Turning points in power, freedom and renunciation.

    Act 1, Scene 1

    The storm

    The opening begins with labour and danger before royal identity matters. Mariners, boatswain and nobles clash under pressure.

    Act 1, Scene 2

    Prospero’s history lesson

    Prospero gives Miranda the past, but he controls the order, emphasis and timing of that knowledge.

    Act 1, Scene 2

    Caliban’s claim

    Caliban’s account of the island turns the play from magical revenge into dispossession and contested authority.

    Act 2, Scene 1

    Gonzalo’s commonwealth

    The speech imports Montaigne and tests European political imagination through comedy, interruption and mockery.

    Act 4, Scene 1

    The masque and its break

    The blessing of Ferdinand and Miranda is interrupted by remembered threat, making harmony temporary and staged.

    Act 5 / Epilogue

    Renunciation and release

    Prospero gives up magic and asks for release. The ending transfers authority from spell to audience.

    Characters

    Rule, service, kinship, conspiracy and the desire for freedom bind the principal figures.

  • Prospero Exiled Duke of Milan, father, scholar and stage-manager of the island action.
  • Miranda Prospero’s daughter, raised on the island, compassionate, sheltered and central to political reconciliation.
  • Ariel Spirit servant promised freedom by Prospero, performer of music, illusion, surveillance and punishment.
  • Caliban Son of Sycorax, island inhabitant and servant who contests Prospero’s authority.
  • Ferdinand Son of Alonso, separated from the ship and tested before marriage to Miranda.
  • Alonso King of Naples, grieving father and political participant in Prospero’s original exile.
  • Antonio Prospero’s brother and usurping Duke of Milan, still dangerous after the storm.
  • Sebastian Alonso’s brother, tempted into attempted murder by Antonio.
  • Gonzalo Counsellor who helped Prospero and Miranda survive, and whose utopian speech is mocked by the courtiers.

    • Performance and adaptation timeline

    Later versions cast Prospero variously as wise ruler, guilty coloniser, artist, parent, tyrant or performer leaving the stage.

    1 November 1611

    Whitehall Palace

    • Performed before King James by the King’s Men.
    • The record establishes the play in court performance before the Folio print record.

    1667

    Dryden and Davenant adaptation

    • The Tempest, or The Enchanted Island adapted the play for Restoration taste.
    • The version added characters, including a sister for Miranda, and reshaped the plot for spectacle and paired relationships.
    • Restoration versions show that The Tempest was adaptable long before modern cinema.

    1957

    Peter Brook and John Gielgud

    • Peter Brook directed John Gielgud as Prospero at the Shakespeare Memorial Theatre.
    • Later accounts remember Gielgud’s Prospero as severe and physically charged, not merely serene.

    1970

    Jonathan Miller and colonial reading

    • Jonathan Miller’s production with Graham Crowden as Prospero helped establish a modern British colonial reading of the play.
    • Norman Beaton played Ariel and Rudolph Walker played Caliban.
    • The production made the island’s politics harder to avoid.

    Victorian image record

    Prospero and Miranda

    • Nineteenth-century painting often made the play domestic, picturing father and daughter before the island becomes political.
    • Such images help show how visual culture softened or selected from the play.

    1991

    Peter Greenaway, Prospero’s Books

    • Greenaway’s film starring John Gielgud turns the play into a book- and image-centred meditation on authorship.
    • The title makes Prospero’s library central to the adaptation.

    2010

    Julie Taymor film

    • Helen Mirren played Prospera in Taymor’s film version.
    • The gender change shifts the political and parental charge of exile, authority and forgiveness.

    2016

    RSC, Gregory Doran

    • Simon Russell Beale played Prospero.
    • The production used real-time motion capture for Ariel, made with The Imaginarium and Intel.
    • It placed digital stagecraft at the centre of a play already concerned with illusion and performance.

    2024-25

    Jamie Lloyd, Theatre Royal Drury Lane

    • Sigourney Weaver played Prospero in Jamie Lloyd’s West End production.
    • The production marked Shakespeare’s return to Theatre Royal Drury Lane after a long absence from that venue.

    Ways to study the play

    Approaches to power, language, colonialism and performance.

  • Start with power Track who can command whom in each scene: Prospero, Alonso, Antonio, Stephano, Ariel and Caliban.
  • Keep Ariel and Caliban separate Both are servants, but their histories, claims and endings differ.
  • Read the storm as theatre The shipwreck is staged danger, but it also reveals social hierarchy under pressure.
  • Use the Folio The lack of quarto makes the 1623 Folio central to textual history.
  • Compare source types Put Bermuda reports beside Montaigne and masque to show how different materials enter the play.
  • Watch endings carefully Forgiveness, restoration, marriage, release and Caliban’s future do not all mean the same thing.
