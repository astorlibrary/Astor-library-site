The Sign of the Four, Arthur Conan Doyle

First published in 1890 in Lippincott’s Monthly Magazine, The Sign of the Four sends Holmes and Watson into a case of missing papers, coded signs, hidden treasure and pursuit through London. Mary Morstan has received a pearl each year since her father's disappearance; her appeal to Holmes leads to the Sholto household, the murder of Bartholomew Sholto and Jonathan Small’s final confession.

The notes place the magazine text and early book editions inside the developing Holmes canon, but they also follow the story out of Baker Street. The Agra treasure brings colonial military history, sensation fiction and imperial adventure into the detective plot, making empire part of the mystery rather than distant scenery.

At a glance

The title, first publication and imperial history behind the case.

  • Magazine title The story appeared in Lippincott’s Monthly Magazine as The Sign of the Four; or, The Problem of the Sholtos.
  • Book title Spencer Blackett’s 1890 book edition used the shorter title The Sign of Four.
  • Langham dinner Joseph Marshall Stoddart commissioned work from Doyle and Oscar Wilde after a London dinner in 1889.
  • Holmes canon The novel sits between A Study in Scarlet and the 1891 Strand Magazine short stories.
  • Watson and Mary Watson’s attraction to Mary Morstan gives the case a domestic and romantic line alongside the investigation.
  • Imperial frame The Agra treasure plot depends on British military service in India and the violence of 1857.
  • Colonial language Tonga is written through racist Victorian assumptions about the Andamanese. This needs direct historical framing in teaching.
  • Method in motion Holmes uses observation, chemical knowledge, disguise, tracking, police contacts, the Baker Street Irregulars and a dog named Toby.
  • River pursuit The chase after the launch Aurora turns the case into a London transport record: wharves, boats, tides, engines and police coordination.

    • Text and publication history

    The novel’s early record matters because its title, format and audience changed quickly between magazine and book publication.

    February 1890

    Lippincott’s Monthly Magazine

    • The story was issued as a complete magazine text, not as a monthly serial in separate instalments.
    • The magazine form placed Doyle beside other prose, reviews and periodical material, rather than inside a dedicated detective series.
    • The cover advertised the title and Doyle’s name before Holmes had become the mass-market figure created by the later Strand stories.

    1890-1892

    Book form and visual marketing

    • Spencer Blackett issued the first book edition in October 1890.
    • The book title dropped the second “the”, giving the common variant The Sign of Four.
    • Later illustrated editions made the treasure box, the Sholto interior and Holmes’s case-work more visibly marketable.

    1859-1930

    Doyle before Holmes became a brand

    • Doyle trained and worked as a doctor before becoming known primarily as an author.
    • A Study in Scarlet had introduced Holmes in 1887, but the detective’s wide public fame came after the Strand short stories began in 1891.
    • The Sign of Four records Doyle between first experiment and serial success.

    1902 / 1903

    Illustrated afterlives in print

    • A German edition illustrated by Richard Gutschmidt appeared in 1902.
    • George Newnes issued an edition illustrated by F. H. Townsend in 1903.
    • By then Holmes had become a periodical and publishing property beyond the original magazine commission.

    Empire and the Agra treasure

    The London mystery grows from a colonial history of rebellion, imprisonment and plunder; the two strands belong together.

    Agra

    Treasure, fort and imperial memory

    • The treasure plot uses Agra as a name loaded with Mughal, military and colonial associations for Victorian readers.
    • UNESCO describes Agra Fort as a red sandstone Mughal fortress with 2.5-km enclosure walls, palaces, audience halls and mosques.
    • Doyle does not reconstruct Agra Fort as architectural history. He uses it as a narrative storehouse for loyalty, betrayal and hidden wealth.

    1857

    Indian uprising and British military rule

    • Jonathan Small’s confession places the treasure’s theft in the violent setting of the 1857 uprising.
    • The backstory is filtered through a British convict-soldier’s account, so it is not a neutral historical record.
    • The novel turns empire into motive: the crime begins abroad, then returns to London through guilt, inheritance and revenge.

    Andaman Islands

    Tonga and the problem of colonial description

    • Doyle’s representation of Tonga draws on nineteenth-century colonial accounts of the Andamanese rather than direct knowledge.
    • The description is racist and dehumanising. A teaching page should name that plainly, not soften it into neutral “exotic” material.
    • The Andaman penal colony was tied to British responses after the 1857 uprising, which makes the novel’s prison and exile material part of a wider colonial system.

    Adventure print

    Sensation, treasure and detective fiction

    • The novel joins detection to sensation and imperial adventure: locked rooms, secret signs, stolen treasure, pursuit, confession and romance.
    • Holmes solves the case through trace evidence and organised inquiry, but the plot also depends on coincidence, testimony and melodrama.
    • This mixed form helps explain why the book feels different from the later short-story cases.

    Holmes, Watson and detection

    The case tests Holmes’s method, Watson’s narration and the partnership between them.

    Opening experiment

    Cocaine, boredom and analysis

    • The novel opens with Watson watching Holmes use cocaine, a scene that immediately sets method beside danger and nervous appetite.
    • Holmes’s mind needs stimulation. The case provides a socially useful version of the same craving for intensity.
    • Watson’s medical disapproval establishes one of the partnership’s recurring tensions.

    Mary Morstan

    Client, witness and marriage plot

    • Mary is not simply a device to bring the case to Baker Street. Her father’s disappearance gives the plot its first human cost.
    • Watson’s attraction affects his narration, especially in scenes where he imagines Mary’s possible wealth and class position.
    • The destruction of the treasure clears a path for Watson’s proposal by removing the barrier of unequal fortune.

    Athelney Jones

    Police procedure and wrong certainty

    • Inspector Athelney Jones arrests too broadly and too quickly.
    • Holmes’s superiority is staged through more patient attention to physical traces.
    • The contrast is comic, but it also gives the detective plot a rule: confidence is not evidence.

    Toby and the Irregulars

    Tracking as teamwork

    • Toby the dog and the Baker Street Irregulars show that Holmes’s work depends on assistance below the level of official authority.
    • The case moves through domestic interiors, streets, wharves and river traffic.
    • Detection becomes a network: scent, shoe marks, disguise, street knowledge, police access and transport timing.

    Timeline

    Publication and adaptation history.

    1887

    A Study in Scarlet

    • Holmes and Watson first appear in print.
    • The earlier novel establishes the partnership, the Baker Street setting and Watson’s retrospective narration.

    30 August 1889

    Langham Hotel dinner

    • Joseph Marshall Stoddart met Doyle and Oscar Wilde in London.
    • Doyle later connected the dinner with the commission for The Sign of the Four.
    • Wilde’s The Picture of Dorian Gray also came out of the same Lippincott circle.

    February 1890

    First magazine publication

    • The story appeared complete in Lippincott’s Monthly Magazine.
    • The title on the periodical record was The Sign of the Four.

    October 1890

    First book publication

    • Spencer Blackett issued the first book edition.
    • The title appeared as The Sign of Four.
    • The edition included a frontispiece by Charles H. M. Kerr.

    1891

    The Strand stories begin

    • The short stories beginning in 1891 made Holmes a wider public success.
    • That later success changed how readers looked back at the two earlier novels.

    1902-1903

    New illustrated editions

    • Richard Gutschmidt illustrated a German edition in 1902.
    • F. H. Townsend illustrated a George Newnes edition in 1903.

    On stage, radio and screen

    The case moves readily between forms: client, mystery, crime scene, pursuit, confession and romance.

    1905 / 1913

    Early silent cinema

    • Adventures of Sherlock Holmes; or, Held for Ransom (1905) loosely drew on the Holmes property and the treasure-rescue pattern.
    • Sherlock Holmes Solves the Sign of the Four appeared in 1913.
    • These early records show the story entering screen culture before a stable Holmes screen grammar had formed.

    1923

    Maurice Elvey and Stoll Pictures

    • The British silent feature The Sign of Four was directed by Maurice Elvey.
    • Eille Norwood played Holmes, with Arthur M. Cullin as Watson.
    • The production belongs to the Stoll cycle of Holmes films.

    1932

    Arthur Wontner sound film

    • Graham Cutts directed The Sign of Four: Sherlock Holmes’ Greatest Case.
    • Arthur Wontner played Holmes and Ian Hunter played Watson.
    • The adaptation rearranged material for sound-film plotting and changed the rhythm of the investigation.

    1968

    BBC television

    • Peter Cushing played Holmes and Nigel Stock played Watson in a BBC television version.
    • The adaptation sits inside the 1960s BBC Holmes run rather than as a standalone cinema film.

    1978 / 1979

    The Crucifer of Blood

    • Paul Giovanni’s stage adaptation reworked the novel as a theatre mystery.
    • The Broadway production opened at the Helen Hayes Theatre in 1978.
    • A London production followed in 1979 with Keith Michell and Denis Lill.

    1983

    Two television versions

    • Ian Richardson played Holmes in a British television film directed by Desmond Davis.
    • The Soviet adaptation The Treasures of Agra formed part of the Lenfilm Holmes cycle.

    1987

    Granada Holmes

    • The Granada adaptation was directed by Peter Hammond.
    • Jeremy Brett played Holmes and Edward Hardwicke played Watson.
    • This version is often remembered for its period texture and its place within Granada’s larger Holmes project.

    Radio

    From WGY to BBC Radio 4

    • Radio versions include a WGY broadcast in 1922 and Edith Meiser’s 1932 episodes.
    • BBC versions followed in 1959 and 1963.
    • Bert Coules adapted the story for BBC Radio 4 in 1989, with Clive Merrison as Holmes and Michael Williams as Watson.

    Ways to study the novel

    Approaches to form, detection and empire.

  • Deduction Track the difference between observation, inference and proof. Holmes often moves faster than the evidence he explains.
  • Narration Watson records admiration, irritation, romance and uncertainty. His voice is part of the case record.
  • Empire Teach the Agra treasure plot with historical framing around 1857, military violence, extraction and colonial stereotyping.
  • Gender and class Mary Morstan’s possible inheritance changes how Watson imagines marriage, money and social rank.
  • Genre mixture Compare detective fiction with sensation fiction and imperial adventure: secret signs, treasure, confession, pursuit and melodrama.
  • Adaptation Compare the 1932, 1987 or stage versions with the novel’s structure. Note what they do with Tonga, Mary and the 1857 backstory.
