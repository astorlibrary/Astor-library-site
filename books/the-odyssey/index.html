The Odyssey

Ten years after Troy, Odysseus is still absent from Ithaca. His return passes through monsters, divine anger, seduction, shipwreck and the world of the dead; at home, Penelope and Telemachus face the suitors who consume the household. The poem asks what kind of person may return from war—and what must be recognised before a home can be restored.

Ancient epic

The Odyssey, Homer, translated by Samuel Butler

Composed in ancient Greece and traditionally attributed to Homer, The Odyssey is one of the foundational works of Western literature.

Ten years after the fall of Troy, Odysseus remains separated from Ithaca, Penelope and Telemachus. His voyage takes him through the lands of the Lotus-eaters and Cyclopes, past Circe, the Sirens, Scylla and Charybdis, and into the realm of the dead. Meanwhile, the suitors occupy his palace, forcing Telemachus to defend his inheritance and Penelope to delay a marriage she does not want.

How the book works

Three ways to see the structure before getting lost in the detail.

Story

The voyage is told twice

The narrator first shows Odysseus on Calypso’s island. Odysseus later tells the Phaeacians about the Cyclops, Circe, the dead and the dangers that destroyed his crew.

Hospitality

Every house is a test

Hosts may feed, question, imprison, seduce or attack a stranger. The treatment of guests becomes a measure of both household order and civilisation.

Recognition

Knowing and being known

Odysseus returns in disguise. Telemachus, Eurycleia, Eumaeus, Penelope and Laertes each require a different sign before identity becomes secure.

A closer reading

The famous monsters occupy only part of the poem. Its full design joins a son’s education, a traveller’s stories and a difficult homecoming.

Telemachus

The poem begins with the person left behind

The first four books follow Telemachus through Ithaca, Pylos and Sparta before Odysseus appears. His journey gathers news, models of adulthood and the confidence to speak before others.

This opening changes the scale of homecoming. Odysseus is not returning to a household preserved outside time; his son has grown, the suitors have consumed his wealth and Penelope has had to invent her own forms of delay.

Storytelling

Odysseus survives by controlling a version of events

The Cyclops, Circe, Sirens and descent to the dead reach us through Odysseus’s account to the Phaeacians. Heroic action is therefore inseparable from the ability to arrange experience for listeners.

Elsewhere, bards sing about Troy while characters respond with pride, grief or unease. The poem keeps asking what stories of war preserve, what they simplify and who has the authority to tell them.

Ithaca

Recognition leads to restoration—and violence

Odysseus cannot simply announce himself. Each recognition scene tests a different bond through a scar, a memory, a secret or a shared history. Penelope’s test of the marriage bed is the most exacting because it concerns knowledge held between equals.

The killing of the suitors restores the household but also creates a new threat of vengeance. Athena’s intervention stops the cycle. Homecoming is completed by political settlement, not private reunion alone.

People to keep in view

The figures who carry the main pressures of the work.

  • OdysseusKing of Ithaca, marked by endurance, cunning, curiosity and a dangerous appetite for reputation.
  • PenelopeKeeper of the household and strategist of delay, whose intelligence is tested against the suitors.
  • TelemachusThe absent hero’s son, learning to speak, travel and act in a house where his authority is ignored.
  • AthenaDivine protector who guides Telemachus, assists Odysseus and manages the conditions of return.
  • PoseidonOdysseus’s divine enemy after the blinding of Polyphemus.
  • EumaeusThe loyal swineherd whose welcome to a stranger matters before he knows his king has returned.

    • How the poem tells the journey

    The voyage is not given from beginning to end. Homer makes storytelling, delay and recognition part of the action.

    Books 1–4

    The hero is absent

    The poem begins in Ithaca, where Odysseus is a memory, a rumour and a missing father. Telemachus’s journey to Pylos and Sparta gathers stories about the generation that returned from Troy.

    By postponing Odysseus, the poem shows what his absence has done. Homecoming matters because a household, a marriage and a political inheritance have been left exposed.

    Books 9–12

    Odysseus becomes his own singer

    At the Phaeacian court, Odysseus tells the Cyclops, Circe, the dead, the Sirens and the dangers at sea. These adventures are memories shaped for listeners, not events narrated as they happen.

    His skill with a story resembles his skill with disguise. The poem admires that intelligence while letting the audience ask what he stresses, excuses or leaves unsaid.

    Books 13–24

    Return depends on withheld identity

    Back in Ithaca, Odysseus must not be recognised too soon. Athena alters his appearance; he tests servants and family; information is released according to loyalty and danger.

    The long delay makes recognition an ethical judgement. A scar, a bed, a bow or a memory proves identity only because someone knows how to read it.

    Six tests of belonging

    Hospitality is more than good manners in the poem. It reveals whether a household recognises limits, strangers and the authority of the gods.

  • Calypso’s islandOdysseus is safe and offered immortality, yet he still chooses a mortal return. Home is defined not by comfort alone but by history, obligation and the life that is properly his.
  • The CyclopsPolyphemus has food and shelter but rejects the duties of a host. Odysseus survives through the name ‘Nobody’, then endangers the escape by announcing the heroic name he wants remembered.
  • The PhaeaciansNausicaa and her parents receive a stranger before they know his identity. Their generous order offers a contrast to both the Cyclops’s lawlessness and the suitors’ abuse of another man’s house.
  • Eurycleia and the scarThe nurse recognises the body beneath Athena’s disguise. The scar carries private history: identity returns not as a claim but as something remembered by another person.
  • The contest of the bowAn object from the household becomes proof of strength, knowledge and rightful place. The suitors cannot even prepare the weapon they assumed they were entitled to inherit.
  • Penelope and the bedPenelope does not accept a returned husband on appearance alone. Her test depends on knowledge shared by the couple, making recognition an act of intelligence equal to Odysseus’s own.

    • The people who read the signs

    Odysseus is celebrated for disguise and invention, but his return succeeds because other people know how to test, recognise and respond to him.

    Penelope

    Recognition is not surrender

    Penelope delays the suitors through the weaving and unweaving of Laertes’s shroud, preserving time inside a household they are consuming. Her caution when Odysseus returns is the same intelligence in another form: she refuses to exchange one uncertain story for another simply because she wants it to be true.

    The test of the marriage bed concerns knowledge only the couple can share. Odysseus must be read before he can be welcomed. The scene makes reunion depend upon Penelope’s judgement, not merely the hero’s arrival.

    Athena

    The goddess stages knowledge

    Athena does more than protect a favourite hero. She alters appearances, manages meetings and controls the order in which identities are disclosed. Her disguises teach Telemachus how to act and give Odysseus time to learn what has happened in his own house.

    She also ends the retaliatory violence after the suitors’ deaths. Human cunning completes the private return; divine authority is needed to prevent that victory from beginning another cycle of revenge.

    Eumaeus and Eurycleia

    Loyalty appears before certainty

    Eumaeus welcomes the disguised Odysseus as a stranger. His hospitality matters because it is offered before status can reward it. Eurycleia recognises the scar while washing the stranger’s feet, reading the body beneath Athena’s altered appearance.

    Both scenes move heroic identity away from public fame. A king is known through household memory, ordinary service and the conduct people maintain when they think power is absent.

    Five moments when heroism becomes difficult

    The poem admires endurance and intelligence without pretending that every famous action is beyond judgement.

  • The Lotus-eatersThe danger is not death but the loss of desire for return. Heroism begins with keeping a purpose alive when comfort offers forgetfulness.
  • The CyclopsThe escape depends upon patience, the false name ‘Nobody’ and collective labour. Odysseus then nearly destroys the success by declaring his true name for glory.
  • Circe’s islandOdysseus rescues his companions, but the long stay also delays the homecoming. Pleasure and leadership pull in different directions.
  • The SirensHe wants forbidden knowledge while arranging for others to bear the labour of his survival. The scene joins intellectual appetite to command.
  • The suitors’ hallThe bow and slaughter restore a violated household, yet the relatives’ response shows that justice cannot be kept private. Victory must become a settlement that the wider community can survive.
