A Midsummer Night's Dream, William Shakespeare

In Athens, Hermia is ordered to marry Demetrius or face death. She loves Lysander, Helena loves Demetrius, and Demetrius wants Hermia. When the lovers flee into the woods, they enter a world ruled by quarrelling fairies, mistaken affections and dangerous enchantment.

Oberon sends Puck to use a love-potion, but the spell goes wrong, turning desire into chaos. Bottom and his fellow tradesmen rehearse a tragic play for a royal wedding and become part of the forest’s comic disorder.

Records worth knowing

Short factual points that help frame the play before reading, teaching or staging it.

  • Three worlds Athens, the forest and the mechanicals’ rehearsal world overlap, collide and correct one another.
  • No single source Shakespeare draws on classical, medieval and folk material, but the play is not a simple adaptation of one earlier story.
  • Printed early The 1600 quarto states that the play had already been performed publicly by the Lord Chamberlain’s Men.
  • Dream logic The final appeal to the audience turns the play’s confusions into a theatrical compact: take offence only if you refuse the dream.
  • Pyramus and Thisbe The final amateur tragedy mirrors, miniaturises and mocks the lovers’ near-tragic confusion.
  • Fairy darkness Later productions often stress that the forest is not just charming: it is a space of coercion, humiliation and unstable desire.

    • Text and publication

    The text survives through quarto and folio transmission. The 1600 quarto is the main early printed witness behind most modern editions.

    1600

    First Quarto: Thomas Fisher

    • Printed as A Midsommer nights dreame.
    • The title page says the play had been publicly acted by the Lord Chamberlain’s Men.
    • Folger notes that most modern editions are based on the Q1 text.
    • The edition was sold at Fisher’s shop at the sign of the White Hart in Fleet Street.

    1619

    Second Quarto and false dating

    • The second quarto was based on Q1.
    • It carries a 1600 date, but belongs to the group often called the Pavier or False Folio quartos.
    • Folger records that Q2 added some stage directions and small corrections.

    1623

    First Folio

    • The First Folio printed the play among Shakespeare’s comedies.
    • Folger records Q2 as the basis for the Folio text, with further small changes.
    • One noted Folio change is the substitution of Egeus for Philostrate in the final scene.

    Sources and source history

    The play combines classical story, medieval romance, English folk material and Shakespeare’s own dramatic invention.

    Ovid

    Pyramus and Thisbe

    • The mechanicals’ play draws on the Pyramus and Thisbe story from Ovid’s Metamorphoses.
    • Shakespeare turns a tragic tale of forbidden love into an amateur performance full of prologues, literal props and comic mismanagement.
    • The material also echoes Romeo and Juliet: secret meeting, mistaken death, and lovers divided by family pressure.

    Chaucer / classical Athens

    Theseus, Hippolyta and courtly marriage

    • Theseus and Hippolyta belong to classical and medieval romance traditions.
    • Chaucer’s Knight’s Tale is a useful source-context for Theseus as ruler, judge and marriage-maker.
    • The play begins with patriarchal legal pressure before moving into the forest’s more unstable system of power.

    Folk belief

    Robin Goodfellow and fairy misrule

    • Puck, or Robin Goodfellow, comes from English folklore rather than classical myth.
    • Shakespeare makes him both servant and improviser: he follows Oberon’s order but also relishes disorder.
    • The final epilogue turns Puck from plot-maker into actor, asking the audience to treat the whole action as a dream.

    Worlds of the play

    The structure depends on repeated crossings: city to forest, law to dream, aristocrats to tradesmen, human desire to fairy interference.

    Act IV image record

    Fairy power and Bottom’s transformation

    • Bottom’s ass-head turns the play’s language of desire into a visible theatrical joke.
    • Titania’s enchantment is comic, but also coercive: Oberon uses humiliation to secure the Indian boy.
    • Victorian illustration often makes this forest world lush, crowded and decorative.

    c.1790

    Fuseli and the darker fairy imagination

    • Fuseli’s painting was commissioned for Boydell’s Shakespeare Gallery.
    • It shows how the play’s fairy material could be read as uncanny, erotic and strange, not simply picturesque.
    • This afterlife matters because modern productions often return to the play’s disquieting undertones.

    Act V

    The mechanicals and theatrical failure

    • Bottom, Quince, Flute, Snout, Snug and Starveling create the court entertainment.
    • Their performance of Pyramus and Thisbe is bad theatre that becomes excellent comedy.
    • The scene exposes how theatre works: wall, moonshine and lion are absurd because they are too literal.

    Chronological record

    Dated points for reading, teaching and cross-referencing.

    c.1594-1596

    Probable composition period

    • The usual dating places the play in the mid-1590s, close to Romeo and Juliet.
    • The wedding-performance theory is long-standing, but there is no secure evidence that the play was written for one specific aristocratic wedding.

    1598

    Francis Meres record

    • Francis Meres listed Midsummers night dreame in Palladis Tamia.
    • This gives an early external record before the first quarto.

    1600

    First quarto

    • Printed for Thomas Fisher.
    • The title page connects the play to public performance by the Lord Chamberlain’s Men.

    1623

    First Folio

    • Printed in the Comedies section.
    • The Folio helped preserve the play as part of Shakespeare’s collected dramatic canon.

    1662

    Pepys at Drury Lane

    • Samuel Pepys saw the play after the Restoration.
    • His diary response was famously hostile, a useful reminder that the play’s reputation was not always secure.

    Stage history

    Stage versions tend to reveal what a period thinks the forest is: pageant, fairyland, erotic disorder, childhood fantasy, circus, dream-space or nightmare.

    1840

    Madame Vestris at Covent Garden

    • Vestris’s production is often treated as a major nineteenth-century staging.
    • Victorian versions leaned into music, spectacle and fairy prettiness.
    • This performance tradition shaped the long association of the play with children, fairies and decorative enchantment.

    1970

    Peter Brook and the white box

    • Directed by Peter Brook for the Royal Shakespeare Company.
    • Sally Jacobs designed the bright white box set, replacing the traditional pretty forest.
    • RSC records describe circus skills, trapeze work and doubled Theseus/Oberon and Hippolyta/Titania.
    • The production made the play stranger, more adult and more physically inventive.

    2016

    Erica Whyman: A Play for the Nation

    • RSC Artistic Director Erica Whyman staged the play with amateur companies across the UK.
    • The project renewed the mechanicals’ amateur-theatre thread by embedding local groups into the production model.
    • It showed how the play can work as a national community-theatre framework as well as a professional repertory text.

    2026

    RSC / Unicorn Theatre retelling

    • The RSC page records a 2026 co-production with the Unicorn Theatre.
    • The production is edited by Robin Belfield and runs at The Other Place, Stratford-upon-Avon.
    • The RSC describes it as a fresh retelling for audiences aged seven and above.

    Music, ballet and screen versions

    The play’s afterlife has often moved through music and image: Mendelssohn, Britten, ballet, Hollywood fantasy and ensemble screen comedy.

    1826 / 1842

    Mendelssohn

    • Felix Mendelssohn composed the concert overture in 1826.
    • His later incidental music, including the famous Wedding March, fixed a bright Romantic sound-world around the play.

    1960

    Benjamin Britten’s opera

    • Britten’s A Midsummer Night’s Dream was first performed at Aldeburgh in 1960.
    • The opera compresses Shakespeare’s play and opens in the forest rather than at Theseus’s court.
    • It gives Oberon a countertenor voice, creating an immediately non-naturalistic fairy realm.

    1935

    Max Reinhardt and William Dieterle

    • Warner Bros. released the film in 1935.
    • The cast included James Cagney as Bottom, Mickey Rooney as Puck, Olivia de Havilland as Hermia, Anita Louise as Titania and Victor Jory as Oberon.
    • It used Mendelssohn’s music, arranged for film by Erich Wolfgang Korngold.

    1999

    Michael Hoffman film

    • Hoffman’s film relocated the action to a turn-of-the-century Italian setting.
    • The ensemble included Kevin Kline, Michelle Pfeiffer, Rupert Everett, Stanley Tucci, Calista Flockhart, Christian Bale, Sophie Marceau and David Strathairn.
    • The bicycles, villas and warm Tuscan setting make the play a late-nineteenth-century romantic comedy rather than a bare theatrical dream.
