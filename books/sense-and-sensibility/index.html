Sense and Sensibility, Jane Austen

After Mr Dashwood’s death, his widow and three daughters leave Norland for Barton Cottage. The move is not sentimental retreat. It is the practical result of inheritance, male succession and John Dashwood’s refusal to give the financial help his father had requested.

Elinor loves Edward Ferrars but keeps her knowledge and grief private. Marianne gives full public force to her attachment to Willoughby. Colonel Brandon watches, waits and suffers quietly. The novel tests whether feeling can be trusted without judgement, and whether judgement has any value without feeling.

Records worth knowing

Short factual records for reading, teaching and cross-referencing.

  • Anonymous debut The title page did not name Jane Austen. It identified the book only as by “A Lady”.
  • Commission risk Austen published the novel on commission, so she carried the financial risk but kept the copyright and profits after costs.
  • Three-volume market The first edition appeared in three volumes, the standard expensive form for much fiction designed for circulating libraries and subscription readers.
  • Earlier form Family evidence connects the novel with an earlier work called Elinor and Marianne, probably epistolary in form.
  • Financial plot The Dashwood women’s reduced income is not background detail. It determines housing, marriage prospects and social dependence.
  • Second edition The first edition sold out by 1813, and a second edition appeared in the same year as Pride and Prejudice.

    • Text and publication history

    The novel moved from family manuscript culture into the London book trade. Its first title page records anonymity, authorial risk and the practical machinery of print.

    1811

    First edition: By A Lady

    • The title page gives Sense and Sensibility: A Novel. In Three Volumes. By A Lady.
    • The imprint records C. Roworth as printer and T. Egerton, Whitehall, as publisher.
    • The phrase “Printed for the Author” matters: Austen was not yet a named literary property.
    • The book was advertised on 30 October 1811 at 15 shillings in boards.

    1775-1817

    Jane Austen before public authorship

    • Austen had written fiction privately for years before publication.
    • Her published novels did not carry her name during her lifetime.
    • After Sense and Sensibility, later title pages could identify new books as by the author of this one.

    c.1790s-1810

    From Elinor and Marianne to revised novel

    • Austen family tradition remembers an earlier version titled Elinor and Marianne.
    • The early form is often described as epistolary, though no complete manuscript survives.
    • The published novel keeps letters as important plot objects, but its mature form depends on controlled narration, irony and withheld knowledge.

    1813

    Second edition and Austen’s next title

    • The first edition sold out by 1813.
    • A second edition appeared in autumn 1813.
    • In January 1813, Pride and Prejudice had appeared as by the author of Sense and Sensibility, turning the anonymous debut into a commercial identity.

    Regency context: inheritance, income and marriage

    Austen’s courtship plot is also an economic plot. The Dashwood women are respectable, but respectability does not protect them from dependence.

    Norland

    Property passes away from the women

    • Mr Dashwood cannot leave Norland to his wife and daughters in the way he wishes.
    • The property passes through John Dashwood, who is already financially secure.
    • John’s promise to help is slowly talked down by Fanny Dashwood until it becomes almost nothing.

    Barton Cottage

    Reduced gentility

    • The move to Barton Cottage reduces household scale, staff, visiting habits and marriage expectations.
    • The Dashwoods are not poor labourers; they are genteel women with insufficient independence.
    • Austen makes that middle position socially uncomfortable and dramatically productive.

    Marriage market

    Attachment and settlement

    • Edward Ferrars is constrained by family expectation and money.
    • Willoughby chooses financial rescue over emotional duty.
    • Colonel Brandon has property and steadiness, but Austen delays any easy conversion of security into romance.

    Reputation

    Women carry the public cost

    • Marianne’s openness makes her vulnerable because society reads female feeling as public conduct.
    • Lucy Steele uses secrecy as a social weapon.
    • Elinor’s self-command protects others, but it also leaves her isolated with information she cannot disclose.

    Places and image records

    Images are used where the source is traceable: Austen’s portrait tradition, Chawton, early print and public-domain illustration history.

    Chawton

    The writing house

    • Austen lived at Chawton from 1809.
    • There she revised, prepared and published the novels that made her career.
    • The house now preserves the material setting of Austen’s mature writing years.

    Chawton House

    Family property and literary context

    • Chawton House belonged to Edward Austen Knight, Jane Austen’s brother.
    • The house gives useful context for Austen’s attention to inheritance, landed property and family obligation.
    • It should not be treated as a direct model for Norland or Barton; it is a context record, not a source claim.

    1833

    Richard Bentley and illustrated Austen

    • Sense and Sensibility was republished after Austen’s death in Bentley’s Standard Novels series.
    • That edition helped bring Austen into a cheaper nineteenth-century reprint market.
    • Later illustrated editions by artists such as Hugh Thomson and Chris Hammond shaped the visual afterlife of Austen’s domestic scenes.

    Characters and dramatic functions

    The major characters are organised around money, self-knowledge and the handling of feeling in public.

  • Elinor Dashwood Sense, restraint and judgement. She feels deeply, but Austen makes her strongest emotions largely private.
  • Marianne Dashwood Sensibility, candour and expressive intensity. Her error is not feeling, but trusting feeling as proof.
  • Edward Ferrars Affection caught inside secrecy, family money and a prior engagement.
  • John Willoughby Charm, theatrical feeling and moral evasion. He knows how to perform sincerity.
  • Colonel Brandon Memory, patience and protection. His history with Eliza gives the novel a darker counter-plot.
  • Lucy Steele Social calculation in polite form. She understands secrecy, leverage and advantage better than most of the gentry around her.

    • Language and study points

    The novel works by controlling what readers know, when they know it, and which character’s judgement they trust.

  • Irony Austen often makes polite speech reveal selfishness more clearly than open confession would.
  • Free indirect style The narration can move close to a character’s assumptions while keeping critical distance.
  • Letters Written messages carry proposal, rejection, confession, manipulation and proof.
  • Illness Marianne’s illness is not merely melodrama. It forces a public reckoning with feeling, disappointment and bodily consequence.
  • Money words Income, settlement, fortune, estate and dependence are part of the emotional grammar of the novel.
  • Silence Elinor’s silence is both strength and burden. Austen makes self-command costly.

    • Stage and screen afterlives

    Adaptations repeatedly choose how much of Austen’s economic structure to preserve beside the romance.

    1971 / 1981

    BBC serial forms

    • The BBC adapted the novel for television in 1971.
    • A seven-part BBC series followed in 1981, directed by Rodney Bennett.
    • The serial form gives more room to the social network around the sisters than most feature films can allow.

    1995

    Ang Lee and Emma Thompson

    • Ang Lee directed the major feature-film version from Emma Thompson’s screenplay.
    • Thompson played Elinor; Kate Winslet played Marianne; Alan Rickman played Colonel Brandon; Hugh Grant played Edward Ferrars.
    • The film won the Academy Award for Adapted Screenplay and the Golden Bear at Berlin.

    2000

    Kandukondain Kandukondain

    • Rajiv Menon’s Tamil-language film reworks the Austen plot in an Indian setting.
    • The adaptation shows how the novel’s structure can travel: sisters, prospects, music, class, family pressure and delayed recognition.

    2008 / later

    BBC, radio, stage and Hallmark

    • Andrew Davies adapted the novel for a three-part BBC television version in 2008, directed by John Alexander.
    • BBC Radio 4 broadcast a Helen Edmundson adaptation in 2013.
    • Stage versions include Kate Hamill’s fast-moving adaptation, first associated with Bedlam’s acclaimed New York production.
    • A Hallmark Mahogany television film appeared in 2024 with a predominantly Black cast.
