Richard II, William Shakespeare

First printed in quarto in 1597, Richard II is the opening play of Shakespeare’s second history cycle, leading into the two parts of Henry IV and Henry V.

Richard banishes Henry Bolingbroke, then seizes the inheritance left to him by John of Gaunt. Bolingbroke returns from exile to reclaim what is his, but his challenge soon becomes something larger: the removal of an anointed king.

This Astor Library Edition presents the complete play, with scene summaries and concise notes on sources, textual history, performance, language and interpretation.

  • Textual note on the 1597 quarto, later quartos and First Folio.
  • Context on Richard II, Bolingbroke, Gaunt, the Lancastrian succession and medieval deposition.
  • Scene support for Mowbray’s challenge, Gaunt’s death, Bolingbroke’s return, Flint Castle, deposition, Pomfret and the murder.
  • Character studies of Richard, Bolingbroke, Gaunt, York, Aumerle, Mowbray, Northumberland, the Queen, Exton and the Duchess of York.
  • Key passage studies on royal ceremony, England’s garden, the hollow crown, the mirror, the deposition and Richard’s prison soliloquy.

The line of succession

The play looks back to Richard’s inheritance and forward to the troubled Lancastrian reigns.

1377Richard II becomes king as a child after the death of Edward III.
1381The Peasants’ Revolt tests royal authority early in Richard’s reign.
1398Bolingbroke and Mowbray are banished after the quarrel that opens Shakespeare’s play.
1399John of Gaunt dies; Bolingbroke returns; Richard is deposed.
1400Richard dies in captivity at Pontefract, called Pomfret in the play.

At a glance

Essential dates, texts and historical contexts.

  • Quarto first The 1597 quarto was titled The Tragedie of King Richard the second and attributed to public performance by the Lord Chamberlain’s Men.
  • Wise and Simmes The first quarto was printed by Valentine Simmes for Andrew Wise, whose shop was at the sign of the Angel in St Paul’s Churchyard.
  • Deposition scene Early quartos did not print the full deposition material later advertised in the 1608 quarto.
  • Folio text The play was included in the 1623 First Folio among the histories.
  • Second tetralogy Richard II opens the sequence followed by Henry IV, Part 1, Henry IV, Part 2 and Henry V.
  • All verse The play’s total reliance on verse separates it sharply from the tavern prose and mixed registers of the Henry IV plays.

    • Text and print history

    The deposition scene lies at the centre of the play’s textual and political history: early editions differ over whether and how it appears.

    1597-1623

    From quarto playbook to Folio history

    • Richard II appeared in quarto before the First Folio, unlike plays such as The Tempest.
    • Q1 was printed in 1597. Further quartos followed in 1598, 1608 and later.
    • The 1623 Folio incorporated the play into the printed sequence of Shakespeare’s English histories.
    • Modern editions usually collate the quarto and Folio witnesses rather than treating one as a simple transparent source.

    1608 quarto

    The advertised deposition material

    • The 1608 quarto advertised ‘new additions’ concerning the Parliament scene and the deposing of King Richard.
    • The scene is central because it turns political crisis into theatrical ritual: Richard is made to participate in the undoing of his own title.
    • The printing history suggests why the play mattered in a culture alert to the danger of representing deposition.

    1601

    Essex rebellion performance

    • On the eve of the Essex rising, supporters of the Earl of Essex paid for a performance of a play about Richard II.
    • The record is often linked to Shakespeare’s play because of its deposition subject.
    • Whether or not every detail can be recovered, the event shows that Richard II material could be read as politically charged in Elizabethan London.

    Historical and source context

    Shakespeare turns chronicle material into a theatrical argument about language, title, land, legality and the public making of power.

    Historical king

    Richard II, 1367-1400

    • Richard became king as a child in 1377.
    • His reign was marked by conflict with magnates, expensive rule, favourites, political crisis and the final return of Bolingbroke.
    • Shakespeare compresses, orders and stylises the record for dramatic force.

    Sacred kingship

    The anointed body

    • Richard’s language repeatedly invokes sacred monarchy, balm, anointing, angels and divine protection.
    • The play tests what happens when ritual language no longer secures political obedience.
    • Bolingbroke rarely sounds less legitimate by law than by theatre: he knows when to move and when to wait.

    Chronicles

    Holinshed and the history-play method

    • Shakespeare drew heavily on Holinshed’s Chronicles, as he did for many English history plays.
    • Samuel Daniel’s The Civil Wars is also often discussed in relation to the play’s treatment of Richard.
    • The play is not neutral reconstruction: it arranges history around public speech, legal claim and theatrical reversal.

    Gaunt

    Inheritance and land

    • Gaunt’s death changes the play’s political stakes.
    • Richard’s seizure of Bolingbroke’s inheritance makes the king look not only ceremonially weak, but legally dangerous.
    • The move lets Bolingbroke return with a claim that can appear limited before it becomes revolutionary.

    Language and theatrical form

    The play is a study in political speech: accusation, prophecy, ceremony, lament, deposition, self-performance and silence.

    All verse

    Heightened speech as pressure

    Because the play is entirely in verse, social difference is less visible through prose contrast than in the Henry IV plays. Power is carried through formality, imagery, rhythm, rhyme, antithesis and public ceremony.

    Names

    King, cousin, subject, traitor

    Titles shift as authority collapses. Richard’s identity is attached to the word king, but Bolingbroke’s movement through cousin, subject, appellant and claimant shows how political language changes before the crown does.

    The mirror

    Seeing the king made private

    The mirror scene makes visible what the deposition has done: Richard searches for a face that can still match the name and office being removed from him.

    Gardens

    The nation as mismanaged plot

    The garden scene turns government into cultivation: pruning, order, weeds and neglect. It lets servants speak political diagnosis while the court world fails.

    Key passages

    Turning points in the language of rule, loss and deposition.

    Act 1, Scene 1

    The challenge

    Bolingbroke and Mowbray’s formal accusation scene establishes law, honour, ceremony and Richard’s unstable handling of conflict.

    Act 2, Scene 1

    Gaunt’s England speech

    Gaunt turns national identity into a dying rebuke. The speech is patriotic, but also accusatory: England has been leased out by misrule.

    Act 3, Scene 2

    The hollow crown

    Richard imagines kingship as mortality dressed in ceremony. The speech is brilliant, self-aware and politically useless.

    Act 3, Scene 3

    Flint Castle

    Bolingbroke’s upward pressure meets Richard’s theatrical descent. The visual staging of height and submission matters.

    Act 4, Scene 1

    Deposition and mirror

    Richard turns surrender into performance. He loses power, but briefly controls the language and spectacle of losing it.

    Act 5, Scene 5

    Pomfret prison

    Richard’s final soliloquy turns inward: time, thought, identity and kingship collapse into confinement before murder.

    Characters

    Title, inheritance, loyalty and public speech shape every alliance.

  • Richard II Anointed king whose eloquence intensifies as practical authority collapses.
  • Henry Bolingbroke Gaunt’s son, first returning for inheritance, then becoming the political centre of deposition.
  • John of Gaunt Duke of Lancaster, dying uncle and father whose estate seizure gives Bolingbroke his practical cause.
  • Duke of York Richard’s uncle, caught between loyalty to the king, legal order and the reality of Bolingbroke’s success.
  • Aumerle York’s son and Richard’s supporter, pulled into the aftermath of regime change.
  • Mowbray Duke of Norfolk, accused by Bolingbroke and banished before the play’s later political reversal.
  • Queen Isabel Historically a child, made older and emotionally articulate by Shakespeare to give Richard’s fall a domestic witness.
  • Northumberland Bolingbroke’s ally, later a sign of the unstable political debt Henry IV inherits.
  • Exton Murderer of Richard, acting on what he thinks Henry wants but can disown.

    • Performance and adaptation timeline

    Productions cast Richard variously as martyr, narcissist, poet, failed ruler, sacred king or political performer.

    6 February 1601

    Essex-related performance

    • Followers of the Earl of Essex commissioned a Richard II play before the failed rising.
    • The episode made the play a long-running example of Shakespearean history meeting state anxiety.
    • Its proximity to the rising made royal deposition newly charged in performance.

    1857

    Charles Kean, Princess’s Theatre

    • Kean’s staging belonged to a Victorian culture of historical spectacle.
    • Such productions emphasised costume, pageantry and the visual authority of monarchy.
    • They help explain how the play moved between political argument and ceremonial display.

    1929 / 1937

    John Gielgud and the poetic Richard

    • Gielgud became closely associated with Richard II through early twentieth-century stage performance.
    • His reputation helped establish Richard as a part for vocal beauty, fragility and ceremonial intelligence.
    • That tradition still shapes casting expectations for the role.

    1960

    BBC television: An Age of Kings

    • The BBC cycle placed Richard II at the opening of its long account of Lancastrian and Yorkist power.
    • The play works especially well in cycle performance because Bolingbroke’s victory becomes Henry IV’s burden.

    1995

    Fiona Shaw, National Theatre

    • Deborah Warner directed Fiona Shaw as Richard II.
    • The casting invited audiences to reconsider the role’s theatricality, vulnerability and performed power.

    2005

    Trevor Nunn / Kevin Spacey

    • Trevor Nunn directed Kevin Spacey as Richard at the Old Vic.
    • Modern-dress political readings made the play legible as a study of leadership crisis rather than remote pageant.

    2013

    RSC, Gregory Doran / David Tennant

    • David Tennant played Richard in Gregory Doran’s RSC production.
    • The production emphasised ritual, visual beauty and Richard’s cultivated self-image.
    • It was filmed and circulated through cinema broadcast, widening access to the stage interpretation.

    2019

    Shakespeare’s Globe / Adjoa Andoh and Lynette Linton

    • Adjoa Andoh and Lynette Linton co-directed a production with a women of colour company.
    • Andoh played Richard.
    • The women-of-colour company placed monarchy, exclusion and English identity at the centre of the production.

    2025

    Bridge Theatre / Jonathan Bailey

    • Nicholas Hytner directed Jonathan Bailey as Richard at the Bridge Theatre.
    • A 2025 Guardian review read the production through contemporary elite politics and succession drama.
    • Its reception continued the play’s long history of contemporary political comparison.

    Richard kneeling

    1381 memory

    Rule under pressure

    • The Peasants’ Revolt is not Shakespeare’s main plot, but it belongs to Richard’s historical reign.
    • It supplies background for a king whose authority had already been tested in public crisis.

    Source book

    Chronicle into theatre

    • Chronicle sequence becomes staged confrontation.
    • Shakespeare uses history as material for speech, ceremony, accusation and role-change.

    Ways to study the play

    Approaches to kingship, language, history and performance.

  • Begin with title Track when Richard is called king, cousin, uncle, landlord, prisoner and nothing.
  • Separate claim from ambition Bolingbroke first names inheritance. The play then shows how a limited claim becomes regime change.
  • Use the deposition scene It is the centre of the textual, political and theatrical record.
  • Follow objects Crown, sceptre, land, mirror, tears, blood and coffin carry political meaning.
  • Read as cycle Bolingbroke’s victory is not an ending. It opens the troubled reign of Henry IV.
  • Compare productions Ask whether each Richard is sacred king, poet, weak ruler, narcissist, martyr or performer.
