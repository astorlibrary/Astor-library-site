Henry VI, Part 2, William Shakespeare

First printed anonymously in 1594 as The First Part of the Contention, Henry VI, Part 2 is one of Shakespeare's earliest and most politically violent history plays.

King Henry VI is too gentle to control the nobles around him. Gloucester is destroyed, Suffolk rises and falls, Jack Cade leads rebellion through London, and York begins to press his claim to the crown. The play shows England moving from court intrigue into civil war.

Records worth knowing

Short factual records for reading, teaching and cross-reference.

  • First quarto The 1594 quarto was printed by Thomas Creede for Thomas Millington as The First part of the Contention betwixt the two famous Houses of Yorke and Lancaster.
  • Anonymous print The 1594 quarto did not name Shakespeare on the title page.
  • Folio expansion The 1623 Folio text is substantially longer than the 1594 quarto.
  • 1619 combination The Pavier/Jaggard Whole Contention combined the texts now associated with Parts 2 and 3 and attributed them to Shakespeare.
  • Chronicle sources Shakespeare drew on Hall and Holinshed, and probably used other chronicle material such as Fabyan and Grafton for particular details.
  • Cade compressed The play compresses Cade's rebellion, York's return and St Albans into a rapid dramatic sequence.
  • Early style The play belongs to Shakespeare's early history-writing: public action, formal rhetoric, factional lists, reversals and abrupt violence.
  • Political centre The weak king is not the only subject. The play studies what happens when nobles, crowds and claimants all treat government as available for seizure.

    • Text and print history

    The play has one of the most important early print records in Shakespeare's history cycle.

    1594 / 1600 / 1619 / 1623

    Contention before Folio

    • The 1594 quarto presents the play under the title The First Part of the Contention.
    • A second quarto followed in 1600.
    • In 1619 the text was combined with the play now called Henry VI, Part 3 as The Whole Contention.
    • The modern title Henry VI, Part 2 comes from the Folio arrangement of the history sequence.

    Textual problem

    Quarto and Folio

    • The 1594 quarto is much shorter than the Folio text.
    • Many editors treat it as a reported or memorially reconstructed text, though the exact relation between quarto and Folio remains debated.
    • The difference matters because the play's political detail, rhetorical density and scene-shape alter between versions.

    Title record

    Long title, clear selling points

    • The 1594 title advertises Gloucester's death, Suffolk's banishment and death, Winchester's end, Cade's rebellion and York's first claim.
    • That title turns the play into a sequence of political shocks.
    • The selling language shows how early modern playbooks marketed event, faction and notoriety.

    Sources and historical compression

    Shakespeare does not reproduce chronicle history neutrally. He selects, compresses and rearranges events into a political cascade.

    Edward Hall

    The Union of the Two Noble and Illustre Families

    • Hall is a major source for the Wars of the Roses material.
    • He gives Shakespeare a strong account of Lancaster-York division and dynastic narration.
    • Hall's contrast between a saintly Henry and forceful Margaret helps shape the play's court politics.

    Raphael Holinshed

    Chronicles, 1587

    • Holinshed supplies details and arrangement not found in Hall.
    • The meeting before St Albans is one example often linked to Holinshed's chronicle version.
    • Holinshed gives Shakespeare usable national history in a form suited to stage selection.

    Fabyan and Grafton

    Other chronicle pressure

    • Robert Fabyan's chronicle may explain the play's tight connection of Cade, York and St Albans.
    • Richard Grafton offers material for details such as the false miracle scene.
    • The play is therefore a chronicle construction, not a single-source transcript.

    Dramatic compression

    Four years into one chain

    • The historical events did not unfold as quickly as they do in the play.
    • Shakespeare compresses them so that private faction, public rebellion and civil war feel like stages of one collapse.
    • This makes the play structurally violent before the battles begin.

    Scene route

    The play moves from court ceremony to accusation, murder, rebellion and battle.

    Act 1

    Marriage, faction, ambition

    • Margaret arrives as Henry's queen, but the marriage settlement weakens English interests in France.
    • Suffolk gains influence through Margaret.
    • Gloucester, Winchester, York and the court factions begin the struggle for control.

    Act 2

    Public signs and private plotting

    • The false miracle exposes fraud and the hunger for signs.
    • Gloucester's enemies close in through his wife Eleanor.
    • York gives his dynastic claim and begins to look like the future centre of force.

    Act 3

    Gloucester falls

    • Gloucester is arrested and then murdered.
    • Suffolk is implicated and banished.
    • The court's political language can no longer hide killing and revenge.

    Act 4

    Suffolk and Cade

    • Suffolk is captured at sea and killed.
    • Jack Cade's rebellion brings popular grievance, theatrical absurdity and brutal anti-literacy violence into the centre of the play.
    • London becomes a stage for collapsing order.

    Act 5

    York and St Albans

    • York returns from Ireland and moves openly towards power.
    • The Battle of St Albans begins the armed conflict between Lancaster and York.
    • The ending points directly toward Henry VI, Part 3.

    Characters and political functions

    The play is crowded because power itself is crowded. No one institution is strong enough to control the rest.

  • Henry VI Pious, gentle and politically weak. His goodness becomes dangerous because it cannot restrain others.
  • Margaret Queen, political actor and forceful public presence. She refuses the passivity Henry often accepts.
  • Suffolk Court operator and favourite. His rise depends on access, rhetoric and intimacy with power.
  • Gloucester Protector and public servant. His fall marks the removal of one of the last stabilising figures.
  • York Dynastic claimant, strategist and future rebel. He knows how to wait until disorder becomes useful.
  • Jack Cade Rebel leader and grotesque popular king. His scenes combine social anger, carnival inversion and state terror.
  • Winchester Churchman and factional rival, whose death gives the play one of its strongest images of spiritual-political corruption.
  • Warwick and Salisbury Noble power brokers whose loyalties help prepare the wider Yorkist movement.

    • Performance records

    Modern productions often treat the play as part of a cycle, because its ending is deliberately unfinished.

    1963-64

    RSC, The Wars of the Roses

    • John Barton and Peter Hall adapted the three Henry VI plays and Richard III into a major RSC cycle.
    • The sequence helped establish the plays as a coherent theatrical account of English civil conflict.
    • Brewster Mason, who played Warwick in the production, later introduced the BBC Prefaces to Shakespeare episode for this play.

    1983

    BBC Television Shakespeare

    • Jane Howell directed The Second Part of Henry the Sixt for the BBC cycle.
    • The production used the same broad set world as the other Henry VI plays but made it more decayed as England deteriorated.
    • Trevor Peacock played Jack Cade; Peter Benson played Henry VI.

    2000 / 2006-08

    RSC, Michael Boyd histories

    • Michael Boyd directed the Henry VI plays for the RSC's major history-cycle work.
    • The 2000 This England: The Histories sequence included David Oyelowo as Henry VI.
    • The productions were noted for physical violence and for connecting the Henry VI plays to a larger national fall.

    2022

    RSC, Henry VI: Rebellion

    • Owen Horsley's version retitled Part 2 as Henry VI: Rebellion.
    • The RSC community blog records 74 adult community participants from partner-theatre areas appearing as working people in Cade's rebellion and as townsfolk.
    • The title foregrounds the Cade material and the play's movement from court intrigue to social rupture.

    2016

    The Hollow Crown

    • Dominic Cooke and Ben Power adapted the Henry VI material for the BBC's The Hollow Crown: The Wars of the Roses.
    • The second episode combined material from Henry VI, Part 2 and Henry VI, Part 3.
    • Tom Sturridge played Henry VI, Sophie Okonedo played Margaret and Benedict Cumberbatch appeared as Richard.

    Language and study points

    The play's violence is carried by rhetoric as well as by bodies.

  • Faction lists Names, titles and allegiances matter. Students need to track who benefits from each accusation.
  • Public speech Trials, accusations, curses, claims and rebel proclamations make politics theatrical.
  • Literacy panic Cade's rebellion attacks writing, law and learning, but the play does not present popular politics simply or safely.
  • Female power Margaret and Eleanor are framed through ambition, public shame and male anxiety about influence.
  • Weak kingship Henry's gentleness creates a vacuum that stronger figures enter.
  • End without closure The battle ending drives the audience into the next play and into the Wars of the Roses sequence.
