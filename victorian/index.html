Period

Victorian.

These novels move from London streets to Yorkshire moors, from drawing rooms to laboratories. Again and again, they test the respectable surface of Victorian life.

1843

A Christmas Carol, Charles Dickens

Dickens’s little Christmas book appeared in 1843 with illustrations by John Leech. Scrooge’s night with Marley and the three spirits leads back to Tiny Tim and the poverty beyond his own door. Dickens’s public readings helped carry the story towards its long life on stage and screen.

1891

Tess of the d’Urbervilles, Thomas Hardy

Hardy’s novel moved from its serial appearance in The Graphic to a three-volume book edition. Tess’s journey through Marlott, Talbothays, Flintcomb-Ash and Stonehenge makes Wessex a place of rural labour, social judgement and hypocrisy, all bearing upon her relationships with Alec and Angel.

1860–61

Great Expectations, Charles Dickens

Published in serial parts in 1860–61, the novel follows Pip from Kent to London as ambition draws him towards crime, Miss Havisham and Magwitch. Its two published endings leave the question of his reward open in different ways; screen versions have kept returning to both his rise and his reckoning.

1847

Wuthering Heights, Emily Brontë

Published as the work of Ellis Bell in 1847, the novel reaches us through the layered accounts of Lockwood and Nelly Dean. Haworth and the moors ground Heathcliff and Catherine’s story of inheritance, revenge, Gothic violence and dialect, which later versions have carried to stage and screen.

1886

Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde, Robert Louis Stevenson

Victorian London, scientific ambition and secrecy close around Stevenson’s story of divided identity. Its publication history and carefully managed narrative explain how the novella creates its mystery, before the story moves into its stage and screen afterlives.

1890/1891

The Picture of Dorian Gray, Oscar Wilde

The novel first appeared in a magazine in 1890, then in a revised book text in 1891. Those differences place Dorian’s portrait amid aestheticism, decadence and moral controversy, before Wilde’s trials and later versions gave the story further lives on stage and screen.

1890

The Sign of Four, Arthur Conan Doyle

Conan Doyle’s second Sherlock Holmes novel appeared in Lippincott’s in 1890. Mary Morstan’s case brings together the Agra treasure and a pursuit along the Thames, joining Holmes’s detective method to the story’s 1857 colonial frame. Later versions took that mystery to stage and screen.

Open pageBuy / view edition

1897

Dracula, Bram Stoker

Stoker tells his vampire story through documents, bringing Whitby, Victorian technology, medicine and gender into an older tradition. The link between the Count’s name and the historical Vlad Dracula remains debated, while stage and screen adaptations reveal how freely later artists have remade him.

1897 / 1898

The War of the Worlds, H. G. Wells

Serialised in 1897 and published as a book in 1898, Wells’s invasion begins at Horsell Common and brings terror to Woking. The Heat-Ray and fighting-machines turn Victorian science and imperial power inside out. Radio, screen and other adaptations have kept finding new fears in the story.
