Editorial standards

How the library is made.

The added material should make a book easier to enter and richer to return to. It should never become a substitute for the book.

Astor Library publishes complete classic editions, study editions and free literature resources. The work differs from page to page, but the standard does not: begin with the text, make factual claims checkable and leave room for a reader’s own judgement.

01 · The work

Begin with what is on the page.

Plot, character and theme are explained through the language, structure and form of the work itself. A summary should help a reader keep their place. It should not flatten a novel or play into a set of approved answers.

02 · The record

Facts must be traceable.

Publication dates, textual history, performances and historical contexts are checked against dependable records. Book pages name the libraries, archives, museums and scholarly collections used for further reading.

03 · Interpretation

Explain the question, not only the answer.

Good literary criticism does not close a text down. Astor pages distinguish fact from interpretation, show where readers have disagreed and give enough evidence for someone to form a different view.

04 · Revision

A live page can still be improved.

No page is finished simply because it has been published. As an edition develops, the website can gain better sources, clearer explanations, new performance material and corrections. That continuing work is part of the library.

Sources in view

Readers should be able to go further.

Where a page uses external historical or visual material, its sources are linked at the end. Astor Library favours primary texts and established collections such as the Folger Shakespeare Library, the British Library, the Library of Congress, university archives and author societies.

The free guides remain separate from the main editions, but they follow the same principle. They support reading and teaching; they do not replace the original work.

The simplest test

Does this help someone read?

If a note, explanation or design choice draws attention away from the work without giving something useful back, it needs to change.
